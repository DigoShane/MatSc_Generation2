Flip-chip-based microwave spectroscopy of Andreev bound states in a planar
Josephson junction

M. Hinderling,1 D. Sabonis,1 S. Paredes,1 D. Z. Haxell,1 M. Coraiola,1 S. C. ten Kate,1

E. Cheah,2 F. Krizek,1, 2 R. Schott,2 W. Wegscheider,2 and F. Nichele1, ∗

1IBM Research Europe - Zurich, S¨aumerstrasse 4, 8803 R¨uschlikon, Switzerland
2Solid State Physics Laboratory, ETH Zurich, Otto-Stern-Weg 1, 8093 Z¨urich, Switzerland
(Dated: January 18, 2023)

We demonstrate a flip-chip-based approach to microwave measurements of Andreev bound states
in a gate-tunable planar Josephson junction using inductively-coupled superconducting low-loss
resonators. By means of electrostatic gating, we present control of both the density and transmission
of Andreev bound states. Phase biasing of the device shifted the resonator frequency, consistent with
the modulation of supercurrent in the junction. Two-tone spectroscopy measurements revealed an
isolated Andreev bound state consistent with an average induced superconducting gap of 184 µeV
and a gate-tunable transmission approaching 0.98. Our results represent the feasibility of using
the flip-chip technique to address and study Andreev bound states in planar Josephson junctions,
and they give a promising path towards microwave applications with superconductor-semiconductor
two-dimensional materials.

I.
INTRODUCTION

A
class
of
gate-tunable
superconducting-
semiconducting materials have recently been investigated
as potential building blocks for hybrid amplifiers [1],
tunable quantum buses [2], non-reciprocal devices [3]
and facilitation of integration with cryogenic control
systems [4]. Moreover, hybrid superconducting devices
based on Andreev bound states (ABSs) [5] were realized
as gate- and flux-tunable two-level system [6–13] and
qubits
[14–20],
potentially
enabling
long
coherence
times [21, 22].
Such ABS-based qubits [23–25] have
been realized in superconducting atomic contact break-
junctions [14], hybrid Josephson junctions (JJs) using
nanowires [16, 18], graphene [26] and two-dimensional
hybrid systems [27].
Two-dimensional superconductor-semiconductor het-
erostructures grown on III-V substrates, such as InP [28],
offer several advantages in terms of reproducibility and
scalability, but pose difficulties in realizing high-quality
microwave components, e.g. coplanar waveguide (CPW)
resonators.
This is due to, for example, the large di-
electric losses in the host structure [29–32] as well as to
the piezoelectric properties of III-V materials [33]. Fur-
thermore, techniques used in the fabrication of microwave
components degrade semiconductor quality. Attempts to
overcome these limitations included removal of the buffer
layers in the area surrounding the device [27], or by selec-
tive area growth of superconductor-semiconductor hybrid
structures on silicon [34]. Coupling quantum devices on
III-V substrates to high-quality microwave components
via a flip-chip approach [35–40] has not yet been demon-
strated. Owing to the promise of scalability presented by
planar systems, investigation of this platform is of partic-
ular interest for future hybrid quantum systems. Wide

employment of this technique could enable a new class
of experiments using microwave techniques on lossy sub-
strates, where the realization of high-quality microwave
components is otherwise challenging.
Here, we demonstrate microwave spectroscopy of gate-
and flux-tunable ABSs in a planar JJ defined in an epi-
taxial InAs/Al heterostructure hosting a two-dimensional
electron gas (2DEG) using a flip-chip approach.
Pla-
nar JJs and coplanar microwave resonators were defined
on different chips and inductively coupled via a vacuum
gap.
We performed single- and two-tone spectroscopy
measurements of the ABS spectrum and found that the
flip-chip approach, yielding low-loss resonators, enabled
readout of the JJ and control of the individual ABSs.
Moreover, when the electrostatically-defined transport
channel in the junction hosted an isolated ABS, its trans-
mission could be tuned up to 0.98 via electrostatic gating.

II.
DEVICE DESIGN, FLIP-CHIP TECHNIQUE
AND RESONATOR CHARACTERIZATION

The investigated system was composed of two chips.
The Device Chip was fabricated from an InAs/Al het-
erostructure grown on an InP substrate [28]. The Res-
onator Chip was realized with a high-resistivity Si sub-
strate.
After the two chips were independently fabri-
cated, they were coupled via flip-chip bonding. An op-
tical image of the measured system, where the Device
Chip is shown face-down on top of the Resonator Chip,
is shown in Fig. 1(a) and a simplified cross-section of the
flip-chip layout is shown in Fig. 1(b).
Scanning electron micrographs of the Device Chip are
shown in Figs. 1(c) and (d). The active part of the Device
Chip consists of a planar InAs JJ with epitaxial Al leads
(red), embedded in a radio-frequency superconducting
quantum interference device (rf-SQUID). The JJ had a
length L ≈ 110 nm and a width W ≈ 940 nm. Both
the JJ and the rf-SQUID were obtained by wet etching

Two Ti/Au gates (light gray) with a minimal separa-

HfO2 layer, and used to electrostatically define a tunable
constriction in the InAs. Furthermore, the gates were ar-

The phase difference ϕ across the JJ was tuned by ap-

The Resonator Chip was fabricated by sputtering of
200 nm Nb and dry etching. Key elements on the Res-
onator Chip are represented in Fig. 1(e) and included
three λ/4 CPW resonators capacitively coupled to a com-
mon transmission line (blue), drive lines (turquoise), a
ground plane (purple) and DC control lines (light gray).
Device Chip and Resonator Chip were connected via sev-
eral indium bumps, evaporated on the Resonator Chip.
The bumps provided connection to the DC control lines,
a ground to the rf-SQUID via under-bump metallization
(UBM), and ensured mechanical stability of the struc-
ture. Particular care was taken to align rf-SQUIDs with
the shorted end of the λ/4 resonators and with the drive
lines to ensure the couplings with inductances Mr and
Md, respectively.
The green rectangle in Fig. 1(b) in-

dicates the position of the rf-SQUID measured in this
work. Our flip-chip procedure allowed an alignment accu-
racy below 1 µm. The separation between the two chips
was 5 µm, and was controlled by the size of the indium
bumps and the force used during the flip-chip bonding
process. To minimize dielectric losses, the Device Chip
was considerably smaller than the Resonator Chip and
was placed to minimize its overlap with the resonators.
The resonators were measured with a vector network
analyzer by sending a signal Pin into the left port of the
transmission line and reading out the response Pout at
the right port. Here, the leftmost drive line in Fig. 1(b)
was used to drive ABS pair transitions with a continu-
ous microwave signal Pd. The parasitic coupling between
resonator and drive line is also reported [see Fig. S.2 in
the Supplemental Material (SM)]. The two gates were
controlled by voltages V1 and V2, which could be set in-
dividually (see Fig. S.5 in SM). However, a gate voltage
VG ≡ V1 = V2 was applied in the remainder of this ar-
ticle. The magnetic flux in the rf-SQUID was produced
via a home-made superconducting coil mounted on top
of the printed circuit board that hosted the device.

III.
OPERATION AND CONTROLLABILITY

Figure 1(f) displays the magnitude |S21| (blue) and
phase ∠S21 (orange) of the readout resonator while the
JJ was pinched-off (VG = −2.3 V). In this configuration,
a bare resonator frequency f0 ≈ 5.9162 GHz was mea-
sured. The asymmetry of the resonance dip in |S21| is
explained by an impedance mismatch in the circuit [41].
The internal quality factor was extracted using the circle
fit technique with diameter correction [42], yielding in-
ternal and external quality factors being Qi ≈ 5·104 and
Qe ≈ 4 · 104, respectively. Similar values of quality fac-
tors were found for all three resonators on the Resonator
Chip (see Fig. S.3 in SM).
Figure 2 shows the magnitude of the complex scatter-
ing parameter |S21| in the vicinity of f0 as a function
of gate voltage VG and magnetic flux Φ treading the rf-
SQUID. The effect on |S21| is mediated by the variable
Josephson inductance of the JJ [43]. Figure 2(a) displays

|S21| as a function of VG measured at Φ = 0. A clear de-
viation of the resonance frequency fres. ≡ fr(|S21| = 0)
from f0 appeared for less negative values of VG. The in-
set shows the internal quality factor Qi of the resonator,
extracted from the data in Fig. 2(a). The flux depen-
dence of |S21| for four selected values of VG, indicated
by the colored bars in Fig. 2(a), are shown in Fig. 2(b-
e). Periodic modulations of fres. as a function of Φ were
observed, with maxima and minima of fres. assigned to
integer and half-integer multiples of the flux quantum Φ0,
respectively. At VG = −0.8 V, fres. was modulated over
all flux values Φ [Fig. 2(b)]. Setting the gate more neg-
ative, the effect of Φ on fres. was limited to half-integer
flux quanta [Fig. 2(d)].
Measurements presented in Fig. 2 are consistent with
gradual depletion of lateral ABSs in the JJ [44].
For
VG > −1.4 V, a high density of ABSs was present within
the full width of the JJ. In such a situation, a quasi-
continuous distribution of ABSs populates the JJ [45],
including several ABSs with low and high transmission
probabilities [46].
Therefore, the readout signal with
frequency fr could excite transitions between ABSs at
Φ = 0, resulting in a shift of fres. and a decrease in Qi.
When setting VG more negative, a narrow constriction
is defined in the InAs, allowing the presence of only a
few ABSs orthogonal to the leads. In this scenario, tran-
sitions between ABSs enabled by microwave driving are
expected only in the vicinity of half-integer multiples of
Φ0 [47, 48]. A more quantitative description of the situa-
tion and analysis of the modulation of fres., which follows
Ref. 43, are presented in SM and further support the in-
terpretation above.

transitions between ABS pairs within the superconduct-
ing gap, as schematically shown in the inset of Fig. 3(a).

At VG = −1.65 V, an ABS pair transition with a min-
imum frequency of 14 GHz was probed [Fig. 3(b)]. In
addition to the pair transition, weaker features at lower
and higher fd than the main ABS transition were ob-
served, as indicated by arrows. These replicas, which are
spaced by fres. from the ABS transition, are explained
by finite photon population in the resonator during ABS
drive. In this context, additional lines appear when the
resonator emits or absorbs the additional photon that
satisfies the energy relation 2EA ± hfres. = hfd, where
h is Planck’s constant [38]. The horizontal resonances
in Fig. 3(b) are associated to standing waves in the mi-
crowave cabling of the measurement circuit. Such flux-
independent resonances were previously observed in two-
tone spectroscopy measurements in the presence of strong
driving signals and are likely caused by impedance mis-
match along the signal propagation path [38, 50].
In
particular, the strongest resonance, marked with a white
dashed line, corresponds to the second harmonic of the

resonator. All of these lines became more pronounced at
higher driving powers (see Fig. S.11 in SM).
The measured ABS pair transition was extracted from
Fig. 3(b) and fitted with 2EA, with EA from Eq. 1
to determine the induced superconducting gap ∆ and
the transmission τ of the ABS. From the fit, shown in
Fig. 3(c), ∆ = 186±2 µeV and τ = 0.976±0.001 were ob-
tained. The value of ∆ is consistent with previous trans-
port studies of hybrid superconductor-semiconductor
heterostructures [51, 52]. Following Ref. 14, we estimated
the coupling strength g(Φ, τ) between the isolated ABS
and the resonator. At Φ = Φ0/2 a coupling strength of
g ≈ 35 MHz was extracted.

To further investigate the ABS spectrum in the planar
JJ, the applied flux was fixed to Φ = Φ0/2 and two-tone
spectroscopy measurements were performed as a function
of the gate voltage VG, as shown in Fig. 4(a). No isolated
ABS pair transitions could be resolved for VG > −1.4 V,
indicating a dense spectrum of ABSs in the JJ. This is
consistent with the observations from single-tone mea-
surements. At more negative VG, two isolated ABS pair
transitions were observed. Their transition frequencies
increased with decreasing VG until, below VG = −1.75 V,
no ABS transitions were observed in the experimentally
accessible frequency range.

VI.
CONCLUSION

We presented a framework for microwave spectroscopy
measurements in two-dimensional heterostructures using
a flip-chip approach. We fabricated a planar JJ where
the conductive channel was defined by electrostatic gat-
ing, allowing the probing of an isolated ABS with trans-
mission up to 0.98. Efficient and reliable coupling be-
tween superconductor-semiconductor hybrid devices and
microwave electronics fulfills a crucial requirement to-
wards scalable architectures for Andreev qubits, Andreev
molecules and topological phenomena.

removal of the Al, the wafer had a peak mobility of µ = 18000 cm2V−1s−1 and an electron density of n = 8·1011 cm−2,

Device Chip and the DC control lines on the Resonator Chip.
The Resonator Chip was fabricated from a high resistivity (ρ > 10000 Ω·cm) intrinsic Si ⟨100⟩ wafer.
After
removing the native oxide using hydrofluoric acid, 200 nm of Nb was sputtered on the wafer. In a single lithographic
step, readout resonators, device drive and DC control lines, as well as holes in the ground plane, were patterned into
the Nb with a Cl2/Ar dry etching procedure. Afterwards, the In was evaporated directly on top of the Nb using
lift-off techniques. Hydrofluoric acid was used again to remove the surface oxide of the Nb and In before flip-chip
bonding.
The Device Chip was bonded to the Resonator Chip with a flip-chip bonder (Karl Suss FC 150). To facilitate the
alignment of both chips, alignment markers were defined during the mesa etch and resonator etch step. After gluing
the sample into the printed circuit board (PCB) holder, the microwave and DC lines from the PCB were connected
to the corresponding bonding pads on the Resonator Chip with Al bond wires. Moreover, Al air bridges across the
transmission line, resonators and drive lines connecting the different ground planes ensured a homogeneous ground
potential all over the Resonator Chip.

II.
EXPERIMENTAL SETUP

The measurements were carried out in a BlueFors BF-LD cryogen-free dilution refrigerator with a mixing chamber
base temperature of 9 mK. A schematic of the wiring setup is depicted in Fig. S.1. The resonator readout and device
drive tones Pin and Pd were continuously applied using a Keysight PNA-X vector network analyser (VNA). Signals
were attenuated in the lines by 66 dB and 39 dB, respectively for readout and drive tones. Readout was performed
in transmission mode, where the signal from the transmission line on the Resonator Chip, after interacting with the
device, passed through a DC block, a circulator, a dual isolator and an RF low-pass filter and was subsequently
amplified with a cryogenic high electron mobility (HEMT) amplifier (bandwidth 0.3-14 GHz, average gain 37 dB)

(Pout). The voltages on gates V1 and V2 were controlled with DC voltages sourced with a QDevil QDAC. The voltages
applied via the DC loom were filtered with QDevil RF and RC filters (cutoff above 80 MHz and 65 kHz, respectively)
before reaching the Device Chip.
The chips were mounted on a QDevil high frequency PCB with no additional
shielding present. A vector magnet was present in the dilution refrigerator but not used in this study and hence left
grounded. Instead, the flux was supplied by sourcing a current through a coil, mounted in the near vicinity of the
chips.

IV.
MEASUREMENT PROCEDURE AND DATA PROCESSING

In single-tone spectroscopy measurements, a signal Pin with frequency fr, swept around the readout resonator
frequency, was sent to the transmission line. Ater the interaction with the readout resonator, the magnitude |S21|
and phase ∠S21 of the transmitted part of the input signal (Pout) was measured using a VNA. To acquire flux- and
gate-dependent measurements, this procedure was repeated at multiple values of Φ or VG ≡ V1 = V2, respectively.
We assigned Φ = 0 to the central DC current value, where the resonator frequency was maximum and independent
of small deviations of DC current. Since the resonator frequency is modulated with a period of one flux quantum Φ0,
Φ = 0 was defined within the flux quantum when no current was applied to the coil.
For two-tone spectroscopy measurements, the readout frequency fr was first swept around the resonance frequency of
the readout resonator to determine the minimum in the magnitude |S21| corresponding to fres.. The readout frequency
was then offset by 80 kHz, fr = fres. + 80 kHz, to position the readout tone on the slope of the resonator response
to increase the sensitivity of shifts in fres. for signal magnitude measurements.
The spectroscopy measurement
was then performed by monitoring the resonator response at the aforementioned fixed fr, while simultaneously, a
continuous drive tone Pd at frequency fd was applied to the device drive line. The drive tone with frequency fd
was swept while measuring the magnitude and phase of the resonator signal Pout. Finally, the background of the
resonator response was removed by subtracting the median of the fd sweep. For flux- and gate-dependent two-tone
spectroscopy measurements this procedure was repeated at each value of Φ or VG, respectively.
In all single- and two-tone spectroscopy plots, the colorbar of the magnitude |S21| was rescaled to [0,1]. This was
done by finding the minimum value of |S21| for each dataset. Then, this minimum magnitude was added to all values
of |S21| in the dataset, such that the new dataset consisted of only positive values. Afterwards, the maximum value
of |S21| was found and all |S21| were normalized to this value.
The transmission τ and induced superconducting gap ∆ of the JJ were obtained by fitting two-tone spectroscopy
data representing a Φ-dependent Andreev bound state (ABS) pair transition using Eq. S.1. In the short junction
limit, an ABS pair transition frequency fABS is described by:

V.
ADDITIONAL MEASUREMENTS

A.
Additional single-tone spectroscopy data

The influence of readout power on resonator response is shown in Fig. S.4. For VG = 0 V and VG = −1.66 V, the
readout power Pin was swept at fixed flux Φ = 0. The green markers in Fig. S.4(a) and (c) indicate the value of Pin at
which the measurements in the Main Text were taken (−30 dBm). The change in resonance frequency at high readout
power in Fig. S.4(a) is commonly observed in coupled resonator-rf-SQUID systems under strong driving, and could
signify non-linear effects such as over-driving of the resonator or saturation of the available ABSs [43, 56]. Moreover,
the internal quality factor Qi was also reduced as the readout power was increased beyond −30 dBm as shown in
panel (c). Yet, when the gate voltage was set to VG = −1.66 V, these effects were no longer present: the resonance
frequency and Qi remained constant as the readout power was increased as depicted in panels (b) and (d). Based on
these results, we conclude that the change in resonance frequency and Qi were related to the density of ABSs. At
VG = −1.66 V, no ABSs were available for microwave-induced transitions at Φ = 0, because in this configuration the
JJ was likely in the ballistic short junction regime (L < le ≪ ξ). At VG = 0 V lateral ABSs, which were extended
longer than le and comparable to ξ, were present in the JJ, yielding a minigap with many ABSs with low and high
transmission probabilities [45, 46]. Therefore, at high readout power the number of transitions between ABSs was
increased, resulting in a larger number of loss channels and thus a decrease in Qi in Fig. S.4(c) [57]. Moreover, the
transitions to ABSs at higher energy, that have lower transmission, reduced the supercurrent in the rf-SQUID, yielding
an increase of the inductance of the JJ for high readout powers and thus a decrease in the resonance frequency.

VG = −0.8 V, the resonance frequency remained nearly unaffected because ABSs with low transmission were depleted,

transitions at Φ = 0 and a reduction in the transmission of individual ABSs. This interpretation is consistent with
the two-tone spectroscopy data in Figs. 3 and 4 of the Main Text.
