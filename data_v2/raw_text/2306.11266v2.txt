Crossover from Cooper-pair hopping to single-electron hopping in Pbx(TiO2)1−x
granular films

Zhi-Hao He, Zi-Yan Han, Kuang-Hong Gao, Zi-Wu Wang, and Zhi-Qing Li∗

Tianjin Key Laboratory of Low Dimensional Materials Physics and Preparing Technology,
Department of Physics, Tianjin University, Tianjin 300354, China
(Dated: February 5, 2024)

The electrical transport properties of Pbx(TiO2)1−x (x being the Pb volume fraction and ranging
from ∼0.45 to ∼0.69) granular films are investigated experimentally. The charging energy of the
Pb granules is reduced to less than the superconducting gap of Pb granules for the low temperature
insulating films by using high-k dielectric TiO2 as the insulating matrix. For the insulating films
in the vicinity of the superconductor-insulator transition, Cooper-pair hopping governs the low-
temperature hopping transport. For these films, the low-temperature magnetoresistance is positive
at low field and the resistivity vs temperature for Cooper-pair hopping obeys an Efros-Shklovsii-type
variable-range-hopping law. A crossover from Cooper-pair-dominated hopping to single-electron-
dominated hopping is observed with decreasing x.
The emergence of single-electron-dominated
hopping in the more insulating films is due to the causation that the intergrain Josephson coupling
becomes too weak for Cooper pairs to hop between adjacent superconducting Pb granules.

I.
INTRODUCTION

Disordered superconductors have attracted great at-
tention over the past few decades since a range of novel
phenomena, such as superconductor-insulator transition
in two-dimensional superconducting films [1–8], anoma-
lous metallic state [9–16], macroscopic quantum tunnel-
ing effect [17–20], and disorder-induced inhomogeneity
(emergence of superconducting islands or puddles) [21–
24], are related to the interplay between the supercon-
ductivity and localization.
Among the disordered su-
perconductors, granular superconductor is an important
experimentally accessible model system, which offer a
unique testing ground for studying tunable combined ef-
fects of disorder, Coulomb interactions, and supercon-
ductivity [25]. The knowledge on these effects is impor-
tant in understanding the properties of high-temperature
superconductors (the disorder-induced granularity was
evidenced in high-temperature superconductors [26–29])
as well as in understanding the behaviors of devices re-
lated to Josephson junctions [30–35]. In granular super-
conductors, the ground state of the system is ultimately
determined by the competition of Josephson coupling en-
ergy EJ and charging energy Ec. The granular super-
conducting arrays with EJ < Ec are insulators, while
those with EJ > Ec are superconductors at low temper-
ature [25]. In the insulating regime and below the lo-
cal superconducting transition temperature T L
c , Cooper
pairs are localized on the granules with sufficiently large
size by Coulomb repulsion. Thus an interesting question
is whether the electric transport below T L
c is dominated
by the hopping of Cooper pairs or single electrons at the
insulating regime.
Theoretically, it has been proposed that the current
through a superconducting grain is sensitive to the rela-

tion between the superconducting gap ∆ and the charg-
ing energy Ec. For Ec > ∆, the transport is governed
by single-electron hopping, while the hopping trans-
port is mediated by Cooper pairs in the opposite case
Ec < ∆ [36–38]. When a magnetic field is applied, the
single-electron-hopping could result in a negative mag-
netoresistance and the Cooper-pair hopping should lead
to a positive magnetoresistance due to the suppression
of the order parameter [4]. In addition, Lopatin and Vi-
nokur further proposed that the temperature dependence
of conductivity would obey the Efros-Shklovsii (ES) type
variable-range-hopping (VRH) law in the Cooper pair
hopping regime [38]. In the insulating regime of granular
superconductors, the single-electron-hopping conduction
is widely observed in experiments [39–42]. However, the
Cooper pair hopping transport has only been reported in
a limited number of low dimensional granular systems, in-
cluding one-dimensional Josephson junction chains [33],
two-dimensional (2D) Josephson junction arrays [30], and
2D InOx and In-InOx granular films [43, 44].
In 2D
Josephson junction arrays, the temperature dependence
of the Cooper pair hopping conductance was found to
obey the Arrhenius form equation [30], while an ES-
type-VRH conductance was observed in the 2D InOx
and In/InOx granular films [43, 44]. Thus, more com-
prehensive investigations on the transport properties of
Cooper pair hopping in granular superconductor are de-
sirable. On the other hand, the physical properties in
three-dimensional (3D) systems may differ from those
in low-dimensional systems. It is therefore nontrivial to
check whether the Cooper pair hopping, as well as the
ES-type-VRH conduction of Cooper pair, could be real-
ized in 3D granular superconductors.

To satisfy the condition for Cooper-pair hopping,
Ec < ∆, one needs to choose a suitable superconductor-
insulator granular system with relative small Ec and large
∆. For a superconductor-insulator granular composite,
the charging energy of a grain can be estimated via
Ec ≃ e2/(4πǫ0˜κa) [36], where e is the electronic charge,

ǫ0 is the permittivity of free space, ˜κ is the effective di-
electric constant and proportional to the permittivity of
the insulator constituent ǫr, and a is the mean grain size.
Therefore, an effective way of reducing the charging en-
ergy is to select a high-k dielectric as the insulating ma-
trix of the granular composites.
In the present paper, we successfully realized the
Cooper-pair hopping transport and observed the ES-
type-VRH conductivity [45–48] of Cooper pairs in the in-
sulating regime (near the superconductor-insulator tran-
sition) of Pbx(TiO2)1−x (x being the Pb volume fraction)
granular films. A crossover from Cooper-pair hopping to
single-electron hopping is also observed as the films are
extended to a more insulating region. It is noted that
the room-temperature permittivity of TiO2 is ∼10 (∼20)
times as large as that of Al2O3 (SiO2) [49], and the zero-
temperature superconducting gap of Pb is 1.37 meV [50],
being larger than the gaps of most elemental supercon-
ductors.

II.
EXPERIMENTAL METHOD

The Pbx(TiO2)1−x films with x ranging from ∼0.45 to
∼0.69 were deposited on glass substrates at room tem-
perature by co-sputtering Pb and TiO2 targets in Ar at-
mosphere. The details of the deposition procedures are
similar to those described in Ref. [42]. The thicknesses
of the films (∼400 to ∼600 nm) were measured using a
surface profiler (Dektak, 6M). The Pb volume fraction
x in each film was obtained from the energy-dispersive
x-ray spectroscopy analysis (EDS; EDAX, model Apollo
X). The microstructure of the films was characterized
by transmission electron microscopy (TEM; Tecnai G2
F20). The resistance as well as the current vs voltage
were measured using the standard four probe method
and the temperature and magnetic field environments
were provided by a physical property measurement sys-
tem (PPMS-6000, Quantum Design). For films with large
x, both the current source and the voltmeter were pro-
vided by the model 6000 PPMS controller, while for the
small-x films, a Keithley 236 and a Keithley 2182A were

III.
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Figure 1(a) and 1(b) show the bright-field TEM im-
ages for two representative films with x ≃ 0.45 and 0.52,
respectively. The dark and bright regions represent Pb
granules and TiO2 matrix, respectively. Both the elec-
tron diffraction and x-ray diffraction results (not shown)
indicate that TiO2 is amorphous.
Thus the films are
granular composites in which the Pb granules are em-
bedded in the amorphous TiO2 matrix. The mean size
of Pb granules, a, obtained by taking into account ∼80
grains for each film, decreases with decreasing x, e.g.,
the mean grain size is ∼6.1 nm for the x ≃ 0.69 film, de-
creases to ∼5.1 nm when x ≃ 0.52, and further decreases
to ∼4.3 nm as x is reduced to 0.45. Since the thickness
of the film is at least 50 times larger than the mean size
of Pb granules, the Pb granules form three-dimensional
granular arrays.

been explained by a model that includes both thermally

the x ≃ 0.55 and 0.52 films, the temperature dependence
of resistivity exhibits a reentrant behavior, i.e., the re-

According to Parendo et al. [51], in
granular films of amorphous bismuth the drop in resis-
tivity is due to the occurrence of superconductivity on
the isolated grains below the superconducting transition
temperature of the grain, while the increase in resistivity
at low temperature is caused by the opening of the energy
gap on the superconducting grains coupled by quasipar-
ticle tunneling (hopping). Then if the charging energy
Ec is less than the superconducting gap ∆, the Cooper
pairs do not have to decompose into electrons, and could
conduct directly among the grains via ES-VRH [38]. (For
the Pbx(TiO2)1−x films, which mechanism makes the re-
sistivity rise at low temperature will be amply studied
below.) For the x ≲ 0.50 films, the resistivity increases
with decreasing temperature and increases more rapidly
below ∼7.0 K. We will focus our attention on the four
films with x ≲ 0.52 in the following discussion.
Before presenting the hopping transport results for
those x ≲ 0.52 films, we first briefly discuss the low-
temperature transport properties of the films with global
superconductivity (x ≳ 0.59). Figure 3(a) and 3(b) show
the temperature dependence of the normalized resistiv-
ity ρ/ρN at different temperatures for the x ≃ 0.59 and
0.69 films, respectively. Here the values of ρN were taken
as the resistivity at 15 K for each film. Clearly, the re-
sistivity at each measured temperature increases with in-
creasing field, revealing typical characteristics of a type-II
superconductor. For type-II superconductors, the upper
critical field Hc2 for superconducting transition is gener-
ally defined as the field at which the resistivity reaches
to ρN/2. From Fig. 3(a), one can see that the value of
Hc2 for the x ≃ 0.59 film is much greater than 7.0 T
at 4.0 K, while its value decreases to ∼5.6 T at 5.0 K.
For the x ≃ 0.69 film, the values of Hc2 are greater
than 7.0 T below ∼4.0 K and decrease to 5.5 and 2.7 T
at 5.0 and 6.0 K, respectively. At a fixed temperature,
Hc2 increases with decreasing x for films with x ranging
from 0.59 to 0.69, which is quite similar to that for the
Pbx(SiO2)1−x films with global superconductivity [42].

For our Pbx(TiO2)1−x films, the percolation threshold
xc for metal to insulator transition is estimated to be
xc ≃ 0.55. Thus, in the films with global superconduc-
tivity there are many conducting paths composed of ge-
ometrically connected Pb granules. Then the charge car-
riers are mainly transported through those paths and do
not need to hop between Pb granules in the conducting
process. The Cooper pair hopping or single-electron hop-
ping conduction, therefore, cannot be observed in these
films.
Figure 4(a) and 4(b) show the normalized resistivity
ρ(H)/ρ(0) as a function of magnetic field µ0H (with µ0
being the permeability of free space) at different temper-
atures for the x ≃ 0.52 and 0.50 films, where ρ(H) and
ρ(0) are the resistivities at H and zero field, respectively.
Below ∼4 K, the resistivity increases with increasing field
at low field, peaks at HP(T ), then decreases with further
increasing field above HP(T ), and finally tends to be less
than 1.0 at high field regime (i.e., the magnetoresistance
tends to be negative under high field). The field HP(T )
is sample dependent and decreases with increasing tem-
perature for each film. For a single Pb grain, the critical
size for existing superconductivity is ∼2.0 nm [52], thus
the granules remain superconducting in our insulating
Pbx(TiO2)1−x films. The behaviors of low-temperature
magnetoresistances strongly suggest that the transport
process is governed by Cooper-pair hopping at least in
low field regime:
the magnetic field can destroy the
Josephson coupling between the superconducting grains
and suppress the amplitude of superconducting order pa-
rameter in each individual grain; when the magnetic field
is relative small, the order parameter amplitude is almost
unchanged, while the intergrain Josephson coupling is
gradually suppressed with increasing field [4], which re-
duces the hopping probability of Cooper pairs between
the superconducting grains and thus leads to a positive
magnetoresistance.
Along with increasing field, the charging energy of
some Pb granules with small size will be larger than the
energy gap ∆(H). (The evolution of ∆ with field can
be estimated via ∆(H) = ∆(0)[1 − (H/Hc2)1.6]3/2 [30].)

This opens the channels for single-electron hopping, and
will slow down the increasing rate (dρ/dH) of the resis-
tivity. At HP(T ), the decreasing rate of the conductiv-
ity [53] caused by the reduction in the concentration of
Cooper pairs (RCCP) for hopping is equal to the increas-
ing rate of the conductivity resulted from the growth in
the concentration of single-electron (GCSE) for hopping,
ρ(H) reaches its maximum. Above HP(T ), the decreas-
ing rate of the conductivity by RCCP is less than the
increasing rate of the conductivity by GCSE, the resis-
tivity will decreases with further increasing field. When
the reduction of the conductivity by RCCP is as large
as the increment of the conductivity by GCSE, the mag-
netoresistance will be zero [ρ(H) = ρ(0)]. Since ∆(H)
increases with decreasing temperature, the magnetore-
sistance peak, as well as the field for ρ(H) = ρ(0), will
shift to a higher field with decreasing temperature, which
is what we see in Fig. 4(a) and 4(b).
In fact, for the
x ≃ 0.52 and 0.50 films, Cooper pair hopping still dom-
inates the low-temperature transport process even at a
relatively high field used in the measurements. For ex-
ample, when a field with magnitude of 8.0 T is applied,
the increments (reductions) of the conductivities (resis-
tivities) for the x = 0.50 and 0.52 films are less than 11%
(10%) and 28% (22%), respectively, at 3.0 K, which indi-
cates that hopping transport is still governed by Cooper
pair hopping at this situation.

We evaluate the average charging energies of the in-
sulating Pbx(TiO2)1−x films using Ec = e2/(4πǫ0˜κa).
The effective dielectric constant can be estimated via
˜κ = ǫr(a + s)/s with s being the intergrain spacing be-
tween neighboring grains [54]. Assuming the Pb parti-
cles form a simple cubic lattice, one can roughly obtain
s via x = a3/(a + s)3. Taking ǫr of TiO2 as ∼86 [49],
we obtain the values of Ec for the films in the dielec-
tric regime and list them in Table I. Clearly, the charg-
ing energy increases with decreasing x, and satisfies the
condition for Cooper-pair hopping (Ec < ∆0 with the
zero-temperature gap ∆0 ≃ 1.37 meV for the Pb gran-
ules) for the x ≲ 0.52 films. For comparison, we give the
low temperature ρ(H)/ρ(0) vs µ0H data in Fig. 4(c) for
a Pb0.50(SiO2)0.50 film with thickness of ∼1 µm and fab-
ricated previously [42]. This film is in insulating regime,
and its resistivities at 2.0, 3.0, and 4.0 K are 1086, 987
and 722 Ω cm, respectively. For this film, taking a and
ǫr as 7.9 nm and 4.5 [54], respectively, one can obtain
Ec ≃ 8.4 meV, which is much greater than the energy
gap ∆0 of Pb granules.

and 0.55) films and negative magnetoresistance in the

Figure 5(a) and 5(b) show the low-temperature mag-
netoresistances of the x ≃ 0.47 and 0.45 Pbx(TiO2)1−x
films, respectively.
Interestingly, the low-temperature
magnetoresistances of the two films are all negative, in-
dicating that single-electron hopping dominates the hop-
ping transport. In the insulating Pbx(TiO2)1−x granular
films, the average separation between two adjacent Pb
granules increases with decreasing x, which will greatly
enhance the normal-state intergrain resistance RN. On

the other hand, the Josephson energy EJ between neigh-
bor grains is EJ = RQ∆/2RN, where RQ = h/4e2 is the
quantum resistance of Cooper pair [25]. For a 3D periodic
cubic granular array, the intergrain resistance RN can be
estimated via RN ≃ ρN/a with ρN being the normal-
state resistivity. Taking the normal-state resistivity ρN
as the resistivity at 15 K, we obtained the average EJ
between neighbor grains for the x ≲ 0.52 films and listed
them in Tabe I. From Tabe I, one can see that the Joseph-
son energies of the 0.47 and 0.45 films are reduced by ∼2
orders of magnitude compared to that of the x ≃ 0.50
film. In this situation, the intergrain Josephson coupling
becomes too weak for Cooper pairs to hop between the
adjacent superconducting Pb granules. As a result, the
electrical transport process undergoes a transition from
a Cooper-pair-dominated hopping to a single-electron-
dominated hopping as x decreases from 0.50 to 0.47.

Before finishing up, we compare the superconductor to
insulator transition (SIT) of the 3D Pbx(TiO2)1−x films
with that observed in other material systems. From a
macro point of view, the overall properties of the 3D
Pbx(TiO2)1−x films are consistent with those observed in
other granular systems, including 3D Al-AlOx films [57],
3D Al-Ge films [40, 58], 3D Pb-SiO2 films [42, 59], 2D
In-InOx films [44], and quench-evaporated 2D granular
films (ultrathin films of Sn, Pb, Ga, Al, and In) [2, 7, 8].
The commonalities of these systems are that they all
experience global superconductivity, transition, and in-
sulating regions.
In the global superconductivity re-
gion, the grains are strongly coupled and the system
can be treated as a dirty type-II superconductor.
In
this region, there are no significant differences in prop-
erties between the Pbx(TiO2)1−x films and other gran-
ular superconductor systems. In the transition region,
most granular superconductor systems would undergo
an intermediate anomalous metallic state and then re-
veal reentrant behavior before entering the insulating re-
gion [2, 7, 8, 15, 42, 59]. In the intermediate metallic
state, the resistance of the sample drops several decades
as temperature is lowered and tends to saturate at a
constant with further decreasing temperature. For the
3D Pbx(TiO2)1−x series, the intermediate metallic state
presents in the x ≃ 0.56 film and the reentrant behavior
occurs in the x ≃ 0.55 and 0.52 films as mentioned above.
While for the 3D Pbx(SiO2)1−x system, the intermediate
metallic state also appears in the films with x near ∼0.57,
and the reentrant behavior occurs in the 0.50 < x < 0.57
films [42].
For the 3D Pbx(TiO2)1−x system, the SIT
occurs near x ≃ 0.55 and 0.56. Thus, the average critical
junction resistance for SIT is ρN/a ∼ 5.8 × 106 Ω, which
is far larger than the theoretical value (9.7 kΩ) [60] and
about ∼4 times as large as that for the Pbx(SiO2)1−x
series [59].
In the insulating region, hopping conduc-
tance is prevalent and the temperature dependence of

the resistance of the samples generally exhibits a signifi-
cant increase in resistance below the local superconduct-
ing transition temperature T L
c due to opening an extra
gap ∆ [5, 61].
As mentioned in section I, the single-
electron hopping prevails in the insulator region of most
granular superconductors.
In the Pbx(TiO2)1−x films
with x ≃ 0.52 and 0.50, we observed the ES-type-VRH
Cooper-pair-hopping by using the high-k dielectric TiO2
as the insulating matrix.
IV.
CONCLUSION

In summary, we successfully realized Cooper-pair hop-
ping in insulating Pbx(TiO2)1−x (x ≃ 0.50, 0.52, and
0.55) granular films by using the high-k dielectric TiO2
as the insulating matrix.
For these films, the low-
temperature magnetoresistances are positive under low
field, and the temperature dependent behavior of resis-
tivity obeys the ES-type VRH law in low-temperature
region. As x is reduced from ∼0.50 to ∼0.47, a crossover
from Cooper-pair-dominated hopping to single-electron-
dominated hopping is observed. These x ≲ 0.47 films
possess negative magnetoresistance at low temperatures
and the resistivity vs temperature obeys a law of thermal-
activation form. For the x ≲ 0.47 Pbx(TiO2)1−x films,
the intergrain Josephson coupling is too weak for Cooper
pairs to hop between the adjacent superconducting Pb
granules, and the low-temperature transport can only be
mediated by single-electron hopping.
