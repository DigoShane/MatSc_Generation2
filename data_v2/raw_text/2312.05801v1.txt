in Hybrid Magnetic Multilayer Nanodots

Ambient magnetic skyrmions stabilized in multilayer nanostructures are of immense interest due to their
relevance to magnetic tunnel junction (MTJ) devices for memory and unconventional computing applica-
tions. However, existing skyrmionic nanostructures built using conventional metallic or oxide multilayer
nanodots are unable to concurrently fulfill the requirements of nanoscale skyrmion stability and feasibility
of all-electrical readout and manipulation. Here, we develop a few-repeat hybrid multilayer platform con-
sisting of metallic [Pt/CoB/Ir]3 and oxide [Pt/CoB/MgO] components that are coupled to evolve together
as a single, composite stack. Zero-field (ZF) skyrmions with sizes as small as 50 nm are stabilized in the
hybrid multilayer nanodots, which are smoothly modulated by up to 2.5× by varying CoB thickness and
dot sizes. Meanwhile, skyrmion multiplets are also stabilized by small bias fields. Crucially, we observe
higher order ”target” skyrmions with varying magnetization rotations in moderately-sized, low-anisotropy
nanodots. These results provide a viable route to realize long-sought skyrmionic MTJ devices and new
possibilities for multi-state skyrmionic device concepts.

Introduction

1. Magnetic skyrmions are nanoscale spin textures
with fixed handedness, or chirality1,2.
They can be
stabilized at room temperature in commercially vi-
able multilayered magnetic thin films3–5, wherein they
exhibit efficient current-induced motion6, nucleation7,
and
topological
dynamics8,9.
These
device
at-
tributes
are
promising
for
applications
in
next-
generation memory10–12, logic13–15, and computing
technologies16–18. The stability and character of chi-
ral spin textures are governed by the interplay of con-
ventional and chiral magnetic interactions, with the
latter arising from heavy metal (HM) − ferromagnet
(FM) interfaces5,19,20. Within nanoscale device geome-
tries, confinement effects considerably influence their
formation and evolution3,21,22. A comprehensive pic-
ture of skyrmion stability in ultra-thin nanodots is es-
pecially crucial for robust realizations of highly sought-
after magnetic tunnel junctions (MTJs) towards all-
electrical readout and manipulation2,23.
2. Early works on confining nanoscale skyrmions
predominantly utilized numerous stacked repetitions
of
metallic
multilayers
([HM/FM/HM]N,
N
>
10)4,6,22,24, wherein texture stability is aided consid-
erably by interlayer dipolar interactions25.
In con-
trast, MTJ compatibility requires an ultrathin metallic
multilayer, capped with a single oxide layer23. How-
ever, chiral nanodots comprising a single oxide trilayer
(HM/FM/oxide) nucleate skyrmions only over a nar-
row window of magnetic and geometric parameters3,26,
with limited utility for scalable MTJ development.
In contrast, for few-repeat (N ≤ 3) metallic multi-
layers, spin texture stability is considerably enhanced
through interlayer exchange coupling (IEC)27,28, with
the added ability to generate strong spin-orbit torques

for efficient manipulation29,30. This motivates case for
nanostructures comprising metallic and oxide multi-
layers, to realize stable skyrmions with electrical de-
tection, and efficient dynamics.
3. In
this
work,
we
develop
few-repeat
hybrid
chiral multilayer nanodots by combining metallic
(Pt/CoB/Ir) and oxide (Pt/CoB/MgO) stacks, and
engineering them to evolve together.
Using magne-
tometry, magnetic force microscopy (MFM), and mi-
cromagnetic simulations, we show that such nanodots
behave as one composite stack, with chiral spin tex-
tures stabilized over a wide range of FM thicknesses
and dot sizes, including at ZF. The spin textures ex-
hibit a variety of configurations, including single, mul-
tiple and higher-order skyrmions, and exhibit marked
variation with magnetic parameters, geometry, and ex-
ternal field. This provides a strategic platform to in-
tegrate the benefits of metallic and oxide multilayer
stacks, with immediate relevance to functional spin-
tronic devices.

5. Our multilayer films (schematic: Fig. 1(a)) com-
bine two distinct chiral stacks – a metallic stack
[Pt/CoB(x)/Ir]3 (represented as CoBM(x)) and ox-
ide component [Pt/CoB(y)/MgO] (CoBO(y)). The 3-
repeat chiral CoBM stack was shown to host skyrmions
with tunable properties at film-level32,33, and enables
their spin-orbit torque driven nucleation and dynam-
ics in devices29,30. Meanwhile, the CoBO multilayer is
capped with MgO, which can serve as a tunnel bar-
rier for electrical detection of chiral spin textures34–37.
The two component stacks are integrated by a metal-
lic Ir(1)/Pt(1) spacer, which is known to exhibit strong
IEC28,38.

6. The stack components were carefully engineered to
ensure their evolution as one chiral multilayer. First,
magnetometry measurements were performed to de-
termine the out-of-plane (OP) saturation fields, Hs, of
the individual CoBM and CoBO, in each case, over a
range of CoB thicknesses (CoBM: 1.6–2.2 nm; CoBO:
1.0–1.4 nm). These measurements were used to cal-
ibrate and match pairs of CoBM and CoBO stacks
with similar Hs values (see SI §S1). For example, in
Fig. 1(b), the Hs for CoBM(2.2) (blue) and CoBO(1.4)
(red) is ∼180 mT. Finally, the double-wedged com-
posite stack was fabricated using matched CoBM
and CoBO thicknesses to ensure coherent switching
of the hybrid multilayer.
Fig. 1(b) shows the re-
sulting OP magnetization hysteresis loop, M(H), of
the CoBM(2.2)/CoBO(1.4) stack (green), which ex-
hibits the distinctive shear characteristic of chiral
multilayers.
Meanwhile, wave-vector resolved BLS
spectroscopy measurements, shown in Fig. 1(c) for
CoBM(2.2) and CoBO(1.4), establish the left-handed
N´eel chirality of both stack components.
The lin-
ear dispersion of the counter propagating spin-waves
(Fig. 1(d)) was used to determine the iDMI, D39, found
to be 0.42 mJ/m2 (for CoBM(2.2)) and 1.06 mJ/m2

(for CoBO(1.4)), respectively.
The magnitude and
sign of the iDMI values are in line with published re-
ports on similar metallic40,41 and oxide42,43 multilay-
ers, and are sufficiently large to stabilize homochiral
spin textures28.

7. The stability and characteristics of chiral spin tex-
tures in multilayers are determined by the inter-
play of symmetric exchange, A, iDMI, and effective
anisotropy, Keff 5,20, each of which may vary across
the wedged multilayer. For this work, D, quantified
for the respective stack components in Fig. 1(d), is
treated as approximately constant across the wedge,
as the CoB thickness varies by <
30% for both
stack components44.
Similarly, A is also expected
to vary negligibly, given the < 10% variation in the
measured saturation magnetization, Ms across CoB
thicknesses45.
In contrast, the Keff should be read-
ily tuned with varying CoB thickness32. Indeed, the
M(H) loops in Fig. 1(e) show that with increasing
CoB thickness, the easy axis of the hybrid stack tran-
sitions from OP to in-plane (IP) orientation.

Skyrmion Stability

8. Fig.
2(a)
shows
representative
ZF
MFM
im-

2.2)/CoBO(1.1–1.4) thicknesses and W over 300—1000

The nanodots exhibited five distinct mag-
netic states at ZF, identified as uniform magneti-
zation (UM), skyrmion(s) (SK), labyrinthine stripes
(LS), mixed-state (MIX), and target skyrmions (TSK).
While the first three states (UM, SK, LS) are well-
established6,22, a mixed state reflects the presence
of both SK and LS states.
Meanwhile,
target
skyrmions are higher-order skyrmions that appear as
annular or concentric domains, discussed in detail
subsequently46,47. As seen in Fig. 2(a), these ZF mag-
netic states evolve systematically with varying CoB
thickness, and W. First, for the thinnest CoB sam-
ple – CoBM(1.6)/CoBO(1.0) (not shown) the nanodots
display UM states across all W, with the preferential
stability of single domains arising from the substan-
tially larger film-level Keff (Fig. 1(f)) compared to the
other samples. Next, for CoBM(1.8)/CoBO(1.1) nan-
odots (Fig. 2(a): bottom), reducing W results in a
sharp transition from LS to UM states, albeit with-
out any intermediate states.
As the CoB thickness
is further increased across CoBM(1.9–2.2)/CoBO(1.2–
1.4) (Fig. 2(a): bottom to top), reducing W results in
transitions from LS, MIX, or TSK states to the SK
state, observed for W ∼ 400 − 600 nm. As W is fur-
ther reduced, the dot eventually transitions to the UM
state at ZF.

9. To elucidate the ZF stability of skyrmions con-
fined in multilayer nanodots, we note that the rel-
evant tuning parameter, Keff, includes contributions
from magneto-crystalline anisotropy, shape anisotropy
(Ks), and interface anisotropy (Ki)48.
A suitably
moderate Keff required for ZF skyrmion stability can
be achieved by modulating the counteracting effects
of reducing W, for larger Ks, and increasing CoB,
for smaller Ki, respectively.
To this end, we statis-
tically examine the stability of the varied magnetic
states across compositions and W, shown in Fig. 2(b),
obtained by characterizing ∼18 nominally identical
nanodots for each composition and W (see SI §3).
First, for CoBM(1.8)/CoBO(1.1) (Fig. 2(b): bottom),
the high Ki is inherently unfavourable for stabilizing
skyrmions5,20, and thus the sparse LS or mixed con-
figurations directly transition to a stable UM config-
uration at a relatively large W ≲ 600 nm. As Ki is
decreased (Fig. 2(b): bottom to top), LS, mixed, and
TSK states are stabilized at large W ≳ 700 nm, with
greater preference for mixed over LS states in compari-
son to film-level magnetic textures. For these composi-
tions, the modulation of Ks with W enables the stabi-
lization of ZF skyrmions for intermediate W (400–600
nm), wherein the required Keff is realized. Skyrmion
nucleation probabilities of up to 50% are observed in
400-500 nm-sized dots with optimal compositions. No-
tably, the optimal window for ZF skyrmion stability
shifts to smaller W, and increases in extent with in-
creasing CoB thickness, in line with the expected mod-
ulation of Ks and Ki respectively.

10. Next, we turn to our striking observation of tar-
get skyrmions, which present as concentric domains,
stabilized by confined geometries46.
We define RM

11. Here we observe ZF target skyrmions with RM of
2π and ∼300 nm sizes inherently stabilized in low-Ki-
nanodots of W ≳ 700 nm (Fig. 2(a): top right), and
occasionally, with higher-order RM (3π, i.e. two con-
centric rings) as well. The nucleation of TSKs in our
hybrid multilayer nanodots, and their absence in pre-
vious such works, can be attributed to several key fac-
tors: (a) the variation of magnetization between the
distinct, albeit coupled CoBM and CoBO stacks47, (b)
low interfacial anisotropy49, and (b) the presence of
moderate geometric confinement, and (d) few-repeat
stack geometry53, the confluence of which likely coerce
the elongated chiral stripes to coil onto themselves, or
induce the nucleation of a smaller skyrmion within an
oppositely-polarized skyrmion. While absent in corre-
sponding thin films, such target skyrmions are nucle-
ated with ∼10-15% probability at optimal dot sizes,
against LS and mixed states. Their optimal stability
at W ∼ 700 nm dots of thick CoB, suggest that, analo-
gous to conventional skyrmions, their stability can be
attributed to a window of Keff, arising from moder-
ately large W, and the low Ki of thick CoB. These con-

ditions may be required to support the in-plane curling
critical for the stability of higher-order skyrmions rel-
ative to LS and mixed states.
12. To elucidate the interplay of confinement and
anisotropy effects on skyrmion stability in our hybrid
multilayer nanodots, we performed grain-free micro-
magnetic simulations using the effective medium ap-
proximation. The stack structures and magnetic pa-
rameters were chosen to emulate their experimentally
studied counterparts (CoBM(1.6–2.2)/CoBO(1.0–1.4))
and the ZF states of nanodots were studied for a
range of W of 400–600 nm (Fig. 3, see SI §S4). As
seen in experiments, simulations support the stability
of TSKs in nanodots with near-zero or negative Keff
(Fig. 3: top half). Meanwhile for dots with interme-
diate Keff (CoBM(1.9)/CoBO(1.2)-equivalent), we ob-
serve a multi-state transition with reducing W, from
TSK to SK to UM states. Finally, for intermediate val-
ues of W and Keff, the simulations suggest the stability
of skyrmionic multiplets, which have been experimen-
tally observed in nanodots with higher repetitions22.
While the simulations qualitatively reproduce the ex-
perimentally observed trends of skyrmion stability evo-
lution with W and Ki, we note quantitative discrepan-
cies that inhibit one-to-one comparisons with experi-
ment.
One likely source is the imprecise magnitude
of interlayer exchange coupling (IEC), both within the
CoBM stack and between the CoBM - CoBO. In con-
trast to multilayers with high stack repetitions, where
dipolar effects assume greater importance25, IEC is
known to influence the stability of ZF spin textures in
few-repeat multilayers27,28,54. Future simulation works
may build on accurately determined IECs, e.g., us-
ing bespoke magnetometry techniques28,54,55, to quan-
titatively elucidate transitions between SK and TSK
states, towards their practical utilization.

Skyrmion Size Dependence

13. Next, we examine the evolution of skyrmion sta-
bility with in situ magnetic field (µ0H).
Fig. 4(a)
shows the MFM-imaged magnetic states for represen-

of W and µ0H. Expectedly, the W ≃ 300 nm dots re-

sistently form a single skyrmion over the field range.
Meanwhile, for the larger 500 and 600 nm dots, a small
OP field is sufficient to destabilize LS and TSK states,
giving way to skyrmion multiplets over moderate fields
(20–50 mT), which diminish to single skyrmions at
higher fields en route to saturation. Finally, the size
of skyrmions, dS, is also visibly influenced by the ap-
plied field, stack properties, and geometry, as detailed
below.

The ZF dS goes as low as ∼ 50 nm and
varies by up to 2.5× with CoB thicknesses. Expect-

15. Finally, we examine the field-dependence of dS for
the case of moderate Keff (CoBM(2.0)/CoBO(1.3)) by
imaging 9 nominally identical nanodots for each field
and W (Fig. 4(c)). Expectedly, smaller skyrmions are

Consistent with our previous

the emergence of skyrmion multiplets at finite fields,

the sub-100 nm ZF skyrmions stabilized in our hybrid

trends driven by stack properties and confinement ef-

Conclusion

16. In summary,
we report on chiral nanostruc-
tures harnessing a bespoke few-repeat hybrid multi-
layer, comprising metallic ([Pt/CoB(x)/Ir]3) and ox-
ide (Pt/CoB(y)/MgO) stack components, both de-
signed with matched switching fields, and strongly
coupled by IEC. The ZF magnetic states of the nan-
odots are determined by the interplay of dot-size-
induced confinement effects and CoB-thickness-driven
stack anisotropy. Smaller dots (W ≲ 600 nm) stabilize
ZF skyrmions over a substantial range of Keff and dot
sizes. The skyrmion size, as small as 50 nm at ZF,
can be tuned by up to 2.5× by engineering the stack
anisotropy and geometric parameters. Finally, large-
W, low-Keff dots host higher-order target skyrmions at
ZF with chiral rotation up to 3π, whose stability rela-
tive to skyrmionic multiplets can be tailored by small
bias fields realizable using stack engineering.

17. The presented stability of ZF skyrmions in our
few-repeat hybrid multilayer nanodots has practical
consequences for several efforts on chiral spin tex-
tures.
On one hand, the inherent realization of
higher-order skyrmions and their interplay with con-
ventional skyrmions in highly-scalable multilayer nan-
odots is expected to enable their much-sought func-
tionalization via device concepts harnessing topolog-
ical transitions18.
Notably, the stability of conven-
tional ZF skyrmions over a wide range of magnetic


6

and geometric parameters within MTJ-compatible hy-
brid multilayer nanodots is of critical importance,
given their limited stability in prior MTJ-compatible
stacks3,26.
Our hybrid stack provides a viable plat-
form for realizing electrical skyrmion readout and
manipulation55, and can be generalized to other mate-
