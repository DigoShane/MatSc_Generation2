Rapid cooling of the Cassiopeia A neutron star due to superfluid quantum criticality

Hao-Fu Zhu,1, 2 Guo-Zhu Liu,3, ∗ and Xufen Wu1, 2

1Department of Astronomy, University of Science and Technology of China, Hefei, Anhui 230026, China
2School of Astronomy and Space Science, University of Science and Technology of China, Hefei, Anhui 230026, China
3Department of Modern Physics, University of Science and Technology of China, Hefei, Anhui 230026, China

The rapid cooling of the neutron star in Cassiopeia A is speculated to arise from an enhanced
neutrino emission caused by the onset of 3P2-wave neutron superfluidity in the core. However, the
neutrino emissivity due to Cooper-pair breaking and formation is too small to yield the observed
cooling rate. Here, we show that such a rapid cooling can be explained once the non-Fermi liquid
behavior of the non-superfluid neutron liquid induced by superfluid quantum criticality is included
into the theoretical description of neutron star cooling, without assuming the existence of additional
energy loss processes. Our results indicate that the neutron star in Cassiopeia A remains in the
thermal relaxation stage, which is greatly prolonged by the non-Fermi liquid behavior. The good
agreement between our theoretical results and recent observational cooling data points to the pivotal
role played by superfluid quantum criticality in neutron stars.

Exploring the thermal evolution of neutron stars (NSs)
[1–4] is of paramount importance in astrophysics as it
provides crucial constraints on many quantities ranging
from the equation of state (EOS) of dense matter [5, 6] to
the transition temperatures of neutron superfluidity and
proton superconductivity [7, 8].
The Center Compact
Object (CCO) in the supernova remnant Cassiopeia A
(Cas A), which is first observed by the Chandra X-ray
Observatory in 1999 [9], exhibits a distinct rapid cooling
rate from many other NSs and thus has been extensively
investigated. This CCO is one of the youngest NS (≈
340 yr old) [10] and its observed X-ray spectrum is well
described by a model of a carbon atmosphere with weakly
magnetized (B ≤ 1011 G) [11]. Its surface temperature
Te had been observed [12] to decrease by 4%, from 2.12×
106 K to 2.04 × 106 K, during the decade of 2000-2009.
Subsequent analysis adjusted the cooling rate from 4%
to 2% during 2009-2019 [13–18]. However, such a rapid
cooling rate is still much greater than the one (≈ 0.7%)
predicted by the standard cooling paradigm dominated
by modified Urca (MU) processes [19, 20].
A variety of non-standard scenarios [21–33] have been
proposed to account for the exceptional rapid cooling of
Cas A NS. Among such scenarios, the minimal cooling
paradigm [22, 23] is more promising than others as it of-
fers the only natural explanation of the late-time cooling.
A key assumption of this paradigm is the recent onset of
spin-triplet 3P2-wave neutron superfluidity in the core
[22, 23]. At temperatures slightly below the transition
temperature Tcn(ρ), which depends on the density ρ, the
pairing gap is quite small and thermal fluctuations can
readily destroy Cooper pairs. Meanwhile, the unpaired
neutrons are recombined by the attractive nuclear force
into pairs.
The constant pair breaking and formation
(PBF) [22], also known as Cooper-pair formation [23],
enhances the neutrino emission [34–36]. This provides a
possible source for the extra energy loss needed to yield

the rapid cooling. Moreover, 1S0-wave proton supercon-
ductivity is also supposed [22, 23] to occur in the core.
However, the minimal cooling paradigm encounters a
severe challenge [17, 18, 37]. The neutrino emissivity of
the PBF process is formally given by qQPBF

a constant factor. To produce the rapid cooling of Cas A
NS, the factor q should be large enough. In Ref. [21], it is
assumed that q = 0.76. Shternin et al. [18] showed that
the observed rapid cooling can be explained only when
q = (0.4 − 2.6). Nevertheless, Leinson [37] found that q
could be as small as q = 0.19, which casts doubt on the
validity of minimal cooling paradigm. While such a small
q may be changed by the in-medium effects and relativis-
tic corrections [17], there is currently no known physical
mechanism that is capable of yielding the desired large
q-factor.
It thus appears [18] that either the minimal
cooling paradigm is incorrect, or it is incomplete due to
the ignorance of certain crucial physical ingredients.
In this paper, we propose that the non-Fermi liquid
(NFL) behavior of the dense neutron matter induced by
the superfluid quantum criticality (QC) plays a unique
role in the thermal evolution of NSs [38] and hence should
be carefully taken into account. The NFL behavior leads
to ∝ T ln T type corrections to the neutrons’ specific heat
Cn(T ) and the total neutrino emissivity Qν(T ) [38]. We
find that, once such singular corrections are included into
the theoretical description of NS cooling, the recent ob-
servational data of the redshifted effective temperature
T ∞
e
of Cas A NS can be reproduced even for small values
of q in the range 0.19 < q < 0.76 without resort to direct
Urca (DU) processes or other extra neutrino emission
processes. Our analysis show that this NS is currently
still in the thermal relaxation stage, since the thermal
relaxation time tw is drastically extended by the super-
fluid QC. The maximal values of superconducting and
superfluid critical temperatures, dubbed TCp and TCn,
respectively, can be constrained from our results.
QC is one of the central concepts of condensed matter
physics [39–49].
It comprises all the striking quantum
many-body effects emerging near the quantum critical
point between the disordered (symmetric) and ordered

(symmetry broken) phases. Normally, each continuous
quantum phase transition driven by symmetry-breaking
has its own QC. Indeed, a variety of unusual properties
of many condensed matter systems are believed to be
caused by some type (e.g., anti-ferromagnetic, nematic,
excitonic) of QC [41–49].
Especially, superconducting
QC is found to exhibit intriguing behaviors in certain
Dirac/Weyl fermion systems [46–48]. It is interesting to
explore whether similar phenomena emerge in NSs. In a
recent work [38], we introduced the concept of QC into
the area of NS and performed the first theoretical study
of superfluid QC near the critical density that separates
a U(1)-symmetric normal neutron liquid from a U(1)-
symmetry-broken neutron superfluid.
The minimal cooling scenario [22, 23] assumes that
the PBF processes occur within a narrow temperature
range below Tcn and the neutrons form a normal FL
above Tcn. Such a normal FL assumption of the non-
superfluid phase is premature because it naively ignores
the breakdown of FL. Mean-field calculations [7, 8] and
phenomenological analysis [8] found that the spin-triplet
3P2-wave neutron superfluid exists within a finite den-
sity range ρc1 < ρ < ρc2. See the upper panel of Fig. 1
for a schematic illustration. Near the critical densities
ρc1 and ρc2, the quantum fluctuations of the superfluid
order parameter φ are significant [38], even though the
expectation value of φ vanishes (⟨φ⟩ = 0). Such fluctu-
ations fundamentally alter the properties of the gapless
neutrons excited on the Fermi surface and drives them
to exhibit NFL behavior at all temperatures around ρc1
and ρc2 [38].

The rapid cooling caused by this internal thermal
non-equilibrium cooperates with the PBF processes to
produce the steep slope of the cooling data shown in
Fig. 2. Heinke and Ho [12] attributed the rapid cooling
of Cas A NS to its being in the thermal relaxation stage.
Our cooling scenario provides a microscopic mechanism
for this hypothesis. It turns out that the influence of su-
perfluid QC on the internal thermal equilibrium process
is nontrivial and deserves further investigations.
The tension between the large q (q ≥ 0.4) demanded
by minimal cooling paradigm [18] and the much smaller
q (q = 0.19) determined by microscopic calculations [37]
has sparked a fierce debate on the efficiency of minimal
cooling paradigm [17, 18, 33]. To examine whether our
cooling scenario still works well for small q, we also com-
puted the cooling curve by choosing two small values:
q = 0.38 and q = 0.19. Our results are compared solely
with the cooling data accumulated during the decade of
2009-2019, which, due to the remarkable advancements
in both the instrument calibration and the data analysis
techniques, are more reliable than those measured from
2000 to 2009. As shown in Figs. 3 and 4, most of the
cooling data can still be re-produced for q = 0.38 and

q = 0.19 provided that the parameters h, TCp and ∆M
take appropriate values.
Within our cooling scenario, the rapid cooling of Cas
A NS can be explained for any q greater than q = 0.19
once the NFL corrections induced by superfluid QC are
considered. This is the most prominent advantage of our
cooling scenario. We do not require the presence of DU
processes [33], which occur under extremely stringent
conditions [55], or any extra exotic energy loss process
(e.g., axion emission). Unlike the artificial manipulation
of adopting a large q-factor, our cooling scenario is based
on the NFL effects of superfluid QC and thus has a solid
physical ground. Notice that the superfluid QC emerges
naturally as long as a zero-T superfluid transition takes
place at some critical density [38]. Therefore, superfluid
QC is a concomitant of neutron superfluidity, rather than
an additional assumption. Hopefully, the inclusion of the
superfluid QC into the minimal cooling paradigm would
lead to a more complete theory of NS cooling.
Figure 4 shows that the agreement between theoretical
cooling curves and observed cooling data becomes less
satisfactory as q takes very small values, especially for
the first three observational data. The agreement may
be improved if the APR EOS used in our calculations
is replaced by some other more realistic EOS, which is
subjected to future works.
One can see from Fig. 3 that removing PBF processes
(q = 0) results in a much lower cooling rate during the
thermal relaxation stage, which drives the cooling curve
to deviate dramatically from the cooling data. This is a
clear indication that PBF processes are essential in the
interpretation of rapid cooling.
The applicability of the generalized cooling scenario
proposed in this work could be testified by keeping mon-
itoring the cooling trajectory of Cas A NS. As depicted
by Fig. 4, the cooling curves obtained at different sets of
model parameters predict distinct cooling rates. Based
on the cooling data to be observed in the next two
decades, it is possible to verify or falsify the prediction
of our cooling scenario and estimate the values of q, h,
TCp, and other parameters with a higher accuracy.
