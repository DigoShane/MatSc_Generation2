I.
INTRODUCTION

The Dirac delta function or impulse function ќі(t) is
a generalized function that has a wide range of applica-
tions in various fields of sciences and engineering1. Con-
ceptually, it represents a function singular at a point on
the real axis and zero elsewhere, and the integral of any
continuous function when multiplied by the Dirac delta
function gives the value of the function at that specific
point. Its generalization may provide a convenient way
to work around its singular nature, which is the main
purpose of this work.
Our analysis in this work originates from the fact that
the delayed Dirac delta function ќі(t вИТ 1) is the inverse
Laplace transform of the exponential decaying function
eвИТs. From there, a fractional generalization of the delta
function associated with the inverse Laplace transform of
the Mittag-Leffler (ML) function Eќ±(вИТs) arises in terms
of the M-Wright function Mќ±(t)2вАУ4, given that the ML
function can be viewed as a generalization of the expo-
nential function5. A schematic illustration of the Laplace
transform pair ќі(t вИТ 1) and eвИТs, and its (fractional) gen-
eralization Mќ±(t) and Eќ±(вИТs) is depicted in Fig. 1, and
summarizes the principal result of this work.

tion II we present an overview of the standard delta func-
tion and its main properties, the ML function and its
inverse Laplace transform, as well the representation of

IV.
CONCLUSION

In this paper we studied the fractional generaliza-
tion of the Laplace transform pair ќі(t вИТ 1) √Ј eвИТs giving
Mќ±(t) √Ј Eќ±(вИТs) with focus on the behavior and applica-
tion of (i) the fractional-order delta function Mќ±(t) and
(ii) its integral giving the fractional-order unit step func-
tion. Both functions are crucial for the evaluation of the
impulse response and step response of circuits and sys-
tems. We have experimentally demonstrated the current
charging of a supercapacitor using the fractional delta
function, and hence its fractional impulse response ob-
tained in the form of the measured voltage. The results
fully agree with the numerical modeling of the device.
The results may find many other applications in prob-
lems associated with differential and integral equations
of fractional order arising in different disciplines of phys-
ical sciences and engineering.
