We report on the evolution of the charge density wave (CDW) and spin density wave (SDW) order of a
chromium film following photoexcitation with an ultrafast optical laser pulse. The CDW is measured by
ultrafast time-resolved x-ray diffraction of the CDW satellite that tracks the suppression and recovery of the
CDW following photoexcitation. We find that as the temperature of the film approaches a discontinuous phase
transition in the CDW and SDW order, the time scales of recovery increase exponentially from the expected
thermal time scales. We extend a Landau model for SDW systems to account for this critical slowing with
the appropriate boundary conditions imposed by the geometry of the thin film system. This model allows us
to assess the energy barrier between available CDW/SDW states with different spatial periodicities.

I.
INTRODUCTION

Significant condensed matter research is focused on
correlated electron materials in which emergent phenom-
ena arise from the interplay between degrees of free-
dom such as charge, spin, and lattice.1–3 Ultrafast pump
and probe techniques have opened possibilities for under-
standing how these degrees of freedom first uncouple and
then recouple and evolve dynamically, quantifying the en-
ergy scales in the system and advancing the possibility
of manipulating such phenomena for technological appli-
cation. Ultrafast dynamics and transient nonequilibrium
states from optical excitation have been observed in mag-
netic materials,4–7 including Ni,8,9 Py,10 rare earth tran-
sition metal alloys and heterostructures,11,12 FePt,13,14

and Sr2Cu3O4Cl2.15 On longer time scales, the degrees
of freedom recouple and reach equilibrium at the ini-
tial temperature as the sample cools. During the latter
process, studies in some magnetic materials, such as for
La1/3Sr2/3FeO316 and MnWO4,17 have shown a critical
slowing down of the recovery near discontinuous phase
transitions.
This work demonstrates this critical slowing of the
magnetic and structural ordering in elemental chromium
(Cr) thin films. In bulk, Cr is an antiferromagnet that
exhibits an incommensurate spin density wave (SDW)
and charge density wave (CDW) that leads to a peri-
odic lattice distortion as a second harmonic of its SDW
(see Figs. 1(a) and 1(b)).18–20 The wavelength of the
SDW/CDW evolves continuously with temperature dur-
ing cooling. Previous studies have shown that the CDW
order in thin film Cr takes on discrete wavelengths due

to the boundary conditions set by the substrate and sur-
face interfaces.21–23 Exploiting these discrete allowable
wavelengths and the time resolution of the free electron
laser x-ray source, we resolved the dynamics on ultrafast
time scales following photoexcitation.24–27 We observed
ultrafast SDW demagnetization and the transformation
of the static CDW into a dynamic coherent phonon with
transient enhancement of the CDW order at low laser
fluences.24,26

Here, we study the recovery of SDW and CDW orders
in thin film Cr following ultrafast photoexcitation at dif-
ferent initial temperatures. We observe two distinct time
scales to the CDW recovery. One time scale is consistent
with thermal recovery for a metallic film. The second
time scale ranges from thermal recovery time scales to
two orders of magnitude longer as the ground state tem-
perature of the system approaches the phase transition
in which the CDW and SDW undergo a discrete change
in wavelength. This critical slowing of the recovery in
the time domain provides a method of assessing the en-
ergy scales of different states in the system by comparing
the experimental results to a Landau model for the Cr
CDW/SDW systems to thin films with fixed boundaries.

B.
Time-resolved x-ray diffraction

Time-resolved x-ray diffraction measurements were
conducted at the XPP instrument at the Linac Coher-
ent Light Source (LCLS) x-ray free-electron laser, with
8.9 keV x-ray pulses of duration 15 fs. The sample was ex-
cited by 800 nm optical pulses with a duration of 45 fs and
fluences of 4.2 mJ/cm2, 7.4 mJ/cm2, and 9.2 mJ/cm2 at
initial sample temperatures ranging from 130 K to 300 K.
The inset in Fig. 1(c) shows a detector image where the
experimental geometry is aligned to fringe 7, and we
can observe the neighboring peaks with reduced inten-
sity captured by the Ewald sphere. The time-dependent
x-ray diffraction signal of several of the Laue fringes and

interfering CDW satellite peaks was measured to a max-
imum time delay of 400 ns following photoexcitation to
determine the evolution of the charge ordering in the film.

III.
RESULTS

We will focus our discussion on the response at 10 ps
and longer after photoexcitation. The sub-10-ps response
is discussed in detail in Refs. 24–27. Following photoex-
citation, a lattice expansion is observed through a shift
in the Bragg peak and Laue fringes consistent with lat-
tice heating through electron-phonon coupling, as shown
in Fig. 2(a).
This shift allows the temperature of the
sample to be monitored. In addition, the amplitudes of
some of the fringes also change, depending on the initial
sample temperature and the laser fluence. This change in
amplitude is attributed to a change in the CDW ordering
and the appearance, shift, or disappearance of the CDW
satellite peak. An example is shown in Fig. 2(b), which
shows the time-resolved CDW satellite peak intensity on
the 6th, 7th, and 8th Laue fringes following photoexcita-
tion with laser fluence 7.4 mJ/cm2 at an initial sample
temperature of 150 K. This is plotted on a log scale in
time, with the inset showing the initial response on a lin-
ear scale. The CDW intensities are normalized to the
initial CDW intensity on the 8th fringe. In the ground
state at 150 K, the CDW has 8.5 wavelengths between
the film interfaces, and the CDW satellite peak intensity
appears on the 8th Laue fringe. Within 10 ps, the lattice
temperature increases by about 125 K to 275 K24,27 (see
supplementary material Fig. S1), which heats the system
to where the CDW satellite peak intensity has completely
disappeared from the 8th fringe. The intensity of the 8th

fringe increases and recovers back to its initial configu-
ration within about 1 ns, a time scale that is consistent
with the thermal recovery of a metallic film.
Figure 2(b) shows that within 10 ps, the satellite peak
appears with a smaller intensity on the 7th fringe, consis-
tent with the preferred spin orientation at 275 K. The in-
tensity of the 7th fringe initially increases before decreas-
ing back to its initial intensity. This can be explained
by the change in intensity of the CDW satellite peak as
a function of temperature, which decreases to zero at
TN.23 At a lattice temperature of 275 K, the CDW peak
would be expected to appear entirely on fringe 7, and as
the system cools, the CDW peak intensity will increase
on this fringe before switching to fringe 8 as the sam-
ple cools past the hysteretic region. We also show that
there is little change in intensity of fringe 6. The dashed
lines in Fig. 2(b) show the expected CDW intensities on
each fringe calculated quasi-statically from the film tem-
perature (determined by the Bragg peak shift) and the
ground state CDW peak locations and amplitude.22,23

The time scales and intensities follow these expectations
reasonably well.

At 220 K, there is initially a

disappears when pumped and recovers back with greater

appears entirely on the 7th fringe, disappears with the

Interestingly, at 300 K, we see evidence of the persistence

remains incomplete after 400 ns, indicating that we may
be near another critical transition here that is not easily
determined from static measurements.
Since we expect the CDW satellite peak to appear pri-
marily on the 7th and 8th fringes for this film based on
previous static x-ray diffraction measurements,23 the in-
tensity of the other fringes, including the 6th, should have
little response to photoexcitation. Figure 3(c) shows this
response. At the lower temperatures, this proceeds as ex-
pected. However, at higher temperatures, there is some
indication of CDW satellite peak appearing on the 6th

fringe, which would be in agreement with Ref. 25. At
260 K, there is a small increase in intensity during the
thermal recovery. At 300 K, when the system is expected
to be excited to above TN, there is a significant drop and
then a rise in intensity, followed by a slower recovery.

and the discretized number of wavelengths between the
interfaces gives multiple distinct spin and charge configu-
rations of the film. The ground state at any temperature
is given by configuration with the minimum potential,
and the recovery time, then, reflects the energy required
to reconfigure the spins throughout the depth of the film

temperature, there is an energy barrier to reforming the

the data for our film. These energy barriers increase with
temperature in the measured ranges, resulting in the crit-
ical slowing that we have observed.

V.
CONCLUSION

In conclusion, we have observed the slowing down of
the recovery of CDW order following photoexcitation
from temperatures that approach a discontinuous hys-
teretic reorientation of spin and charge in thin film Cr to
several orders of magnitude longer than expected from
thermal recovery. An Arrhenius model allows us to make
an estimate of the energy barrier between these states.
We have extended the Landau SDW model to account
for the boundary pinning observed in films and describe
both the temperature dependence of the SDW and CDW
ordering and the evolution of the potential wells with
temperature.

Fringe intensities at all temperatures and fluences

Figure S2 compares the response of the 7th and 8th fringes to different laser fluences at
initial sample temperatures of 150 K, 170 K, and 190 K. The magnitude of the change of
the fringes in each case is consistent with what would be expected from heating the sample
by 75 K, 125 K, and 160 K for laser fluences of 4.2 mJ/cm2, 7.4 mJ/cm2, and 9.2 mJ/cm2,
respectively. In particular, the magnitude of the decrease in intensity of the 8th fringe with
the initial laser pulse is comparable for the medium and high laser fluence, where it is
expected that the satellite peak will completely disappear from the 8th fringe and appear on
the 7th fringe. For the lowest fluence, the drop is much lower, as the sample is not heated
to a temperature above the transition to 7.5 CDW wavelengths. At 190 K (see Fig. S2(c)),
the increase in intensity of the 7th fringe is initially lower for the highest fluence than for
the medium and lowest fluence, consistent with heating the film above the N´eel temperature
where the satellite peak disappears. We note, however, that the time scales for recovery are
independent of the laser fluence, changing only with the sample temperature. The recovery
at all three fluences for an initial temperature of 150 K occurs on the order of 1 ns. This is
increased by an order of magnitude at 170 K, and the reorientation has not occurred by 400
ns at any of the fluence at 190 K. Thus, the critical slowing depends on the initial sample
temperature but not on the strength of the photoexcitation.

where τ0 is the attempt time. The attempt time for magnetic systems varies widely from
10−11 to 10−8 s for different materials2 and is unknown for this system. Figure S7 shows the
calculated energy barrier for the reorientation from 7.5 to 8.5 CDW wavelengths for fluence
7.4 mJ/cm2 for attempt times of 10 ps, 100 ps, 250 ps, and 1 ns. There is a small change
in the temperature dependence associated with changing the attempt time, but the biggest
difference we see in this range is a shift that changes the temperature at which the energy
barrier goes to zero. For an attempt time of τ0 = 1 ns, a linear fit to the data shows the
energy barrier goes to zero at about 135 K. For τ0 = 250 ps, the energy barrier would go
to zero at 120 K, for τ0 = 100 ps at 100 K, and for τ0 = 10 ps at 80 K. We are unable
to experimentally determine where the barrier goes to zero in this experiment because we
cannot resolve a time constant smaller than the thermal time constant for recovery.
