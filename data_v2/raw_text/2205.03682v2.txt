Abstract

A quantum spin liquid (QSL) would be an exotic stage for superconductivity.
A promising

candidate for a doped QSL is the organic triangular-lattice system, κ-(BEDT-TTF)4Hg2.89Br8,

which hosts a non-Fermi liquid and magnetism of a QSL nature and shows superconductivity

upon cooling.
Here, we report that its superfluid density is anomalously reduced, indicat-

ing the existence of substantial incoherent spectral weight and weak superconducting phase

stiffness.
Moreover, the ratio of the superconducting transition temperature to the nominal

Fermi temperature is as large as 0.1, orders of magnitude beyond typical BCS values.
These

observations in a system free from competing orders that complicate the similar issue in un-

derdoped cuprates give a clue to the enigmatic missing superfluid density in doped Mott insulators.

The superfluid density, a spectral weight contributing to superconducting condensates,

determines the fundamental nature of superconductivity, such as phase stiffness [1], and

thus closely pertains to phase fluctuations. A quantum spin liquid (QSL) is a nontrivial

magnetic state free from order and possibly bearing fractionalization, entanglement and/or

chirality [2–4]. The superconductivity that emerges by doping such an anomalous magnetic

state [5–9] is of keen interest with regard to the unconventional pairing nature out of a

BCS framework. In the absence of adequate model materials that address this question, the

layered organic conductor with a triangular lattice, κ-(BEDT-TTF)4Hg2.89Br8 (κ-HgBr) [10],

has recently arisen as a promising doped QSL candidate. The present work experimentally

investigates the nature of superconductivity that emerges in this system in terms of the

superfluid density, which is a key parameter to elucidate the possible unconventionality of

pairing.

κ-HgBr has a quasi-two-dimensional structure with insulating and conducting layers al-

ternately stacked, and both layers have monoclinic sublattices [12, 13]. In the conducting

layers, the ET molecules form dimers, which constitute a quasi-triangular lattice character-

ized by two kinds of transfer integrals, t and t′, between the adjacent dimer molecular orbitals

(Fig. 1(a)). According to the molecular orbital calculations, the ratio, t′/t, in κ-HgBr is 1.02

[11], which means a nearly right triangular lattice. Although most κ-(ET)2X compounds

have half-filled bands, or equivalently, one hole carrier per dimer because of the monovalent

anion X−1, the band filling in κ-HgBr deviates from half by 0.055 assuming Hg+2 and Br−1

2


because of the nonstoichiometric composition of Hg, 2.89; if it is 3.0, the band filling will

be half. The compositional ratio of 4.0 : 2.89 : 8.0 that results from the incommensurate

sublattice of Hg against ET and Br is precisely determined by x-ray diffraction [12, 13] and

cannot be varied. The band-filling deviation from half corresponds to 11% hole doping to

the half-filled band and is consistent with Raman spectroscopy [14].

Several experiments indicate that κ-HgBr is a hole-doped version of the QSL candidate,

κ-(BEDT-TTF)2Cu2(CN)3, hosting a similar triangular lattice and comparable or larger

electron correlation [11]. The resistivity and Hall coefficient [15] show that κ-HgBr hosts

a non-Fermi liquid with the effective carrier density largely reduced from that of the total

band electrons, suggesting that strong electron correlation prohibits double occupancy as if

mobile carriers are restricted to doped holes. Interestingly, the spin susceptibility of κ-HgBr

is nearly perfectly scaled to that of κ-(BEDT-TTF)2Cu2(CN)3. κ-HgBr is thus regarded as a

hole-doped version of κ-(BEDT-TTF)2Cu2(CN)3. Notably, superconductivity that appears

at low temperatures is suggested to be of an unconventional nature out of the BCS picture

in that the Cooper pairs are barely overlapping, saliently robust to the magnetic field and

preformed prior to their condensate [16, 17], reminiscent of BEC-like pairing (Fig. 1(b)).

The superfluid density ns of such unconventional condensates is of profound interest. The

present work investigates ns in this doped spin liquid candidate.

We evaluated the ns value by measuring the in-plane magnetic penetration depth, λ∥,

whose inverse square is related to ns as 1/λ2
∥ = µ0nse2/m∗ (µ0: magnetic permeability, ns:

superfluid density, e: elementary charge, m∗: effective mass). Among several experimental

ways to estimate the λ∥ value, we employed a method to evaluate it from the magnetization

curve, as decribed later. The magnetization was measured with Quantum Design SQUID

magnetometers (MPMS-XL and MPMS-5S), where a crystal was fixed to a plastic-straw

sample holder with a Kapton film.
The net sample magnetization was obtained as the

difference between the magnetizations with and without the sample. The sample used in

the present work is a rhombic single crystal with diagonal lengths of ∼2 and ∼2.3 mm, a

thickness of ∼0.5 mm and a weight of ∼3 mg. To date, the difficulty in obtaining crystals

showing bulk superconductivity at ambient pressure has prohibited experimental access

at ambient pressure [15]. The present work at ambient pressure was, nevertheless, made

possible by confirming the bulk superconductivity in the used crystal in terms of the ac

susceptibility (Fig. 1(c)). Spin susceptibility in the normal state was also confirmed to

λ∥ can be deduced from the field dependence of the magnetization, M(H), in the reversible

field region under a magnetic field applied perpendicular to the layers. The M(H) for strong

type II superconductors with κ = λ∥/ξ∥ ≫ 1 (ξ∥; the in-plane superconducting coherence

length) is expressed by

− 4πM = (αφ0/8πλ2
∥) ln(βHc2⊥/H)
(1)

for a field range of Hc1⊥ ≪ Hirr < H ≪ Hc2⊥ (Hirr: irreversible magnetic field above

which the magnetization is reversible, Hc2⊥: perpendicular upper critical field, φ0: flux

quantum, α: core contribution factor, β: a constant of the order of unity) [18]. In this

field range, vortices are free from collective pinning, and magnetization is reversible against

field sweeps. According to Eq. (1), dM/d(ln H) gives an estimate of λ∥. As the correction

factor α from the London model, which does not consider the vortex core contribution, we

employed α = 0.77, a value obtained in Ref. [19]. The M(H) curves at fixed temperatures

were measured under upward and downward field sweeps in the range of 0-5 T. Fig. 2(a)

and 2(b) shows the M(H) curve of κ-HgBr at 1.8 K along with that of the half-filled system

κ-Br at 5 K (the same reduced temperature, T/Tc ∼0.42) for comparison. κ-HgBr exhibits a

peak in M(H) at Hp = 30 Oe and reversible behavior above Hirr = 300 Oe; both the Hp and

Hirr values are approximately an order of magnitude below those in κ-Br [20], suggesting

that the superconductivity in κ-HgBr is highly resilient to magnetic fields. Accordingly,

the temperature dependence of Hirr shows that the vortex liquid phase is largely extended

in the H − T plane in κ-HgBr in comparison with κ-Br (Fig. 2(c)). The extended vortex

liquid state in superconductivity with BEC-like character is theoretically suggested [21].

Magnetization curves above Hirr at several temperatures are shown in Fig. 3(a). The gray

lines indicate the fits of Eq. (1), and their slopes correspond to (αφ0/8π)[1/λ∥(T)2]. The

fit also yields the values of βHc2⊥, which is approximately 50 T at 2 K. Conventionally,

the β value ranges from 1-2, e.g., 1.44 in the case of α = 0.77 [19]. Hc2⊥ of κ-HgBr at low

temperatures is reported to be ∼15 T by torque measurements [17] and largely exceeds 9

T (the highest field studied) by the Nernst effect, which is susceptible to vortex liquidity

[16], suggesting an extraordinarily large Hc2⊥ for Tc = 4.2 K. Nevertheless, the β value

in κ-HgBr likely exceeds the conventional range; probably, β = 3-4.

an argument on possible modifications of Eq.
(1) [22], considering that the β in κ-Br

The temperature

dependence of 1/λ∥(T)2 is shown in Fig. 3(b). The 1/λ∥(T)2 data are fitted by the formula

1/λ∥(T)2 = 1/λ∥(0)2 × (1 − (T/Tc)p) with exponent p, penetration depth λ∥(0) and Tc as

fitting parameters. The gray line is the best fit with p = 2.4, λ∥(0) = 1820 nm and Tc= 4.0

K and, for reference, the dotted line indicates a fit with p fixed at the empirically adopted

value, 2, which yields λ∥(0) = 1740 nm and Tc = 4.0 K. The λ∥(0) value in κ-HgBr is three

to four times larger than those in the half-filled systems: 490-650 nm in κ-Br [20, 24, 25]

and 430-535 nm in κ-NCS [25, 26]. With these λ∥(0)) values, the relation 1/λ2
∥ = µ0nse2/m∗

yields ns/m∗ = 9.4 × 1054, 1.0(±0.3) × 1056, and 1.4(±0.3) × 1056 (m3kg)−1 in κ-HgBr, κ-Br

and κ-NCS, respectively (Fig. 4(a)). Further assuming that m∗ is proportional to the specific

heat γ, which is 55-57 mJ/K2mol for κ-HgBr [17, 27], 22-28 mJ/K2mol for κ-Br [28, 29] and

25-35 mJ/K2mol for κ-NCS [29, 30], and considering the conducting plane cross-sectional

area of the unit cell, one obtains ns = 4.9 × 1019, 2.0(±0.6) × 1020, and 3.3(±0.9) × 1020

(cm)−3 in κ-HgBr, κ-Br and κ-NCS, respectively (Fig. 4(b)). κ-Br is located very close to

the Mott transition [31], which may be responsible for the smaller ns value in κ-Br than

in κ-NCS even in the half-filled case. These values give the relative ratio of the superfluid

density as ns(κ-HgBr)/ns(κ-NCS) = 15±4% or ns(κ-HgBr)/ns(κ-Br) = 25±8%. Clearly,

the superfluid density in κ-HgBr is significantly reduced from that of the half-filled systems,

suggesting that only a fractional spectral weight approximately equivalent to the doped hole

density contributes to the superconducting condensate. This behavior is called gossamer

superconductivity, which is argued to emerge in doped Mott insulators.

The superfluid density is related to the phase stiffness, which is expressed as ℏ2d/4µ0e2λ2
∥
(d: interlayer distance) and yields approximately 4 K in κ-HgBr. This value is considerably

smaller than the values of the BCS case, typically 104-105 K [1]. As the phase stiffness

measures the macroscopic phase coherence in energy, this small value in κ-HgBr is consistent

with the previously observed field resilience and strong fluctuations in superconductivity

[16, 17]. From the penetration depth, the nominal Fermi temperature TF is evaluated through

the relation kBTF = πℏ2d/µ0e2λ2
∥ [32] to be approximately 45 K, which gives a ratio, Tc/TF,

of ∼0.09. This is a large value beyond the BCS framework, where Tc is several orders of

magnitude smaller than TF. κ-HgBr is located in the unconventional pairing region in the

Tc versus TF plane (so-called Uemura plot), as shown in Fig. 4(c) [32–35].

As seen above, the superfluid density takes a reduced value as if only the doped carri-

ers condense into the superconducting state. Such behavior is reminiscent of underdoped

cuprates, in which the normal-state carrier density [36], the Drude weight [37] and the super-

fluid density [32, 38] are discussed to be governed by the doped carriers. Theoretically, such a

significant reduction in the superfluid density is suggested to occur when double occupancies

of particles are strongly prohibited [6, 39, 40]. Previous transport studies on κ-HgBr at low

pressures revealed that the mobile carriers are in a quantum critical state with a spin-liquid

background [15, 16, 41, 42], which may bring about an incoherent part, preventing the entire

spectral weight from contributing to the conduction and/or superconducting condensate. It

is argued for underdoped cuprates that some competing or hidden orders are responsible for

the reduction in the superfluid density [43] because various orders or anomalies appear in

the spin and charge degrees of freedom competing with superconductivity [44, 45]. In this

respect, κ-HgBr is distinct from cuprates in that no such competing orders have ever been

observed, probably because a QSL free form order is the mother phase of the supercon-

ductivity. Although in recent years the possibility of competing orders in doped triangular

lattices has theoretically suggested in certain parameter spaces [46, 47], the observation of a

considerable reduction in the superfluid density without apparent competing orders in the

present system would offer a focused target to diverse theoretical challenges with regard to

the issue of missing superfluid spectral weight.
