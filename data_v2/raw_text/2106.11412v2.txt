tapes

Abstract

When modeling superconducting devices based on REBCO tapes and working near or
above the critical current value (i.e. I > Ic), the power-law model is not always accurate.
In our previous works, we proposed the overcritical current model, based on a combi-
nation of fast pulsed current measurements and finite element analysis. The overcritical
current model was provided in the form of look-up tables and was validated experimen-
tally. We showed that the overcritical current model could better reproduce experimental
measurements than the power-law model, and that the power-law model predicts a faster
quench than the overcritical current model. In this contribution, we propose the eta-beta
model, a mathematical expression to model analytically the overcritical current model,
based on measurements performed between 77 K and 90 K in self-field conditions. The
proposed model is verified by comparing DC fault measurements with the results of nu-
merical simulations using the eta-beta model to represent the electrical resistivity of the
superconducting layer of REBCO tapes.

1
Introduction

High-temperature superconductor (HTS) materials in the form of Rare Earth Barium Copper
Oxide (REBCO) commercial tapes hold immense potential for future applications such as
hadron therapy [1], nuclear fusion [2], particle accelerators [3], superconducting fault current
limiters (SFCL) [4] and superconducting motors [5].
It is paramount to develop accurate
numerical methods for modeling their electro-magneto-thermal behavior. The non-linear E −J
relationship of the REBCO material is usually described using the well-established power-law
model [6].
One of the significant drawbacks of the power-law model is that it poorly describes the
overcritical current regime (I > Ic) of the superconducting material, and other models remain
empirical [7–12]: for instance, they use a piecewise power-law in which the overcritical current
regime is modeled as the flux creep region, but with a lower n-value [13]. Accurate knowledge of
the E − J relation can help improve the reliability of the models, and thus, the performance of
the simulated devices working in the overcritical current regime or where the electro-magneto-
thermal aspects must be studied carefully, for instance a hotspot scenario [14–16], a non-
insulated coil [17,18] or a superconducting dynamo [19].

In our previous works, we developed the overcritical current model ρOC(I, T), based on
fast pulsed current measurements and finite element analysis [20, 21]. We showed that the
overcritical current model better reproduces the experimental measurements than the power-
law model, and that the power-law model predicts a faster quench than the overcritical current
model [22]. The overcritical current model was provided in the form of a look-up table obtained
from data regularization [23,24]. The major drawback of look-up tables is that they are limited
to the interval of available data. An extrapolation (or regularization) to a wider data range is
not simple to perform.
The model proposed in this work, named eta-beta model, uses a mathematical relationship
that overcomes this problem. The mathematical expression of the overcritical current model is
based on measurements performed between 77 K and 90 K, in self-field conditions.
The paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we outline the experimental methods and
the characterization of the samples.
In Section 3, we present the mathematical expression
of the overcritical current model, we report the experimental data in the overcritical current
regime and compare them with the power-law model. Section 4 provides a discussion on the
fitting parameters for the overcritical current model and their typical values. In Section 5,
the overcritical current model is validated by comparing DC fault measurements performed
on silver-stabilized samples with the results of a 2-D electro-thermal model implemented in
COMSOL Multiphysics.

2
Experimental methods and characterized

The experimental data in the overcritical current regime were obtained by combining ultra-fast
pulsed current measurements and finite element analysis techniques developed in [20,21,25,26].
In the present work, we characterized state-of-the-art REBCO coated conductors from various
manufacturers.
We present the results for selected samples fabricated by SuperPower Inc.
[27], SuperOx [28], SuNAM [29], and Shanghai Superconductor [30]; we will refer to these
samples as SP01 and SP02 (SuperPower), SO01 and SO02 (SuperOx), SU01 (SuNAM) and
SH01 (Shanghai), respectively.
Current pulses up to I = 4 Ic and as short as 20 µs were applied without destroying the
samples. The detailed characteristics of the samples are given in Table 1.

Numerical model

the length of the tape. Figure 5 is a schematic representation of the 2-D longitudinal cross-
section of the tape. The tape consists of a REBCO layer (black), a MgO buffer layer (green)
deposited on a substrate (brown), and a surrounding layer of silver stabilizer (gray).

5.3
Comparison with numerical results

In Figure 6, we compare the results of the experimental measurements and the simulations
for sample SP02b for homogeneous and inhomogeneous critical current distributions. In fig-
ure 6 left (Pulse A), the maximum voltage (electric field) applied on the sample was 5.11 V
(55.83 V · m−1), which generated a peak current Ipeak = 212.5 A. In figure 6 right (Pulse B),
the maximum voltage (electric field) applied on the sample was 5.82 V (63.53 V · m−1), which
generated a peak current Ipeak = 219.4 A. If we consider the simulation results obtained for a
homogeneous critical current distribution (figure 6(a-b)), the measured current profile (yellow)
is overall well reproduced, especially the current peak at the beginning when using the ρηβ
model (red-dashed). The ρPWL model (blue) underestimates the initial current peak at the
beginning. Both resistivity models, however, fail around 2 ms. A discrepancy between the DC
limitation tests and the simulations was observed already in a previous work of ours [22]. The
abrupt change of slope around 1.8 ms in the homogeneous case indicates that the entire tape is
quenched and all the current flows in the stabilizer and the Hastelloy.
A possible explanation since the current regime explored in these measurements is near the
critical current of the tape (109 A) and the critical current distribution is not uniform, the tape
does not quench uniformly and different regions quench at different time.
To verify this hypothesis, we implemented an inhomogeneous critical current distribution,
as described in the previous section. For our simulations, we selected an inhomogeneity degree
of 3 %, which means Ic,nom = 109 A, Ic,av(T) = Ic,nom/(1 − 0.03) ≃ 112 A, Φav,3 % ≃ 1.03 and
σΦ,3 % = 0.01.
For both DC limitation tests, an inhomogeneous critical current distribution improves the
matching between the simulated and experimental current profile. These results confirm our
previous hypothesis about the impact of the inhomogeneity on the simulated current; an in-
homogenoeus critical current distribution improves the matching between simulations and ex-
periments at 2 ms.
Specifically, since different regions quench at different time, the overall
resistance of the tape is lower and the current is higher, which explains the smoother profile of
the simulated current.
Further remarks on the simulation results are necessary. Firstly, there are still some dis-
crepancies around 2 ms, which are probably due to the difficulty of knowing the exact degree
of inhomogeneity and the exact position of the defects in the tape. A precise knowledge of the
critical current distribution may further improve the matching between experiments and sim-
ulations. However, we verified that simply changing the degree of non-uniformity of Ic (up to
20 %) does not improve the agreement with experimental results. Secondly, the current rise at
the beginning of the simulated current pulse is too sharp with respect to the measured current.
This difference may be due to an incorrect compensation of the voltage induced in the voltage
tap during the measurements.
The simulation results, however, confirm that the eta-beta model reproduces better the
temporal profile of the measured current accurately for the two electric fields level considered,
especially the current peak around 1 ms.

Conclusions

In this paper we introduced the eta-beta ρηβ(I, T) model as an alternative to the power-law
model. The advantage of the ρηβ(I, T) model is its extended range of validity in the overcritical
current regime. Through numerical simulations, we validated the ρηβ(I, T) model with DC fault
current measurements. In [22], it was demonstrated that the power-law model predicts a faster
quench than the overcritical current model, but the latter was provided in the form of look-up
table. The ρηβ(I, T) model is analytic and compact and can be conveniently implemented in
