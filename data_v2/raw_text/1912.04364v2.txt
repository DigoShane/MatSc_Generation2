Abstract

GalbrunвАЩs equation, which is a second order partial differential equation describing the evolution
of a so-called Lagrangian displacement vector field, can be used to study acoustics in background
flows as well as perturbations of astrophysical flows. Our starting point for deriving GalbrunвАЩs
equation is linearized EulerвАЩs equations, which is a first order system of partial differential equations
that describe the evolution of the so-called Eulerian flow perturbations. Given a solution to
linearized EulerвАЩs equations, we introduce the Lagrangian displacement as the solution to a linear
first order partial differential equation, driven by the Eulerian perturbation of the fluid velocity.
Our Lagrangian displacement solves GalbrunвАЩs equation, provided it is regular enough and that
the so-called вАЬno resonanceвАЭ assumption holds. In the case that the background flow is steady and
tangential to the domain boundary, we prove existence, uniqueness, and continuous dependence
on data of solutions to an initialвАУboundary value problem for linearized EulerвАЩs equations. For
such background flows, we demonstrate that the Lagrangian displacement is well-defined, that the
initial datum of the Lagrangian displacement can be chosen in order to fulfill the вАЬno resonanceвАЭ
assumption, and derive a classical energy estimate for (sufficiently regular solutions to) GalbrunвАЩs
equation. Due to the presence of zeroth order terms of indefinite signs in the equations, the energy
estimate allows solutions that grow exponentially with time.

1
Introduction

The linearized EulerвАЩs equations constitute a standard model for propagation of sound in a
background flow. What appears to be less known is that the linearized EulerвАЩs equations can be
reduced to a vector вАЬwaveвАЭ equation in the Lagrangian displacement, that is, the displacement
of individual fluid particles. (The precise definition of the Lagrangian displacement will be given
later.) The resulting equation is often referred to as GalbrunвАЩs equation in the literature, in honor
of Henri Galbrun who first derived the equation in 1931 [21][Chapter 3]. Since that first account,
GalbrunвАЩs equation (or at least very similar equations) has been independently rediscovered and
investigated a multiple of times [19, 23, 22, 26, 30, 18, 13], with applications in acoustics and
astrophysics.
The linearized EulerвАЩs equations are derived from EulerвАЩs equations by using an Eulerian
linearization ansatz [20]. Analogously, GalbrunвАЩs equation may be derived by using a Lagrangian
linearization ansatz [20]. However, we will present a complementary derivation, for homentropic
background flows, of GalbrunвАЩs equation that does not rely on Lagrangian perturbations. One
of many possible formulations of GalbrunвАЩs equation reads [20, 5, 16] (note that the last two
references assume that ѕХ0 = ќіѕХ = 0)

Discussion

The abstract definition (10) of the Lagrangian displacement calls for adequate initial and boundary
data. In the special case when the background flow is everywhere tangential to the boundary, no
boundary datum is needed, and the initial datum can be determined so that the вАЬno resonanceвАЭ
assumption is satisfied. However, when the background flow passes through the boundary of
the domain, we do not know whether a boundary datum that guarantees fulfillment of the вАЬno
resonanceвАЭ assumption can be provided for the Lagrangian displacement.
We note that fulfillment of the вАЬno resonanceвАЭ assumption in any case requires that the initial
data of ќіѕБ and w satisfy condition (33); that is, the вАЬno resonanceвАЭ assumption imposes a restriction
on the initial datum of the Lagrangian displacement. However, at least when the flow is everywhere
tangential to the boundary, the вАЬno resonanceвАЭ assumption imposes no restriction on ќіu and ќіѕБ,
that is, it does not restrict the physical behavior of the system.
The role of the вАЬno resonanceвАЭ assumption has a striking resemblance in electrodynamics. In
vacuum, MaxwellвАЩs equations are given by

We have presented a mildly well-posed initialвАУboundary value problem for linearized EulerвАЩs

regularity issue to be challenging and leave this matter open for future investigation.
Although we have not performed any numerical experiments, the ideas behind our analysis
could be transformed into a numerical scheme for solving GalbrunвАЩs equation. The resulting
scheme would not be computationally attractive since the linearized EulerвАЩs equations need to be
solved for the Eulerian perturbations before the Lagrangian displacement can be determined from
definition (10). To make things worse, it is typically not the Lagrangian displacement but rather
the Eulerian perturbations that are the unknowns of interest. Nevertheless, it would be interesting
to compare such indirect numerical scheme to other more direct approaches for solving GalbrunвАЩs
equation or regularized formulations of GalbrunвАЩs equation.
