High-quality Tailored-edge Cleaving Using
Aberration-corrected Bessel-like Beams

Compiled May 8, 2018

We report on the usage of ultrashort laser pulses in form
of aberration-corrected Bessel-like beams for laser cut-
ting of glass with bevels. Our approach foresees to in-
cline the material’s entrance surface with respect to the
processing optics. The detailed analysis of phase distor-
tions caused by the beam transition through the tilted
glass surface allows to pre-compensate occurring aber-
rations using digital holography. We verify theoretical
considerations by means of pump-probe microscopy
and present high-quality edges in non-strengthened sil-
icate glass.

Throughout recent years ultrashort laser pulses have experi-
enced great interest in research and industry, which led to a
broad range of applications ranging from spectroscopic applica-
tions to fabrication of 3D objects [1, 2]. Especially the possibility
to interact with wide band-gap materials enables the precise
processing of, e.g. semi-conductors used in thin-film technolo-
gies, or technical glasses, commonly used for displays and safety
glasses and is of great interest for markets like consumer elec-
tronics [3, 4]. Sensitive tuning of the high intensities that trigger
the initial nonlinear interaction processes and the high temporal
resolution due to the short interaction times determines if the
interaction will lead to ablation, welding or cleaving [5, 6]. The

Generation of Bessel-like beams is often axicon-based. In our
case, a “digital” axicon [11, 12] is used and displayed by a liquid-
crystal-on-silicon-based spatial light modulator (SLM). Within
the approximation of thin elements, a real axicon with given
angle α and refractive index n can be described by a radially
symmetric, phase-only transmission Tax (r) = exp (ıβr) with lin-
ear slope value β determined by β = 2π (n − 1) α/λ [12, 13]. A
4f-setup with 20 × telescopic demagnification scales the shaped
optical field of the SLM plane to the required micrometer di-
mensions and focuses it into the glass volume [14]. Using this
imaging concept, the SLM is placed virtually into the plane of
the tilted glass surface. Usually, when light is focused deep into
a transparent material with a plane surface spherical aberra-
tions occur [15]. Bessel-like beams, on the other hand, exhibit a
natural resistance to those aberrations – a further outstanding
property and an additional reason for their frequent usage for
glass-cutting applications. At the interface between the two me-
dia, all radial field components exhibit the same absolute angle
of incidence value and propagate identical optical path lengths
until they interfere constructively to the desired elongated focus,
see geometric-optical representation of an incident Bessel beam
(profile cut) in Fig. 1(a). Considering the scenario of a tilted glass
surface as depicted in (b), the spatially varying angles of refrac-
tion determined by Snell’s law will break the radial symmetry
and, depending on the tilt angle, will result in a characteristic
interference pattern along the geometric focus zone (red line),
see also Visualization 1. This is accompanied by a tremendous
loss of peak intensity and focal depth.

To compare the experimental data with the theoretical simula-
tions, we want to discuss the relation between our simulations
and in-situ experiments. The wave-optical simulations show the
calculated intensity distribution without considering nonlinear
effects. The experimental pump-probe images on the other hand
show the effect of absorption inside the material including linear
and nonlinear effects. The primary absorption to excite electrons
from the valence band to the conduction band is nonlinear [20].
Then, complex interaction due to different absorption mech-
anisms occurs until no further energy is supplied, see [21, 22].
Furthermore, relaxation processes set in. In this context transient
defects like self-trapped excitons, non-bridging oxygen-hole cen-
ters and electron vacancies have been discussed for fused silica
[23, 24]. This becomes more complex for glasses with additional
components. The defects show a reduced ionization energy
compared to the pristine material. Hence, these regions exhibit
an increased contrast in our shadowgraph images due to early
absorption of the probe beam. Our observation at 100 ps shows
the effect resulting from the electron density in the material.
Assuming a short diffusion length, defects should appear close
to the regions, where the electron density was increased. Com-
parison of Fig. 4(a) and (d) indicates matching profiles for the
linear simulated intensities and the experimental data. Here, the
linear simulations are sufficient to describe the interaction of
the Bessel-like beams. This is important to note because other
studies with different beam profiles, e.g. Gaussian beams [25],
show a visible discrepancy between experiment and theory.
The measured data in Fig. 4(e) and (e*) show the effect of a tilt-
ing angle θ = 30 ◦, or θ′ ≈ 20 ◦ according to Snell’s law with
an assumed refractive index of n ≈ 1.5 for a wavelength of
λ = 1030 nm in Corning Gorilla® glass. The former sharp pro-
file is shortened to less than 700 µm and broadened to more than
20 µm with the result of optical depth attenuation to τmax = 0.2.
To understand this loss, the normalized cross-section insets in
Fig. 4(a) and (b) are helpful. The two insets show the x-y-plane
of the adjacent profile at two different positions (red and green
line). Whereas the insets of Fig. 4(a) remain confined inside the
material, the profile in Fig. 4(b) splits up with increasing depth,
whereby the peak intensities are weakened. Due to this attenua-
tion the threshold of the aforementioned nonlinear absorption is
barely reached, the carrier generation cascade is limited and as a
result no reliable cleaving process can be developed with such a
profile. More absorption projections for different angles of θ are
pointed out in Visualisation 3.

is a slight curvature on the corrected beam profile, but the vi-

The highest achieved chamfer angles of θ′ ≈ 20◦ presented
here are initially limited by the employed microscope objective
with given NA of 0.4 of which already up to 90% are used by
the far-field ring-distribution of the illuminated digital axicon,
cf. Fig 1(c), (d). Applying the phase correction to this axicon
grating even higher spatial frequencies are displayed by the
SLM that are no longer accepted by our microscope objective for
θ > 30◦. This issue could be compensated to a certain extend
using a smaller axicon angle α. However, in our case, the next
limitation represents the objective’s housing which collides with
the glass substrate for θ ≳ 40◦. Depending on the thickness
of the glass substrate to be processed and the available pulse
energy a different microscope objective (with adapted NA and
working distance) could lead to higher θ-values. If the tilt angle
is further increased (θ > 50◦), Fresnel losses at the surface would
require an additional amplitude correction.
To demonstrate the applicability of our approach, we integrated
the resurrected features of the corrected Bessel-like beams into a
cleaving process. The employed processing laser was changed to
a 120 W disk-laser in order to process glasses with thicknesses of
up to 2 mm (SCHOTT borofloat® 33). Here we report, to the best
of our knowledge, on the first single pass edge cleaving laser
application with θ′ = 20 ◦ inside the material, cf. Fig. 5(a),(b).
We use a spatial pulse distance of 6 µm at a processing speed
of 40 mm/s combined with a pulse train configuration (burst)
consisting of four pulses with a temporal delay of 17 ns between
each consecutive pulses, pulse duration of 1 ps and a pulse train
energy of approx. 800 µJ. Figure 5(a) shows a scanning electron
microscope (SEM) image of two cleaved glass edges and reveals
the precise and defined edge quality for all inclined edges on the
1 mm thick glass sample. One exemplary image of the surface
quality created with a laser scanning microscope and one corre-
sponding line plot is depicted in Fig. 5(c). The arithmetic mean
roughness for this process is Ra ≈ 0.8 µm and absolute vertical
height offset Rq ≈ 5 µm, which are comparable to the roughness
achieved by accelerating beams [8].
In conclusion, we presented a detailed wave-optical aberration
analysis occurring for beam transition at tilted interfaces and
diligent correction thereof made accessible via digital hologra-
phy. Experimental in-situ pump-probe diagnostics were applied
to examine the emerging aberrations and verify the efficacy of

the approach. In a final experiment we demonstrated the suc-
cessful integration of our concept into an existing glass-cleaving
system and were able to cut inclined edges for glass thicknesses
up to 2 mm with an arithmetic mean roughness of sub-micron
quality. Future concepts may include combination of different
cleaving strategies, paving the way to inner contours or more
advanced edge-profiles like chamfers and round edges.
