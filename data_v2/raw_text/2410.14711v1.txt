A problem with the conservation law observed in macroscopic quantum phenomena is
a consequence of violation of the correspondence principle.

A.V. Nikulov∗

Institute of Microelectronics Technology and High Purity Materials,

Jorge Hirsch drew attention to the contradiction of the Meissner effect with Faraday’s law and the
law of angular momentum conservation. This article draws attention to the fact that the angular
momentum can change without any force only due to quantization in both microscopic and macro-
scopic quantum phenomena. But if in the first case this change cannot exceed the Planck constant,
then in the second case this change is macroscopic due to a violation of the correspondence princi-
ple. To explain the violation of the correspondence principle, Lev Landau postulated in 1941 that
microscopic particles in superfluid helium and superconductor cannot move separately. Quantiza-
tion can change not only the angular momentum, but also the kinetic energy of a superconducting
condensate on a macroscopic amount. For this reason, the Meissner effect and other macroscopic
quantum phenomena contradict the second law of thermodynamics. The reluctance of physicists
to admit the violation of the second law of thermodynamics provoked a false understanding of the
phenomenon of superconductivity and obvious contradictions in books on superconductivity.

1.
INTRODUCTION

The theory of superconductivity, proposed in 1957
by J. Bardeen, L.N. Cooper and J.R. Schrieffer (BCS),
is considered an outstanding achievement of theoretical
physics of the 20th century. But Jorge Hirsch tries to con-
vince the community of superconductivity researchers in
his numerous articles and in the book [2] that the theory
of BCS [1] is internally contradictory and cannot describe
some macroscopic quantum phenomena observed in su-
perconductors.
Hirsch writes in the book [2] that his
point of view is considered heresy in the scientific world,
since the theory of BCS is universally considered correct.
The history of science knows many examples when what
was considered heresy eventually gained universal accep-
tance.
Hirsch’s arguments against BCS theory should
be considered critically, not ignored. This work reveals
the causes of the puzzle and contradictions that Hirsch
pointed out.

One of Hirsch’s main arguments against the BCS the-
ory is the inability of this theory to solve the puzzle [3]
of the Meissner effect [4]. Hirsch draws attention to the
fact that ”within the conventional understanding of su-
perconductivity electrons appear to change their velocity
in the absence of Lorentz forces” [5]. The conventional
theory of superconductivity cannot eliminate the obvious
contradiction of the Meissner effect with Lenz’s law and
the law of angular momentum conservation [6]. Hirsch
argues that the London-BCS theory cannot explain mag-
netic flux expulsion from both a bulk superconductor [3]
and in a superconducting wire [7].

The essence of the Meissner effect puzzle is that the
shielding superconducting currents (which by W. H. Kee-
som called persistent currents [8]) emerge in the absence

The puzzle of the conservation laws noticed by Hirsch
and the smart students in the Meissner effect is not new
in quantum mechanics.
Bohr and his co-authors ex-
pressed doubts about the conservation laws in elemen-
tary quantum processes in an article [12] published in
1924. Later, Bohr came to the conclusion that the uncer-
tainty relation and the rejection of an accurate descrip-
tion of space-time processes can eliminate the contradic-
tion between the quantum theory and the conservation
laws [13]. But the uncertainty relation can eliminate this
contradiction, unless the change in angular momentum
due to quantization exceeds Planck’s constant. While the
change in angular momentum by a macroscopic amount

Quantum phenomena
were first observed at the
atomic, microscopic level, and quantum theory was cre-
ated on the basis of the correspondence principle, accord-
ing to which quantum-theoretic conclusions converge to
classical results at the macroscopic level, at which the
quantum of action, i.e. Planck’s constant h, can be con-
sidered an infinitesimal value h → 0. A quantum theory
that does not violate the correspondence principle may
be called a microscopic quantum theory. A theory must
violate the correspondence principle in order to describe
macroscopic quantum phenomena. Such a theory may
be called a macroscopic quantum theory.
The angular momentum can change without any
known force, only by quantization in accordance with
both microscopic and macroscopic quantum theory. But
according to the microscopic quantum theory, i.e. Bohr’s
quantization and Schrodinger’s wave mechanics, this
change cannot exceed the reduced Planck constant ℏ =
h/2π due to the correspondence principle, while accord-
ing to the macroscopic quantum theory, i.e. the conven-
tional theory of superconductivity [1, 14], the change in
angular momentum due to quantization can be macro-
scopic.
This distinction is very important because of
Bohr’s remark [13] that only the uncertainty relation
saves quantum theory from contradicting conservation
laws. In this article, the difference between microscopic
and macroscopic quantum phenomena is demonstrated
by the example of the persistent current of electrons ob-
served in mesoscopic normal metal rings and the persis-
tent current of Cooper pairs observed in superconductor
rings.

2.
CORRESPONDENCE PRINCIPLE

Hirsch is one of the few superconductivity experts who
have noticed the discrepancy between the observation of
macroscopic quantum phenomena and the Bohr corre-
spondence principle. He writes about the electric cur-
rent in a superconducting ring, which persists for years
without fading, like electron orbits in atoms: ”It also has
important consequences due to what is known as ’Bohr’s
correspondence principle’. In 1913, Niels Bohr relied on
the principle that microscopic physics should smoothly
evolve into macroscopic physics as the dimensions of the
system grow in order to deduce the laws that govern the
behavior of the hydrogen atom.
Surprisingly, however,
this principle is not taken into account in the contempo-
rary science of superconductivity. It relies on microscopic
physics and disregards Bohr’s correspondence principle”
[2].
Max Jammer wrote about the correspondence princi-
ple in his book about the history of quantum mechanics:
”The conceptual foundation of the correspondence prin-
ciple was based on the assumption that quantum theory
or at least its formalism contains classical mechanics as
a limiting case.
This idea was expressed by Planck as
early as 1906, when he showed that in the limit h → 0
quantum-theoretic conclusions converge toward classical
results, or, as he said: ’The classical theory can simply
be characterized by the fact that the quantum of action
becomes infinitesimally small’” [20].
Bohr’s correspon-
dence principle, according to Max Jammer, does not dif-
fer fundamentally from Planck’s idea: ”The same result,
however, is also obtained if for constant h the frequency
ν approaches zero. This was the idea chosen by Bohr in
his establishment of the correspondence principle. For,
according to his frequency law, the convergence of ν to-
ward zero implies that the energy differences become ar-
bitrarily small or, in other words, that in the limit the
energies form almost a continuum” [20].
The discreteness of the spectrum of permitted states is
one of the main differences between quantum mechanics
and classical mechanics.
The discreteness of the spec-
trum cannot be noticeable when the energy difference
between the permitted states is much less than the ther-
mal energy per particle ∆E ≪ kBT . The correspondence
principle implies that there should be a transition from
a discrete spectrum ∆E ≫ kBT to a continuous one
∆E ≪ kBT with an increase in the size, for example, of
the orbit, since the discreteness of the spectrum is pro-
portional to the square of the Planck constant ∆E ∝ ℏ2.
An experimental example of the intermediate discrete-
ness ∆E ≈ kBT , when the magnitude of the quantum ef-
fect depends exponentially on size and temperature, will

CONTRADICTION WITH THE SECOND
LAW OF THERMODYNAMICS

We cannot doubt that the persistent current (6) ap-
pears in the ring during its transition to the supercon-
ducting state due to quantization (1), since this is not
only a result of the GL theory [14], but also an experimen-
tal fact. But how can the persistent current disappear
when the ring reverts to the normal state? Superconduc-
tivity experts, including the creators of the conventional
theory of superconductivity [1, 14], did not consider this
as a problem for many years, since it was obvious that
in the normal state the electric current is damped due to
non-zero resistance, and its kinetic energy is dissipated

into Joule heat. They stopped understanding what physi-
cists understood ninety years ago. The famous physicist
W.H. Keesom wrote in 1934, after discovery of the Meiss-
ner effect, that ”it is essential that the persistent currents
have been annihilated before the material gets resistance,
so that no Joule heat is developed” [8].
Before the discovery of the Meissner effect in 1933,
W.H. Keesom and other physicists thought that the tran-
sition of a bulk superconductor to the normal state in a
magnetic field is irreversible. A well-known expert on su-
perconductivity, D. Shoenberg, wrote in 1952: ”At that
time it was assumed that the transition in a magnetic
field is substantially irreversible, since the superconductor
was considered to be a perfect conductor (in the sense dis-
cussed in Chapter II), in which, when the superconductiv-
ity is destroyed, the surface currents associated with the
field are damped with the generation of Joule heat” [58].
The generation of Joule heat is an irreversible process
according to the second law of thermodynamics. There-
fore, if Joule heat is generated at the transition to the
normal state then the Meissner effect contradicts to the
second law of thermodynamics.
W.H. Keesom actually set the task [8] for the future
theory of superconductivity to explain how the persistent
currents can be annihilated before the material gets re-
sistance in order that this theory did not contradict the
second law of thermodynamics. The creators of the con-
ventional theory of superconductivity [1, 14] did not even
consider the Keesom task [8]. They, like most other su-
perconductivity experts, kind of forgot that Joule heating
is an irreversible thermodynamic process. For this rea-
son, for many years no one noticed that the conventional
theory of superconductivity [1, 14] contradicts the second
law of thermodynamics [59], until in 2020 Hirsch recalled
[17–19] that the generation of Joule heat is an irreversible
thermodynamic process.
This contradiction, as and the contradiction with the
law of angular momentum conservation and Faraday’s
law, is a consequence of a violation of the correspon-
dence principle: the macroscopic persistent current (6)
emerges because of the quantization (3) when the num-
ber of Cooper pairs in the ring increases from Ns = 0 to
Ns = 2πrsns ≫ 1 during its transition to a supercon-
ducting state. According to the conventional opinion of
most experts, when the density of Cooper pairs decreases
from Ns = 2πrsns ≫ 1 to Ns = 0, upon returning to the
normal state, their mechanical momentum is transferred
to the normal electrons and ”this momentum then decays
with the transport relaxation time” [60] due to the usual
scattering processes. The latter process is irreversible ac-
cording to the second law of thermodynamics, and there-
fore the first process contradict this law [59].
For many years, no one noticed that the conventional
theory of superconductivity [1, 14] contradicts the laws
of thermodynamic, since in our understanding of physics
there is not only progress, but also regression.

CONCLUSION

The contradictions between the authors of the books,
which are highlighted in the previous section, are sur-
prising.
How could it happen that for many years no
one paid attention to this contradiction? V.L. Ginzburg
wrote in 1946 the expression (17) for the change in the
free energy during the transition at H = Hc based on
the fact that ”As is known from thermodynamics, the
difference in free energies after the transition and before
it FsH − FnH is equal to the work done on the body” [69].
Unlike V.L. Ginzburg, A.A. Abrikosov stated in 1988:
”The condition of a superconducting transition is equal-
ity of free energies” [67]. How could A.A. Abrikosov not
know what V.L. Ginzburg wrote about in 1946?
It should be said that A.A. Abrikosov used a trick to
substantiate his statement. The difference in free ener-
gies after the transition and before it should be equal
to the work Asn = V µ0H2
c done on the superconductor,
according to the expression (P.3.5) dF = dF0 + HdB
in Appendix 3 to Abrikosov’s book [67]. M. Tinkham
called this free energy Helmholtz free energy [57]. A.A.
Abrikosov, based on the statement that the use of this
free energy in the case of a superconductor is inconve-
nient, used free energy G = F − V HB, which M. Tin-
kham called Gibbs free energy [57]. The Gibbs free en-
ergy is more convenient only because the change in the
value V HcB is equal to the work Asn = V µ0H2
c per-
formed when the magnetic flux density changes from
B = 0 to B = µ0Hc in the volume V of the superconduc-
tor. Therefore, it is possible to write down the equality
of free energies GnH = GsH that must be present at any
phase transition, despite of the work Asn = V µ0H2
c per-
formed at the superconducting transition.
The trick with the Gibbs free energy cannot eliminate
the work Asn = V µ0H2
c done during the superconduct-
ing transition at H = Hc. This trick can only justify the
illusion that this transition is a phase transition. The
authors [58, 66] used the Gibbs free energy to justify
the equalities (15) and (16).
This justification cannot
be correct since both the Helmholtz free energy F and
the Gibbs free energy G = F − V HB cannot increase
with the magnetic field in the superconducting state in
which the magnetic flux density is zero B = 0.
A.A.
Abrikosov is more right in saying [67] that the magnetic
field does not change the Gibbs free energy G = F−V HB

in the superconducting state and decreases the free en-
ergy in the normal state since GnH = FnH − V HB =
Fn0 +V µ0H2/2−V µ0H2 = Gn0 −V µ0H2/2. The equal-
ities (15) and (16) written in [58, 66, 68] can be deduced
only from the incorrect statement of C.J. Gorter and H.
Casimer that ”the work, done by the current in the coil,
which brings about H” is dA = −HV dM [71].
Hirsch drew attention to the Meissner effect puzzle be-
cause of the confidence that his theory of hole supercon-
ductivity could solve this puzzle [3]. But Hirsch’s theory
cannot replace the conventional theory of superconduc-
tivity [14], which explains, as a consequence of quantiza-
tion (1), not only the Meissner effect [4], the magnetic
flux quantization [42, 43] and quantum oscillation of the
persistent currents [26, 27], but also the Abrikosov state
[44]. Abrikosov vortices are singularities of the GL wave
function ΨGL = |ΨGL| exp iϕ that allow the magnetic
flux to penetrate deep into the superconductor according
to the GL expression (5), due to the fact that the change
of the phase of the GL wave function around each vortex
is not zero, but 2π, �

around n Abrikosov vortices. The magnetic flux can be
equal to Φ = nΦ0 inside a macroscopic closed path l
in accordance with (5), where n is the number of the
Abrikosov vortices inside l.
But Hirsch is right in the sense that quantization and
violation of the correspondence principle can explain the
macroscopic change in the angular momentum of mo-
bile charge carriers, but not of the crystalline lattice of
superconductor ions.
This is not the only puzzle that
seems unsolvable. Another such puzzle is discussed in
publications [47, 72]. The GL theory [14] predicts that a
potential difference with a constant component Vdc ∝ Ip
should be observed when a segment of a ring with the
persistent current (6) is switched between superconduct-
ing and normal state with a sufficiently high frequency
fsw [73]. Observations [26, 29–33, 35] of the dc voltage
Vdc(Φ/Φ0) ∝ Ip(Φ/Φ0) corroborate this prediction. But
there is unsolved puzzle in this prediction. The GL the-
ory [14] predicts that Cooper pairs in a ring segment lB
should start moving due to quantization (3), when the
GL wave function ΨGL = |ΨGL| exp iϕ closes in a remote
segment lA during its transition to the superconducting
state [47, 72]. But no theory can say how quickly the
velocity of Cooper pairs on the segment lB will become
non-zero after the transition of the segment lA and how
the time between these events may depend on the dis-
tance between the segments lA and lB.
Hirsch has drawn attention [17–19] to the internal in-
consistency of the conventional theory of superconduc-
tivity [1], because of his confidence that his theory can
solve the Keesom task [8]: to explain how the persistent
currents can be annihilated before the material gets resis-
tance. He did not take into account that this task lost its
meaning [48] after experimental evidence obtained firstly
in 1962 [38] that the persistent currents are not annihi-
lated even after the material gets resistance. Moreover
experiments [26, 29–33, 35] give evidence that the per-

sistent current Ip ∝ Vdc can flow against the dc electric
field E = −∇Vdc in one of the ring segments [30, 47].
The GL theory [14] can explain even this paradoxical
phenomenon as a consequence of the emergence of the
persistent current (6) because of quantization (5) in the
superconducting state and its disappearance in the nor-
mal state due to the dissipation of its energy into Joule
heat.
It should be emphasized that not only this explana-
tion, but also observations the persistent currents Ip ̸= 0
at non-zero resistance R > 0 [35, 37–41] and the DC
power IpVdc [30], as well as the Meissner effect [59], con-
tradict the second law of thermodynamics [48]. Physi-
cists have not noticed this contradiction for many years

because of their confidence, especially characteristic of
theorists, that they can explain all observed phenomena
without going beyond their beliefs. This confidence and
insufficiently critical attitude to successful theories leads
to the degradation of physical thinking. The Comment
[74] and the article [75] draw attention on examples of
such degradation.
