The transparent semiconductor In2O3 is a technologically important material. It combines optical
transparency in the visible frequency range and sizable electric conductivity. We present a study of
thermal conductivity of In2O3 crystals and find that around 20 K, it peaks to a value as high as 5,000
WK−1m−1, comparable to the peak thermal conductivity in silicon and exceeded only by a handful
of insulators. The amplitude of the peak drastically decreases in the presence of a type of disorder,
which does not simply correlate with the density of mobile electrons. Annealing enhances the ceiling
of the phonon mean free path. Samples with the highest thermal conductivity are those annealed in
the presence of hydrogen. Above 100 K, thermal conductivity becomes sample independent. In this
intrinsic regime, dominated by phonon-phonon scattering, the magnitude of thermal diffusivity, D,
becomes comparable to many other oxides, and its temperature dependence evolves towards T −1.
The ratio of D to the square of sound velocity yields a scattering time which obeys the expected
scaling with the Planckian time.

I.
INTRODUCTION

In2O3 is a technologically appealing wide-gap semicon-
ductor (see Ref. [1] for a review). Its main polymorph
crystallizes in a cubic bixbyite structure [2].
The cu-
bic (primitive) unit cell of this structure contains 16 (8)
molecules and 80 (40) atoms.
Introducing Sn dopants
turns this semiconducting solid to a transparent conduc-
tor known as indium tin oxide (ITO) [3]. Electrical trans-
port properties of doped In2O3 with the carrier density
in the range 1018 - 1020 cm−3 has documented a room-
temperature mobility in the range carrier of ∼ 100-200
cm2V−1s−1. The high transparency in the visible spec-
trum makes this system an established transparent con-
ducting material.
Amorphous or granular indium oxide (InOx) has been
the subject of an independent fundamental research ac-
tivity focusing on a fascinating two-dimensional super-
conductor [4]. It is a platform to study metal-anomalous
metal-superconductor transitions [5] and host to a num-
ber of remarkable low-temperature transport proper-
ties [6].
The connection between this two-dimensional
electronic system and the superconducting ground state
of crystalline doped thin films of In2O3 [7] is yet to be
clarified.
Only recently [8, 9], bulk single crystals of
In2O3 became available. The thermal conductivitives of
indium oxide thin films [10] and single crystals [11] were
previously reported at room temperature. Now, thanks
to the direct growth of single crystals from the melt [9],
it is possible to measure thermal conductivity of single
crystals with a steady-state setup down to cryogenic tem-
peratures.
In this paper, we present a study of temperature de-
pendence of thermal conductivity in single crystals of
In2O3. We find that the peak thermal conductivity in
In2O3 crystal can reach as high as 5000 WK−1m−1. In a

few other solids, such as diamond [12, 13] and sapphire
(crystalline Al2O3) [14], this peak is exceeded in mag-
nitude. Most oxides show a much more modest maxi-
mum thermal conductivity.
We find that this peak is
drastically reduced by disorder.
However, the level of
disorder is not simply set by dopant concentration and
depends on annealing conditions. Samples annealed in
the presence of hydrogen are those showing the highest
thermal conductivity. Above 100 K, thermal conductiv-
ity κ in all samples converges to a similar magnitude. In
this intrinsic regime (governed by phonon-phonon scat-
tering), the ratio of the thermal diffusivity to the square
of sound velocity respects a boundary observed recently
for all insulating solids [15, 16]. Comparison with Al2O3
indicates that, in agreement with a picture recently pro-
posed to explain this boundary, the compound with the
lighter atom has a larger thermal diffusivity driven by its
superior sound velocity [17].

We can identify two potential sources of experimental

The first is the finite size of the thermal

samples. Thermally resistant manganin wires were used
to connect thermocouples to the heat bath, and therefore,
given manganin thermal conductivity (κmanganin ≈ 1
W/Km at 10K and ≈ 20 W/Km at 300K [20]) the margin
due to heat loss is less than 3% of the sample thermal
conductivity.

III.
RESULTS

Figure 1 presents our main results. Near room temper-
ature, thermal conductivity increases with cooling and
its magnitude is the same in all crystals studied. The
room-temperature thermal conductivity of our bulk sam-
ples, κ (300 K) = 15±2 WK−1m−1, is comparable to
the value of 13.1 WK−1m−1 obtained by the laser flash
method [11] and three times larger than what was re-
ported for thin films of ITO (≈ 5 WK−1m−1) [10]. In
all samples, κ(T) shows a broad peak. The magnitude of
this κpeak is strongly sample dependent. It varies from
100 WK−1m−1 in sample 1 to 5000 WK−1m−1 in sam-
ple 6. The higher the κpeak, the lower the temperature at
which it occurs. These features point to a smooth evolu-
tion in disorder among these samples. As we see below,
however, identifying this disorder is not trivial.

Mobile electrons can also conduct heat. In general, the
measured thermal conductivity has two contributions:
κmeas = κph + κe. The latter is the electronic contri-
bution to thermal conductivity, which can be estimated
using the electric resistivity and the Wiedemann-Franz
law: κe = L0T/ρ, where L0 = 2.45 × 10−8 V2/K2 is the
Sommerfeld value. Even in the most metallic like sam-
ple, κe ≪ κmeas. Indeed, for ρ ≈ 2mΩcm, one expects
κe/T=L0/ρ ≈ 10−3 WK−2m−1. Even at room temper-
ature, this is a tiny fraction of the measured signal. We
can therefore safely conclude that κmeas ≈ κph.
The effective phonon mean free path ℓph can be esti-
mated using the kinetic equation κ = 1

3 Cphvsℓph. (Note
that such an estimation neglects the possible variation of
the mean free path among modes and frequencies.) Fig-
ure 2 shows the temperature dependence of the effective
phonon mean free path ℓph extracted from our thermal
conductivity data, the reported specific heat [21, 22]),
and the estimated sound velocity vs = 4.4 km/s [10].
The smooth evolution of ℓph(T) (Fig. 2) is reminiscent
of the way thermal conductivity of an insulator evolves
upon controlled introduction of disorder, such as NaCl
crystals with Ag colloids [23].
Surprisingly, however,
the smooth evolution of ℓph(T) is not correlated with
the electrical properties.
As an example, the sample
showing the longest ℓph (sample 6) and the one showing
the shortest (the as-grown sample, sample 1) are only
slightly different from the charge transport viewpoint.
The room-temperature carrier density in sample 6 is five
times larger than in sample 1. How can then the phonon
mean free path in sample 6 be a hundred times longer,

in spite of having a higher concentration of scattering
centers?

IV.
DISCUSSION

The behavior seen here is to be contrasted with the
case of doped semiconductors such as silicon and germa-
nium [24] or doped oxides such as SrTi1−xNbxO3 [15],
where the introduction of Nb dopants leads to a steady
enhancement in electrical conductivity (because of the
introduction of mobile electrons) and a steady decrease
in thermal conductivity (because of a rise in the number
of the scattering centers).
Our observation implies that the case of In2O3 is more
complicated and disorder length scale is not simply the
interdopant distance. In the as-grown sample sample 1,
the deviation from the intrinsic regime starts below 150
K (see Fig. 2). The typical wavelength of an acoustic
phonon at this temperature is λph (150 K)= hvs

kBT =1.4 nm.
On the other hand, in sample 2, the deviation starts at
30 K, when the phonon wavelength is λph(30 K) = 7 nm.
These numbers set the lower boundary to the size of scat-
tering centers. The most prominent candidates for play-
ing this role are clusters of oxygen vacancies which man-
ifest themselves as In particles. They are formed during
melt growth in the cooling crystal and have been iden-
tified in a recent study combining electron microscopy,
light scattering techniques, and optical absorption spec-
troscopy [25]. The concentration of these clusters, which
can be as large as several tens of nanometers [25], dimin-
ishes upon annealing. This type of extended disorder is
the most plausible candidate for scattering long wave-
length acoustic phonons in low-κ samples.
The two samples with highest κpeak were annealed in
the presence of H2 (see Table I). Hydrogen is a possi-
ble dopant and a shallow donor impurity [26]. H atoms
can replace O atoms at their site [27]. The small size of
H dopants may allow the survival of high phonon ther-
mal conductivity (which requires small disorder) despite
finite electric conductivity (which requires atomic substi-
tution). Note, however, that according to first-principles
calculations [28], hydrogen may also occupy interstitial
positions and form complexes with indium vacancies.
More extensive studies are required to pin down the ori-
gin of the link between annealing conditions and peak
thermal conductivity. Note that in the most heat con-
ducting , the phonon mean free path ℓph(T) becomes as
long as 0.3 mm below 10 K, only slightly shorter than
the sample thickness (0.5 mm), implying that phonons
are close to ballistic.
A theoretical description of the thermal conductiv-
ity can be achieved by calculating the complex phonon
spectrum of this solid and solving the Boltzmann-Peierls
transport equation [29]. In the absence of a rigorous so-
lution, there are approximate treatments. The most in-
fluential is the one introduced by Callaway [30], which in
contrast to relaxation time approximation takes into ac-

ℓph is close to the sample thickness and phonons be-

In a handful of
solids [15, 36–38], the temperature dependence of the
phonon mean free path displays a local maximum fol-
lowed by a local minimum.
This has been attributed
to a regime, first identified by Gurzhi [39], where nor-
mal scattering among phonons prevails over both bal-
listic scattering and umklapp scattering leading to the
so-called Poiseuille flow [36].
It is too early, however,
to conclude at this stage that the peak in ℓph(T) is a
Poiseuille peak. No clear Knudsen minimum is visible
down to 5 K. Moreover, the thermal conductivity of the
best sample may also be affected by extended disorder,
since the phonon wavelength at 10 K is still ”only” 30
nm long. This peak is absent in sample 5, where ℓph falls
well below the crystal thickness. Note that above 100 K,
ℓph of all samples merge and approach a T −1 behavior.
At room temperature, ℓph becomes as short as 4 nm, still
four times longer than the very long lattice parameter.

Figure 3 compares the thermal conductivity of the best
In2O3 sample (sample 6) with a selection of other in-

In all, κ peaks at an intermediate tempera-
ture. The peak observed in In2O3 (5000 WK−1m−1 at
20 K) is smaller than what is reported in diamond (30000
WK−1m−1 at 60 K) [13] and in sapphire [14] and slightly
exceeds the peak in silicon [34]. Remarkably, the crys-
tal structure of In2O3 is quite distinct from either the
diamond structure or the α-Al2O3 structure. It has two
nonequivalent sites for indium atoms [1, 2]. An indium
atom is surrounded by oxygen atoms either in an octa-
hedral coordination or in a trigonal prismatic coordina-
tion. In both cases, indium has six immediate oxygen
neighbors.
In the octahedral coordination all these O
atoms are equidistant from the In atom.
In the trig-
onal prismatic coordination, however, there is a slight
difference in distance between three pairs of O atoms.
Each oxygen atom is surrounded by one indium atom
of the first type and three indium atoms of the second
type [40]. Thus, the octahedral coordination is present in
both sapphire and indium oxide, two binary compounds
with remarkably large low-temperature thermal conduc-
tivity. We note that other insulators such as Tl2O3 [41]
crystallize in cubic bixbyite structure, but their thermal
conductivity remains unexplored. Let us also note that
in β-Ga2O3, κ peaks to 560 WK−1m−1 [42]. This is to be
contrasted with ternary oxides, such as perovskites [43],
where κpeak is in the range of 40 WK−1m−1, two orders
of magnitude smaller than the two binary oxides of Fig.
3. As seen in the figure, and discussed in more detail
below, this difference disappears at room temperature.

A recent theoretical account of the empirical bound-
ary to thermal diffusivity [15, 16, 45] was put forward
by Mousatov and Hartnoll [17], who argued that there
is a quantum-mechanical bound to the sound velocity in
solids, because the energy required to hold the crystal
together and the highest phonon energy are linked. Note
that, the classical distribution of phonons above their
Debye temperature notwithstanding, the speed of sound
in solids can be linked to ℏ, as recently demonstrated by
Trachenko et al. [46]. Mousatov and Hartnoll [17] defined
a ”melting velocity”: vM = kBTMa/ℏ (TM is the melt-
ing temperature, kB is the Boltzmann constant, and a is
the interatomic distance). In their picture, the smaller
vM/vs, the closer would be the system to the Planckian
bound to dissipation and the lower should be the τ/τP
ratio. Their examination of available data on numerous
solids (see Fig. 5, adapted from Ref. [17]) confirmed this
conjecture. As seen in the figure, most solids cluster in
the green stripe, where τ/τP is roughly proportional to
vM/vs. The list includes various families of ionic salts
as well as binary and ternary oxides (in which as we saw
above the magnitude of κpeak can differ by several or-
ders of magnitude). On the other hand, as previously
noticed [17], adamantine solids (in the blue region) do
not follow this trend. One may speculate that the three-
phonon scattering phase space [47] in these solids is too
small for the relevance of the Planckian bound.
In the Mousatov-Hartnoll plot [17], the vertical and
horizontal axes are experimentally measurable quanti-
ties and there is a visible correlation across materials.
Plots showing correlations between two structural prop-
erties across materials were reported by Grimvall and
Sj¨odin [48]. These plots are to be considered in the con-
text of the impressive success of the Lindemann criterion
for melting in spite of its shortcoming, namely, an ex-
clusive focus on the solid phase and the neglect of the
energetics of its liquid competitor [49].
We can add In2O3 to this plot. It has a melting tem-
perature of TM = 2223 K [9], an interatomic distance of
a = 0.21 nm [2], and a sound velocity of vs = 4.4 km/s
[10].
As a result, one finds vM/vs ≈ 15.
Our room-
temperature data set an upper bound to τ/τP ≤ 15. As
seen in Fig. 5, these numbers put In2O3 inside the green
stripe in company of numerous other oxides and ionic
salts. According to Ref. [32], the sound velocity is 6.4

km/s. Taking this value instead of 4.4 Km/s [10] would
change both ratios, but it would keep In2O3 inside the
green strip.
Interestingly, In2O3 and Al2O3 reside at the oppo-
site ends of the green stripe.
This is understandable.
Mousatov and Hartnoll [17] already noticed that what
puts two alkali halides (LiF and RbI) with identical crys-
tal structures (and comparable room-temperature ther-
mal conductivities) far away in this plot is the difference
in the atomic masses. Given that the atomic mass of In
(115) is about four times larger than Al (27), the same
can be said about In2O3 and Al2O3. In both cases, heav-
iness reduces the sound velocity [46] and lightness pro-
motes zero-point motion and proximity to the Planckian
boundary [17].
Note, however, that In2O3 and Al2O3
have different crystal structures.
In summary, we measured the thermal conductivity of
the transparent semiconductor In2O3 and found a peak
at 20 K whose amplitude is surpassed only by a few
solids. The peak thermal conductivity was found to be
extremely sensitive to disorder. In the intrinsic regime,
thermal diffusivity respects a boundary found in other
insulators.
