Acoustophoresis of Legionella species in water and the
influence of collective hydrodynamic focusing

Alen Pavlic,∗a Marjan Veljkovic,b Lars Fieseler,b and Jürg Duala

Legionella are gram-negative, facultative intracellular, and pathogenic bacteria that pose a risk for
human health and cause significant energy losses due to extensive preventive heating-up of water
installations. We investigate acoustically-driven motion — acoustophoresis — of several Legionella
species, Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Acanthamoeba castellanii, a common Le-
gionella host in water. All the investigated cells can be acoustically manipulated in an ultrasonic
standing wave in water, as they possess a non-zero acoustic contrast that is positive for all of the
cells, leading to the focusing into pressure nodes of the standing wave. Multi-body simulations indi-
cate that an increase in cell concentration could significantly accelerate the rate of focusing due to
hydrodynamic interactions — a phenomenon that we call collective hydrodynamic focusing (CHF).
The results form a foundation for acoustic manipulation of bacteria and could pave a path towards
acoustically-aided detection of Legionella in water.

Introduction

Legionella is a genus of gram-negative bacteria that are harmful
for human health and the causative agent of legionellosis that
can appear as legionnaires’ disease, Pontiac fever, or Pittsburgh
pneumonia.1 Due to the danger of Legionella, there are regula-
tions in place to monitor and minimize their presence in water
installations. A common approach to eradicate Legionella is peri-
odically heating-up water supplies to above 60°C. However, pe-
riodic heating requires continued energy supply.2 To save energy
and to minimize the general impact of diseases originating from
Legionella bacterial monitoring and analysis could be further im-
proved. Currently, the detection of Legionella from water samples
relies on manual collection of a rather large sample (1L), fol-
lowed by labour-intensive and time-consuming culturing (up to
10 days).3 Low concentration limits stipulated by legislature and
the associated low abundance of Legionella further complicate the
process. One way to improve the monitoring of Legionella pres-
ence could be an up-concentration of Legionella in an at-line fash-
ion, prior to the analysis, combined with the use of novel and
faster detection systems. Such a combined system could accom-
plish a sustainable and reliable real-time detection of Legionella,
leading to considerable energy savings and to the reduction of
legionellosis risk on a global scale.

The up-concentration of Legionella could be accomplished
through the means of acoustophoresis,4 which is known to be
a non-destructive method for manipulation of microscopic ob-
jects at relatively high throughputs (flow rates of > 100µLmin−1

per device5). Acoustophoresis is often used in biomedical appli-
cations to manipulate cells without affecting their viability, pro-
vided that the operating power remains below thresholds for the
onset of cavitation and for reaching the temperatures harmful
to cells.6,7 The acoustic field giving rise to acoustophoresis can
for example stem from diagnostic or therapeutic medical ultra-
sound, advanced flow cytometers that use standing waves for
higher throughput and accuracy,8 or lab-on-a-chip devices for
characterization of mechanical properties of eukaryotic cells and
organisms.9–12 However, acoustic manipulation of bacteria is lim-
ited, mainly due to the small size of bacterial cells that leads to
a small acoustic radiation force (ARF), which is the main force in
acoustophoresis and scales with the volume of a cell. The acous-
tic streaming that is generally present in acoustofluidic devices
acts on the cells through the Stokes drag, which scales with the
radius of a cell. This difference in scaling of the two competing
forces can be characterized with a critical radius, above which the
cell motion is dominated by the ARF, while the smaller cells move
with the streaming flow. For a cell-like material in water, assum-
ing a standing ultrasonic wave with the frequency f of 2MHz, the
critical radius is typically ≈ 0.7µm and scales with the frequency
as

Despite the scaling of forces hindering the acoustic manipu-
lation of bacteria like Legionella, with the equivalent sphere ra-
dius (ESR) in the range of ∼ 0.5µm,14 various methods were de-
veloped in the recent years.
For example, using larger “seed”
particles that trap bacteria supposedly with acoustic interaction
forces,15,16 using two-dimensional acoustic fields to avoid the dis-

turbance from the acoustic streaming,17 or simply scaling down
the dimensions of the acoustofluidic device and increasing the fre-
quency to boost the acoustic radiation force.18 Acoustophoresis of
bacteria was so far demonstrated on, for example, Salmonella Ty-
phimurium,19 Escherichia coli,17,20,21 Enterobacter cloacae,18 and
even Legionella pneumophila,22 but in a fixed state and with po-
tentially dominating influence of agglutination.

The fundamental behaviour of biological cells in a standing
acoustic wave was so far characterized for various eukaryotic cells
and microorganisms in terms of the acoustic contrast factor Φ that
determines the magnitude of the ARF (ARF ∝ Φ) and the loca-
tions of stable equilibria of cells in the wave at either the pres-
sure nodes (Φ > 0) or pressure antinodes (Φ < 0).9–12 To the best
of our knowledge, there are no such characterizations reported
for bacteria, apart from some studies qualitatively and indirectly
implying a positive acoustic contrast factor of several bacteria in
water for Salmonella Typhimurium,19 Escherichia coli,17,20,21 and
Enterobacter cloacae.18

We study acoustophoresis of several species of Legionella, E.
coli, and P. aeruginosa that are relevant for water safety.1,23 We
experimentally determine the acoustic contrast factor of 10 Le-
gionella species, specifically L. pneumophila, L. fraseri, L. pascullei,
L. anisa, L. cherrii, L. micdadei, L. parisiensis, L. israelensis, L. gor-
manii, L. longbeachae, and of Escherichia coli. All of the measured
species possess a positive acoustic contrast, leading to the focus-
ing into a pressure node of a standing wave. A broad variation
in the shape and size across the species, as well as within each
species, considerably affects the magnitude of the acoustic radi-
ation force, resulting in observable differences in the rate of fo-
cusing among the species. Since Legionella are known to infect
protozoa, such as Acanthamoeba species,1 and grow within, we
also measure the acoustic contrast of A. castellanii in cyst and
trophozoite states, co-cultured with L. pneumophila. A. castellanii
generally also feature a positive acoustic contrast factor and thus
focus into a pressure node. Encysted A. castellanii exhibit a sig-
nificantly higher acoustic contrast factor than the trophozoites.

To complement experimental observations, we introduce a
multi-body dynamics algorithm for investigating the role of the
experimentally-observed cell size distribution and cell concentra-
tion in the collective acoustophoresis of bacteria. The algorithm is
based on Stokesian dynamics for modeling the hydrodynamic in-
teractions,24 supplemented by the Brownian motion,25 acoustic
radiation forces26 and acoustic interaction forces.27 The simula-
tions reveal that an increase in the cell concentration accelerates
the rate of acoustic focusing due to hydrodynamic interactions.
This phenomenon, which we call collective hydrodynamic focus-
ing (CHF), does not significantly influence our measurements, but
could improve the understanding of many other studies and appli-
cations that feature an externally-forced motion of bacteria-sized
objects, such as acoustic seed-particle trapping of bacteria and
nanoparticles,15 bacteria separation using ultrasound,28 or cap-
turing of paramagnetic beads using magnetic field gradient.29

Acoustophoresis of bacteria

Transient behaviour of cells during acoustic focusing is shown in
Fig. 2 for all bacterial species that were investigated. The be-
haviour is quantified through the line width 2σ(yi), where σ(yi)
is the standard deviation of the y-positions of all the bacteria that
are within the 292µm×164µm frame under observation. The line
width is for each species averaged across 4-10 repetitions of the
experiment. The results are split into two series, shown in Fig.

2(a) and 2(b), corresponding to two separate experimental ses-
sions, at f = 3829kHz and with the average pressure amplitudes
of 210 ± 8.4kPa in series 1 and 190 ± 13.7kPa in series 2. In the
experiments of series 3 (f = 3941kHz, pa = 352 ± 19.7kPa) in
Fig. 2(c), the acoustic focusing is analyzed for samples of L. pneu-
mophila that were subjected to different conditioning protocols.

Figures 2(a) and 2(b) indicate that across the tested Legionella
species, rate of focusing varies significantly. Surprisingly, even
subspecies L. pneumophila, L. fraseri, and L. pascullei consider-
ably differ in their acoustic focusability, reaching for example
2σ(yi) = 25µm at 29s, 76s, and 16.5s, respectively. The large
spread in the observed behaviour could be due to variation in the
volume, shape, material properties, or cell concentration. Signif-
icant differences in cell morphology across the species and sub-
species, observable in Fig. 3, indicate potentially large influence
of the morphology on the rate of focusing. However, an important
observation is that all the Legionella species, as well as E. coli and
P. aeruginosa, experience observable acoustic radiation force that
drives the focusing of cells towards the pressure node, despite the
large variability in the rate of focusing.

In the environment (e.g. in water installations), Legionella can
enter a so-called viable but non-culturable (VBNC) state, in which
they are still dangerous for humans, while also very difficult to
detect using standard methods.37 In Fig. 2(c) and Fig. 4(d)-
(f), we compare the acoustophoresis among the same strain of L.
pneumophila, but exposed to various conditions that trigger VBNC
state through starvation: cells conditioned in sterilized tap water,
compared to cells conditioned in buffered yeast extract (BYE) and
transferred into the sterilized tap water shortly before the exper-
iment. Measurements show that cells conditioned in tap water
exhibit larger mean ESR than the cells conditioned in BYE. Anal-
ogously to the general relation of Φeff from Fig. 4(a)-(b), mean
Φeff decreases with the increase in the mean ESR. Prolonged con-
ditioning (2 months compared to 7 days) enhances this trend.
Overall, the conditioning influences the magnitude of Φeff, but
the cells are in all cases focusing towards the pressure node, as
indicated in Fig. 2(c).

Acoustophoresis of amoebae

Measurements of the effective acoustic contrast factor Φeff for
14 amoebae are presented in Fig. 5. The amoebae were classi-
fied into trophozoites, cysts, and undetermined (amoeba in tran-
sition from a trophozoite to a cyst), based on a visual obser-
vation of the presence of the cellulose wall that is characteris-
tic for cysts.38 The results indicate that the cysts have a higher
acoustic contrast factor (Φeff = 0.055 ± 0.018) than the tropho-
zoites (Φeff = 0.018 ± 0.003), while the undetermined are in-
between (Φeff = 0.029 ± 0.010). This is reasonable also from a
theoretical perspective, since the wall of a cyst contains cellu-
lose which is denser (ρ ≈ 1.6gcm−3)39 and less compressible
(κ ≈ 5 × 10−11 Pa−1)40 than a cell-like material (ρ ≈ 1.1gcm−3

Discussion

Bacteria are known to have a cell volume-dependent volumetric
percentage of biomass,47 which has a density larger than water
at 1.35gcm−3 for E. coli.48 This implies that the density of bac-
terial cells increases with the decrease in the volume of the cells,
which is proportional to ESR3. Since the deviation of the cell
density from the density of water increases the acoustic contrast
factor, this relation between the cell density and ESR could offer
an explanation for the observed dependency of Φeff on the ESR
in Fig. 4(a)-(b) and Fig. 4(d)-(e).

In the future, it would be important to analyze Φeff of Legionella
that are grown in amoebae, as the growth process in amoebae
has been shown to significantly influence the morphology of the
bacteria,49 which could influence the acoustic focusability of Le-
gionella.

The difference in the acoustic contrast factor of the amoebae
in different stages of the encystment cycle could be exploited to
acoustically sort the different states of amoebae. This could be
realized by combining an on-the-fly analysis of the acoustic con-
trast factor of an amoeba, similar to the device used by Wang
et al. 50, and a subsequent step that would sort the amoeba into a
state-specific outlet based on the readout of the acoustic contrast
factor. An applicable acoustic sorting device was already devel-
oped, for fluorescent activated sorting of particles.51

Literature suggests that the difference in the size of the two
states of A. castellanii should be more significant, for example,
Bowers and Korn 38 reported mean diameters of 26.5 ± 0.17µm
for trophozoites and 16.2 ± 0.13µm for cysts, but could also be
affected by the culturing process.52 The relatively small sizes of
the amoebae that were present in our experiments could stem
from the co-culturing with L. pneumophila, which are known to
destabilize and lyse the host amoebae.1 Some of the destabilized
amoebae could be lysed during the sample handling steps and
during the pumping of the sample into the BAW device; Mof-
fat and Tompkins 53 for example drew a sample through a 27-
gauge needle with an inner diameter of 210µm three times to lyse
the destabilized amoebae; for comparison, the capillaries used to
pump-in the sample into our BAW device have an inner diame-
ter of 100µm. During such processes, the larger amoebae would
experience higher shear stresses in the narrow sections of the tub-
ing and BAW device, and therefore exhibit a higher probability of
lysis, explaining the small size of amoebae in our analysis.
The theoretically demonstrated acceleration of the acoustic fo-
cusing and thus higher apparent Φeff explains some previously re-
ported experimental findings. For example, the influence of the E.
coli concentration on the feasibility of acoustically trapping them
has been observed by Hammarström et al. 15. Similar concentra-
tion dependencies of bacteria-sized objects were observed already
before,28 but no study so far, to the best of our knowledge, pro-
vided a generally-accepted explanation of the underlying mech-
anism. Our finding agrees also with a theoretical study in mag-
netophoresis, where focusing of paramagnetic particles due to a
magnetic field was found to accelerate with an increase in particle
concentration.29 We call this phenomenon that stems from the
many-body hydrodynamic interactions collective hydrodynamic
focusing (CHF). CHF could apply to many existing studies on
acoustophoresis of bacteria and bacteria-sized particles that ne-
glected multi-body effects.17,18,41–43 The phenomenon could be
significantly influenced by the presence of walls in the experimen-
tal devices, analogous to the paradoxical difference in sediment-
ing particle suspensions of low volumetric concentration that sed-
iment faster when they do not fill the whole container, which al-
lows the fluid flow to go around the cluster of particles.35 A sim-
ilar effect could be caused by local variation in cell concentration
or spatial variation of the force field. In addition, specifically to
acoustophoresis — the presence of the acoustic microstreaming
that can affect the ARF34,54 as well as the AIF55 — could con-
tribute. These, so-far unexplored influences diminish the CHF in
our system for the measurement of Φeff, which we showed exper-
imentally. However, in larger channels and with more complex
acoustic modes, past experiments15 already demonstrated the

8


CHF and its potential benefits. In the future, studying CHF for
differently-sized particles and with an extended computational
model that accounts for the walls56 and other influences could be
used to quantify the multi-body effects in past and future exper-
imental studies. Furthermore, CHF could be exploited to control
and improve methods that rely on the presence of larger func-
tionalized57 or non-functionalized15 particles to up-concentrate
bacteria.

To acoustically focus all the Legionella in an at-line system,
including the swimmers, the pressure amplitudes of ≳ 0.7MPa
would be required, assuming f = 4MHz and an appropriate fo-
cusing length within a device for a given flow rate. In the litera-
ture, pa of above 1.5MPa have been reported for similar devices
and operating conditions,58 making such an application feasible
in the near future.

Methods and theory

Devices and fabrication

The lab-on-a-chip devices, an example of which is shown in Fig. 1, were produced

through cleanroom processing and feature a three-layered structure made of silicon

and glass. A blank silicon wafer (200±10µm thickness) was first anodically bonded

to a glass wafer (500µm thickness). The channel designs with 200µm and 700µm

width of the main channel were then patterned on the silicon through photolithog-

raphy (resist: S1828/S1818, Shipley, 4000rpm; developer: AZ351B, Microchemi-

cals) and then etched with an inductively coupled plasma deep reactive ion etching

(ICP-DRIE) machine (Estrellas, Oxford instruments) through the entire depth of the

silicon ∼ 200µm.
Afterwards, a glass wafer (200µm thickness) was anodically

bonded onto the etched silicon-glass wafer. The wafer was then diced into individ-

ual 11×50mm chips with a wafer saw (DAD3221, Disco corporation). Fused silica

capillaries (164±6µm outer diameter, 100±6µm inner diameter, Molex) were in-

serted into the inlets and outlets of the chips and fixed with a two-component glue (5

Minute Epoxy, Devcon). A piezoelectric transducer (PZT) (length×width×thickness

= 25 × 1.55 × 1mm for 700µm-channel width device and 20 × 4 × 0.5mm for

200µm-channel width device, Pz26, Meggitt Ferroperm) was glued to each device

with a conductive epoxy (H20E, EPO-TEK). PZT was attached to the bottom side of

the device that was not facing the microscope lens, in order to allow for the use of

a high-magnification (100x) solid immersion lens. Copper cables (0.15mm diame-

ter) were connected to the PZT using a conductive silver paste and glued to a device

with instant glue for mechanical stability. To fix the devices under a microscope, a

chip-holder with the outer dimensions corresponding to a standard microscope glass

slide (75mm × 25mm) was designed and laser-cut from acrylic glass (PMMA).

Sample preparation

Bacterial strains used in the study: Legionella pneumophila Philadelphia-1 (DSM

7513), Legionella pneumophila subsp. fraseri (DSM 7514), Legionella pneumophila

subsp. pascullei (DSM 7515), Legionella anisa (DSM 17627), Legionella cherrii (DSM

19213), Legionella micdadei (DSM 16640), Legionella dumoffii (DSM 17625), Le-

gionella parisiensis (DSM 19216), Legionella israelensis (DSM 19235), Legionella gor-

manii (DSM 16641), Legionella longbeachae (DSM 10572), Pseudomonas aeruginosa

(PAO1), and Escherichia coli (ATCC25922).

All Legionella spp. were grown in buffered yeast extract (BYE) according to Chat-

field and Cianciotto 3 overnight at 37°C. P. aeruginosa and E. coli were grown in

lysogeny broth (LB). For acoustophoresis experiments, 1mL of overnight culture

was used. Cells were washed twice by centrifugation at 10000×g and resuspension

of the pellet in 1mL autoclaved tap water. Cell concentrations were determined by

optical density (600nm) measurements and Neubauer counting chambers (Bioswis-

stec). For starvation and conditioning of Legionella in BYE and sterile tap water, cells

were kept in the media during 5 days or two months before washing, resuspension

in sterile tap water, and use in acoustophoresis experiments.

Acanthamoeba castellanii was cultured in peptone-yeast-glucose (PYG) medium

for 3 days at 30°C and resuspended in sterile tap water for acoustophoresis experi-

ments. To induce cyst formation cultures were kept at 4°C for 7 days prior to resus-

pension. Co-cultures with L. pneumophila Philadelphia-1 were prepared according

to Jarraud et al. 59. Briefly, amoebae were resuspended in “Pages’ Amoeba Saline

Solution” (PAS) and the concentration was adjusted to 105cells/mL. The amoe-

bae were incubated at 30°C after addition of L. pneumophila Philadelphia-1 at a

multiplicity of infection ≥ 1 (≥ 105cells/mL). Acoustophoresis experiments were

performed with infected amoebae harvested from 2mL co-culture and resuspended

in 500µL of sterile tap water.

Experimental procedure

For visual observation of phenomena within the channels of the lab-on-a-chip de-

vices, we used a phase-contrast microscope (Leica) with a camera (Leica MC 120

HD) recording at 30 frames per second. To visualize and characterize the acous-

tic field inside a device, 36 polystyrene (PS) beads (white 4.97 ± 0.06µm and red

2.21 ±0.03µm, microParticles GmbH) were used. A regular syringe was connected

to the inlet capillary of a device, for supplying the particles/cells dispersed in wa-

ter, as well as for cleaning the chip in-between experiments. A function generator

(JDS-2900, JUNTEK) was used to drive the PZT, monitored through an oscilloscope

(UTD2025CL, UNI-T). Devices were additionally characterized by measuring the ad-

mittance of the PZT with an impedance analyzer (Sine Phase Z-Check 16777k).

Cleaning in-between experiments with different species was performed by a flush-

ing sequence of ≥ 0.1mL of 10% sodium hypochlorite solution, ≥ 0.05mL of 70%

ethanol solution, and ≥ 0.32mL of sterilized tap water from the same batch as the

water used for suspending the cells.

The trajectories of PS particles that were used for estimation of the acoustic pres-

sure amplitude (pa) in the channel were obtained through a particle tracking ve-

locimetry plugin TrackMate 60 in Fiji, 61 and post-processed with a custom Matlab 62

code that fits the theoretical trajectory from eq. (10) to an experimental trajectory,

using pa and wavenumber k as the fitting parameters. The trajectories of amoe-

bae were obtained the same way as those of PS particles, while an additional cell-

segmentation step was performed for bacteria, using iLastik. 63 Lastly, the acoustic

contrast factor (Φeff) of cells was determined by using a modified version of the

Matlab code that fits the theoretical trajectory to an experimental cell trajectory,

using a measured mean equivalent sphere radius (ESR) of individual species as an

input parameter and Φeff as the fitting parameter. Depending on the rate of focus-

ing, the number of frames for the analysis of trajectories of bacteria was reduced

by a factor of 5 to 15, to minimize the processing time. To minimize the influence

of multi-body interactions, only sufficiently isolated PS particles and amoebae were

considered.

The size of individual bacteria species was measured from high magnification

(1000x) phase-contrast microscopic images, by measuring the maximum and min-

imum Feret diameters in a software package Fiji, and using a methodology intro-

duced by Saccà 64 to compute the mean cell volume V , which was used to extract

the ESR, specifically,
