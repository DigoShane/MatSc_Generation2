An Optimized Self-Adaptive Thermal Radiation Turn-Down Coating with Vanadium 

Dioxide Nanowire Array 

 

Ken Araki & Richard Z. Zhang* 

Department of Mechanical Engineering, University of North Texas, Denton, 76207, USA. 

 

 

ABSTRACT: High performance metasurfaces for thermal radiative cooling applications can be 

identified using computational optimization methods. This work has identified an easy-to-fabricate 

temperature phase transition VO2 nanowire array laid atop dielectric BaF2 Fabry-Perot cavity-on-

metal with total coating thickness of 2 ┬╡m. This optimized structure has ability to self-adaptively 

switch between high reflectance at low temperature to high emissivity at high temperature in the 

broad thermal infrared spectrum. This design demonstrates exceptional turn-down figure-of-merit 

compared to previously realized configurations utilizing VO2 metasurfaces and multilayers. The 

mechanism is achieved with a sub-wavelength nanowire array effective medium that switches 

between anti-reflecting gradient coating and Fabry-Perot interference. This thin metasurface 

coating could impact self-cooling of the solar cells, batteries, and electrical devices where risk 

presents at high temperatures.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1 

Optical properties of thin films can be manipulated by introducing multilayered nano-patterns 
2 

or periodic nanoelements that interface with electromagnetic waves from the visible to infrared. 
3 

Various flavors of engineered nanoscale coatings include sub-diffraction plasmonic gratings [1-
4 

3], ultra-high reflection high contrast gratings [4-6], traditional diffraction gratings [7], and simple 
5 

multilayers such as Fabry-Perot quarter-wave layers [8-10]. Among the considered, these 
6 

nanoscale metasurfaces are potentially useful in photovoltaic device improvement and thermal 
7 

emission regulation [11-14]. Yet the key attribute to be sought after is thermochromic tuning έΑΥ the 
8 

ability to change optical properties with engineered surface temperature έΑΥ which may result in self-
9 

cooling amid temperature or environmental changes. Temperature phase change materials, such 
10 

as vanadium dioxide (VO2) [9, 15, 16], SmNiO3 [17, 18], Ti3O5 [19], and many others can tailor 
11 

the optical properties when things get heated.  
12 

 
VO2 integrated in photonic multilayers and gratings have demonstrated thermal radiative 
13 

switching [9, 15, 20] A common coating design utilizes the concept of Fabry-Perot cavity 
14 

interference to obtain low emittance when cool and high emittance at hot [9]. More complex 
15 

multilayers can be added, for the purpose of daytime radiative cooling, as absorption to solar 
16 

radiation is minimized compared to the emission in the infrared [15]. Other designs have patterned 
17 

at-wavelength size scale cylindrical gratings [21, 22]. So far, the turn-down thermal performance 
18 

of such coatings has been less than anticipated. The optimal thermochromic metastructure needs 
19 

to be designed by weighting the Planck blackbody distribution at the corresponding wavelengths 
20 

and temperatures close to the phase transition, as determined by WienέΑβs displacement law. This 
21 

work also considers the optimized structure that is easily feasible for nano/micro-fabrication, for 
22 

a future in large scale-produced flexible radiative cooling blankets. 
23 

This work proposes a high turn-down grating by computational optimization of VO2 nanowires 
24 

in a thin film. VO2 has a phase transition temperature at 340 K [14, 23, 24], where the phonon-
25 

electron structure transforms from insulating to metallic at temperatures above this transition 
26 

temperature [25]. By randomized trials of at-and sub-wavelength deposited VO2 nano/micro-
27 

features, a periodic VO2 nanowire array and a solid VO2 monolayer that sandwiches a quarter-
28 

wave thick lossless dielectric. Fabricated VO2 nanowires typically of 50-150 nm in diameter were 
29 

recently used demonstrating the breakdown on the Franz-Wiedemann law in thermoelectricity 
30 


 
 

[25]. These VO2 nanowires were fabricated through vapor transport method, a bottom-up method 
31 

[25-28], and these VO2 nanobeams can be laid and aligned in-plane, compared to the top-down 
32 

photolithography and etching processes for traditional at-wavelength gratings [24]. This bottom-
33 

up method can greatly reduce complexity in fabrication, where minimal coating thickness is 
34 

needed. 
35 

We utilize a Monte-Carlo optimization method to obtain the best nanowire array period and 
36 

width of square cross-section VO2 nanowires [29]. This method is applied to obtain the VO2 
37 

monolayer as well, which serves as a gradual index anti-reflection layer above the fully-reflecting 
38 

silver substrate. The broadband reflectance or broadband emissivity turn-down performance is 
39 

quantified with a turn-down performance figure-of-merit (FOM), and compared with other VO2 
40 

phase transition passive thermal radiation self-cooling coatings. The proposed structure has a total 
41 

thickness of about 2.5 ┬╡m, including the 500 nm silver substrate. In this design, the coating on a 
42 

flexible polymer backing sheet can be applied to alleviate the loss in efficiency for solar cells, 
43 

whose temperatures can be as high as 80┬░C in summers under highly irradiative climates, such as 
44 

Texas. The flexible coating can be applied to one or both sides of a vacuum gap between a solar 
45 

cell housing and the roof. Similarly, it can be utilized for cooling compact processor components 
46 

that gets as warm as 80 to 100┬░C. Other usages include thermal management for lithium-ion cells 
47 

casings, passive-switched spacecraft insulation blankets, and other common Earth-temperature 
48 

thermal regulation applications.

3. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
125 

3.1. Radiative Turn-Down Performance of Optimized Coating 
126 

In this work, BaF2 is chosen as the dielectric spacer and dBaF2 is quarter-wave thickness set 
127 

constant as ╬╗0 / 4nBaF2 = 1.385 ╬╝m. The center wavelength ╬╗0 is chosen as 8.2 ╬╝m, corresponding 
128 

to emissive power at 80ΎΓ░C (hot condition). The cold condition is selected to be 30ΎΓ░C, a typical 
129 

warm day or summer night in Texas. BaF2 has a low index of approximately nBaF2 = 1.5, which 
130 

contrasts highly against VO2. BaF2 is preliminarily selected as the lossless dielectric due to its 
131 

broad essentially zero imaginary component of the refractive index up to the Reststrahlen 
132 

absorption peak near 50 ΎΒφm [36]. The dielectric function of Ag is Drude-like with plasma constants 
133 

from Ref. [1], and that of BaF2 is obtained from Ref. [37]. 
134 

First, the dVO2 sub-monolayer is found to have optimal thickness at 450 nm via our 
135 

randomization trials. It is thinner than the anticipated quarter-wave thickness of ╬╗0 / 4nVO2 = 840 
136 

nm, where nVO2 = 2.38 at ΎΒυ = 8.2 ΎΒφm. The maximized FOM is quite broad over N = 104 samples, 
137 

with no random scattering of outliers. Deviations of a few tens of nanometers from this value 
138 

remains high performance. On searching for an optimal filling ratio of VO2 nanowire-to-period (ΎΒο 
139 

= w / ΎΒΝ), we narrowed it to the range between 0.4 to 0.6. Thus, a reasonable additional restriction 
140 

can be applied to the optimization method where the width of the VO2 nanowire cross-section is 
141 

equal to the nanowire height. Optimizations of rectangular nanowires did not result in additional 
142 


 
 

maximization of FOM. Square cross-section VO2 nanowires with high length-to-width aspect 
143 

ratios have been realized for nanoscale applications [23]. This round of optimizations is conducted 
144 

with the aspect ratio restrictions, resulting in the best optimized structure with parameters: ╬δ = 
145 

194 nm, b = 114 nm, w = h = 80 nm, dBaF2 = 1.385 ╬╝m, and dVO2 = 0.450 ╬╝m. This design 
146 

corresponds to the highest FOM = 1.47, meaning that the emitted thermal radiation at high 
147 

temperature exceeds that absorbed at low temperature. 
148

152

153

154

155

156

157

158

159

3.2. Performance Enhancement by Dielectric Spacer Types 
240 

Figure 7 (a) shows a sweeping of a correspondingly quarter-wave thick layer of purely real 
241 

component and wavelength-independent refractive indices from unity to Germanium (n = 4.0). 
242 

The VO2 parameters are unchanged from the previously-found optimized configuration. Here, a 
243 

higher FOM performance was found by an idealized dielectric spacer of refractive index n = 2.2. 
244 

This FOM can be as high as 1.86, slightly higher compared to the optimized structure with BaF2 
245 

of 1.47. In this regard, we learn that other broadly infrared-transparent materials with higher index 
246 

such as silicon (n = 3.4) could achieve similar turn-down performance. Other low-index materials 
247 

can have similar FOM as the BaF2 configuration, such as potassium chloride (n = 1.49) and 
248 

potassium bromide (n = 1.55), as long as the Reststrahlen band is far beyond the thermal infrared 
249 

regime (ΎΒυ > 40 ΎΒφm). Ideally, medium-index materials such as zinc selenide (n = 2.4) or zinc sulfide 
250 

(n = 2.2) can deliver FOM > 1.47. It becomes pressing to seek crystalline and interfacial 
251 

compatibility between these medium-index dielectrics with VO2. Difficulties may arise in more 
252 

common dielectric oxides with medium-index, such as silicon dioxide or titanium dioxide, due to 
253 

their Lorentz absorption in the mid- to far-infrared [42]. 
254 

In the next investigation shown in Fig. 7 (b), it was found the filling ratio of the VO2 NW array

256

257 


 
 

BaF2 shows it reached a maximum at ΎΒο = 0.4, but it is not true for the other dielectric materials.

259

262

265 

with an appropriate filling ratio to improve overall reflectance, but with consideration of realizable 
266 

nanowire sizing. Other dielectric spacers have been used in other studies of turn-down coatings, 
267 

which we will make a comparison.

4. CONCLUSION 
364 

We have proposed a self-adaptive high turn-down VO2 nanowire / BaF2 / VO2 / silver thin film 
365 

of around 2 ΎΒφm for passive thermal management applications. Our Monte-Carlo optimization 
366 

method was utilized to obtain the optimal structure parameters for VO2 components. It is compared 
367 

with Fabry-Perot multilayers using VO2, and a higher figure of merit (FOM) of 1.47 is obtained 
368 

compared to other structures (FOM < 1). Ideally, the optimal refractive index of the dielectric 
369 

spacer is n = 2.2, which is slightly higher than BaF2 (n = 1.55).  The electric field is reflected at the 
370 

interface between VO2 bottom layer and silver for lower temperature for high reflectance, but a 
371 

high value of power dissipation density (PDD) is observed within the VO2 in higher temperature, 
372 

leading to high emissivity in infrared region. The optimized structure provides cooling power of 
373 

271 W/m2 at 350 K whose power is higher compared to previously studies structures. Lastly, this 
374 


 
 

work provides the idea of computational method for optimizing the photonic structures to obtain 
375 

the desired optical properties. The obtained data may be utilized for machine learning (ML) 
376 

technique to obtain predictive FOM with complex structural geometries and exotic materials, both 
377 

dielectrics and phase transition. This structure can be extended to two-dimensional gratings to 
378 

possibly further improve its figure-of-merit (FOM) as well as daytime radiative cooling merit 
379 

(DCM) turn-down to an even higher value. This turn-down allows the device to self-reject the heat 
380 

out of the devices such as solar cells, computer systems, engines, and functions as self-adaptive 
381 

radiative cooling device.
