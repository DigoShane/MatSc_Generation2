Abstract

We perform systematic numerical simulations for
carbon nanotube (CNT) film microstrip antennas to
fabricate flexible and durable applications in terms of
various device design parameters. The selection of ap-
propriate materials for conductive films and a substrate
of the conformable and robust microstrip antennas are
crucial to increase the radiation efficiency and to reduce
the losses while maintaining the mechanical proper-
ties. CNTs have been spotlighted as a promising nano-
material, exhibiting excellent electrical and mechanical
performances as desirable features for microwave wear-
able devices. Considering the material properties of the
conductor and the substrate, we examine the possible
ranges of the CNT film conductivities, conductive film
thickness, and a dielectric constant and thickness of a
substrate.
Furthermore, we model non-uniform spa-
tial distributions of conductivity in the CNT film to
assess their impact on the antenna performance. Our
extensive studies of material constants and conductiv-
ity spatial patterns propose design guidelines for opti-
mal microstrip antennas made of CNT conductive films
operating in microwave frequencies.

1
Introduction

Microstrip or patch antennas have been actively devel-
oped and widely deployed ever since G. A. Deschamps
first demonstrated it in 1953 [1, 2]. Their versatility
is attributed to several advantageous features: simple
planar structures for scalability with antenna arrays,

mature fabrication processes, cost-effectiveness, com-
pact form factors, low weight, and straightforward in-
stallation. In particular, the explosive needs of mobile
phones and the boom of high-speed wireless commu-
nications continue to ask huge demands of innovative
microstrip antennas based on nanomaterials.
A microstrip antenna consists of a dielectric sub-
strate sandwiched by a ground plane at the bottom
and a small (traditionally, rectangular-shaped) conduc-
tive patch on the top, which radiates. When we design
microstrip antennas, we pay attention to two aspects:
(1) right materials for substrates and conductors, and
(2) geometric dimensions. First, for the antenna sub-
strate materials, a dielectric constant (ϵr) and thick-
ness (h) are studied. Several different materials have
been studied as the antenna substrates: paper, textile,
polyethylene terephthalate (PET), kapton polyimide,
polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), and nickel aluminate
[3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8]. In particular, polytetrafluorethlene
(PTFE) and FR4 have been commonly chosen for RF
and microwave antennas, and their ϵr are 2.0 - 2.3 and
3.9 - 4.7, respectively. The impact of ϵr values on the
antenna performance is huge since it affects the ra-
diation efficiency (η) in that the smaller ϵr generally
increases η [9].
On the other hand, the higher ϵr is
advantageous to make a thinner substrate [10]. Next,
thickness of the substrate (h) is another essential design
factor.
It is generally known that thicker substrates
enhance η [11], but h values are closely related to an-
tenna operation frequency and material properties. For
some materials like paper or fabric, h is not necessar-
ily thick. In recent times, there are growing demands
for flexible antennas as an alternative to the conven-
tional rigid substrates, especially in flexible, wearable,

arXiv:2206.09084v1  [physics.app-ph]  18 Jun 2022


and stretchable communication devices and electronics
[12].

Parallel to the substrate material search, a variety
of novel conductive materials, instead of the tradition-
ally used copper (Cu), have been recently tested for the
metallic patch itself such as graphene-based conductive
films [13], silver and carbon-based conductive inks [14],
and carbon nanotubes (CNTs) [15, 16, 17]. Note that
all of these materials exhibit a wide range of conductiv-
ity values, which can directly influence both structure
geometries and device figure of merits, for example, ra-
diation efficiency η or reflection loss. Numerical simu-
lations were performed to assess the effect of materials
with lower conductivity (σ) than Cu in wearable mi-
crostrip antennas [18], and the thickness variation ef-
fect of the conductive films was numerically evaluated
with polypyrrole conductive polymer [19]. While the
design of microstrip antennas with conventional con-
ductors such as Cu is straightforward and well devel-
oped [20], we have not seen quantitative and analytical
explanations to the role of the σ values in the antenna
design. It is often neglected with an ambiguous and
qualitative comment of “sufficient σ is required.”.

High conductivity is a basic and necessary trait of
the conductive material for the flexible microstrip an-
tenna.
The durability is also an essential material
property against strains or stresses from the action
of stretch or compression.
In these regards, CNT is
an appropriate material candidate because the CNT
possesses not only superb electrical attributes of high
conductivity and high mobility [21] but also outstand-
ing mechanical characteristics with high Young’s mod-
ulus [22] and tensile strength [23]. Recently, E. Amram
Bengio et al. demonstrated microstrip antennas made
of aligned single-walled carbon nanotube (SWCNT)
films [16]. An individual SWCNT is indeed a good con-
ductor with σCNT ∼ 106 − 107 S/m comparable to Cu
(σCu ∼ 5.96 × 107 S/m) [21, 24]. However, we assume
that SWCNT films for antenna devices are treated as
macroscopic conducting materials because the wave-
lengths of the propagation waves are on the order of
centi-meters (for 5 GHz) which is much longer than the
typical CNT lengths around several micron. Therefore
we rather focus on the large scale behavior by neglect-
ing the microscopic modeling in our studies. In com-
parison to an individual CNT only, the CNT films often
exhibit lower σ owing to the mixture of different SWC-
NTs and their random orientation in films. The au-
thors made both aligned and non-aligned SWCNT thin
films to make 5 GHz, 10 GHz, and 14 GHz antenna de-
vices whose sheet resistance varies between 0.3 Ω.sq−1

and 1.3 Ω.sq−1, which are related to σ. In order to see
the relations of σ and antenna performances, the au-

thors measured a voltage standing wave ratio (VSWR)
and η [16].
With respect to Cu antennas, the CNT
antennas exhibit similar η of 80-90 % over the sheet
resistance value and thinner films less than the skin
depth at 14 GHz. VSWRs for CNT devices are better
than that of Cu devices by 20 %. However, the aligned
CNT devices do not perform exceptionally better than
the non-aligned as-grown CNT film antennas. These
results indicate that σ is not the only key parameter to
determine the radiation efficiency property of the mi-
crostrip antennas although higher σ has a low ohmic
loss, consequently leading to higher η.
To explain these experimental results, we also look
into the conductor skin depth (δ) carefully with regards
to the conductor film thickness t, and the quality fac-
tor. The skin depth effect is significant in AC devices
because δ can set a minimum thickness of the conduc-
tive patch where the charge carriers can flow. δ indeed
varies as a function of σ according to the well-known
relation:

Numerical Simulations

2.1
Design Parameters of microstrip
Antennas

At a given resonance frequency, the initial design pa-
rameters of a microstrip antenna are geometric dimen-
sions of the simple planar structure and quantities of
material parameters. A main microstrip antenna de-
sign (green rectangle in Fig. 1(a)) has a physical width
Wp and a length Lp. h is the thickness of the underly-
ing substrate on which the microstrip antenna resides.
A transmission-line model [20] often determines the
values of Wp, Lp, and h at the fixed antenna frequency.
The input feed of our antenna is broken into two rect-
angular sections: one serves as a quarter-wave trans-
former (brown area in Fig. 1(a)), which is parameter-
ized by a width W0 and a length L0, whereas the other
section acts as a transmission line defined by a width
W1 and a length L1 colored in blue of Fig. 1(a). We
confirm that these altogether satisfy the necessary 50
Ω impedance matching. Fig. 1(b) illustrates our sim-
ulation setup in the COMSOL Multiphysics®, where
we place the device structure at the center of a sphere,
which sets the computation boundary for the far-field
radiation patterns.

Results & Discussion

3.1
Reference Design Examination

The reference simulation design targets a 5 GHz reso-
nance frequency (f0) with an RT/Duroid® 5880 sub-
strate (ϵr = 2.20, h = 1.575 mm), and the design pa-
rameters are specified in Table 1. A real antenna with
the same design parameters in this reference model is
fabricated, and the S11 parameters are compared in
Fig. 4. Both results from the simulation and the real
prototype device are plotted in orange and blue, re-
spectively. The difference in f0 between the simulated
model and the real device is about 2.2 % (simulation:
5.02 GHz, real device: 4.91 GHz), and the S11 shows
about 10 % difference (simulation: -18.53 dB, real de-
vice: -20.38 dB). The measurement data from the real
device are in excellent agreement with the simulation
prediction, which provides us with confidence for fur-
ther simulations to explore the aforementioned effects
that are not easily controllable in real physical devices.

Following the S11 behavior, we observe a similar
monotonic trend of η in the σ and t domains (Fig. 5(b)).
Given a fixed t, η increases as σ goes up.
If t gets
thicker, the higher η region appears.
This η trend
somewhat overlaps with the S11 behavior except for
two S11 valley regions. More efficient radiation expects
less reflection, yielding lower S11. However, the S11 pa-
rameter is not necessarily lowest at the points of high
σ and high η. When σ > 6 × 105 S/m, most of the
power is radiated over 90 % regardless of the t values.
On the other hand, the loss mechanism is manifested in
the lowest S11 points. We suspect that the differences
between S11 and η come from the loss mechanism in
the patch region at the specific σ and given t around 5
GHz. Another insightful observation in Fig. 5(a) is that
the valley line (red-dotted arrow) in the σ and t phase
spaces crosses the skin depth line near (σ = 6 × 105

S/m, t = 10 um), where η is above 80 %. This can be
interpreted as the skin depth effect is irrelevant in the
higher σ region based on the result of the η. In fact,
the efficiency in 14 GHz devices by E. Amram Bengio
et al. becomes over 80 % of η regardless of t similar to
the η of Cu antennas. On the other hand, η of the lower
σ CNT antennas at 5 GHz and 10 GHz is much lower
than the Cu η at smaller t, which can by explained by
skin depth effect [16]. The t-independence in the η in
the actual CNT antennas made by Bengio et al. is be-
yond our scopre here, requiring further studies. Hence,
in order to fabricate an antenna made of CNT films

or other nanomaterials for higher than 90 % radiation
efficiency, our overall results suggest that:

• σ = 6 × 104 S/m with t > 1,200 µm

• σ = 6 × 105 S/m with t > 50 µm

• σ > 6 × 106 S/m for all t > 5 µm.

Our simulations show that the variations of both σ
and t have a minor impact on the f0 of the antenna as
seen in Fig. 5(c)). They barely shift it from the origi-
nal design value of 5 GHz within 1.1 % error with the
average f0 and standard deviation, 4.97± 0.06 GHz in
all phase spaces excluding the low σ and thick t region
(red left bottom corner in Fig. 5(c)). Fig. 5(d) plots
the FWHM results, which are relevant for designing a
narrow-bandwidth patch antenna. Most of the config-
urations explored have fairly narrow bandwidth with
the FWHM under -10 dB to be 2 % of the f0, which is
consistent with the typical 2-5 % FWHM of the reso-
nance frequency in practical antenna designs [20]. We
conclude that both σ and t do not affect the FWHM no-
ticeably and no obvious trends of σ and\or t on FWHM
are observed.

3.4
Substrate thickness and dielectric
constant variation

The simulations of the h variation are performed with
three representative materials of different ϵr: 1.6045 for
a sheet of paper or fabric, 2.20 for a PTFE plate, and
4 for a FR4 plate. Here we have a conductive film of
σ = 6 × 107 S/m in the simulations. The h values are
set between 180 um and 1575 um to cover the thin sub-

4
Design guidelines for the CNT
microstrip antenna

Our systematic numerical studies give several impor-
tant lessons that can be applied to design a flexible
and durable antenna using a thin conductive film and
a thin substrate based on CNT films whose conductiv-
ity is not as good as that of Cu. First, we have seen
that the σ of the film plays a significant role in the
overall frequency response of the antenna, in the form
of both resistive losses and losses due to the skin depth
effect when it exceeds the thickness of the material.
Above a certain value of σ, however, the antenna per-
formance does not change significantly by t. According
to our simulations, we conclude that for a radiation ef-
ficiency of about 90%, one should aim for a minimum
σ threshold of about 6.0 × 104 S/m at t > 1200 µm.
The film thickness can be reduced to be t > 50 µm for
a conductive film of σ = 6.0 × 105 S/m. If σ is bigger
than 6.0 × 106 S/m, any value of t works.
The σ uniformity in the conductive film plays an es-
sential role to improve the η from the simulations of the
skin-depth effect. Considering the mixture of two dif-
ferent σ materials, the larger areas of the high σ mate-
rial in a given antenna patch produces higher η. In par-
ticular, higher η tends to appear for the patch designs
where high σ materials cover both vertical edges of the
patch. One more observation is that small checker pat-
tern does not significantly effect on the η, however, the
pattern shifts about 4 % on the f0 to the lower fre-
quency.
Lastly, we also examine how the thickness of the
substrate affects the antenna performance which is di-
rectly relevant to designing a thin, flexible substrate.
In general, thinner substrates are not preferable since
they tend to suffer from a low radiation efficiency trend;
however, we think that this may be compensated by ad-
justing the values of the dielectric constant. When the
substrate is thinner, undesirable effects are seen on the
S11 and η responses of the antenna, specifically in re-
ducing the power absorbed by the antenna at the first
resonance frequency.
Overall, we consider the factors discussed in Section
III to reach design guidelines of flexible microstrip an-
tennas using CNT films in the microwave frequency

domain: A suggested target σ value of the CNT con-
ductive film should be at least σ of 6.0×105 S/m with a
minimum thickness of 50 µm. Making uniform σ CNT
films is a key factor and this can be achieved by aligning
purified CNTs in films, that can be placed on a paper
or a fabric as such low-dielectric flexible substrates.

5
Conclusion

We report our methodological analysis of several cru-
cial design factors on the flexible microstrip antenna
performance via numerical simulations in the COM-
SOL Multiphysics®.
Specifically, both σ and δ of
the conductive materials directly influence on the S11
and the radiation efficiency of the antenna. Further-
more, various non-uniform spatial distributions of σ
are modeled together with the substrate parameters of
the thickness and dielectric constant. Our numerical
study allows us to design promising microstrip anten-
nas based on CNT films for flexible, lightweight, and
high-frequency microwave applications.
