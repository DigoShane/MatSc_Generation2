Fe-based superconducting thin films on metallic substrates: growth, characteristics and relevant properties Kazumasa Iida,1, a) Jens H ̈anisch,2 and Chiara Tarantini3 1)Department of Materials Physics, Nagoya University, Furo-cho, Chikusa-ku, Nagoya 464-8603, Japan 2)Institute for Technical Physics, Karlsruhe Institute of Technology, Hermann-von-Helmholtz-Platz 1, D-76344 Eggenstein-Leopoldshafen, Germany 3)Applied Superconductivity Center, National High Field Laboratory, Florida State University, Tallahassee, FL, 32310, USA (Dated: 27 December 2018) 1 arXiv:1812.10264v1 [cond-mat.supr-con] 26 Dec 2018 The discovery of Fe-based superconductors (FBS) as the second class of high-temperature superconducting transition (high–Tc) materials after the cuprates generated a significant impact on the community of fundamental and applied superconductivity research. Whenever a new class of high– Tc superconducting materials is discovered, a lot of effort is devoted to growing single crystals and epitaxial thin films for exploring basic physical quantities. Although several properties of FBS are similar to the cuprates (large upper critical fields, as a consequence short coherence lengths, and small carrier density), others are distinctly different. For instance, in FBS the symmetry of the superconducting order parameter is most likely not a d–wave but an s±–wave, depending on the stoichiometry, crystallographic system, and doping level. Additionally, the critical current densities of FBS are less sensitive to the presence of grain boundaries (GBs) than those of the high–Tc cuprates. These features are highly beneficial for the realization of cheaper conductors for high-field magnets at low temperatures. Indeed, several groups have demonstrated FBS thin films on technical metallic sub- strates and powder-in-tube processed FBS wires as proof-of-principle stud- ies for conductor applications. FBS on technical substrates also give many opportunities for studying how GB networks affect the critical current and how uniaxial strain impacts the superconducting properties. In this article, we review FBS thin films, especially on technical metallic substrates, and focus on application-relevant properties like pinning improvement by nat- ural and artificial defects as well as the transparency of grain boundaries and GB networks. The recent development of FBS thin films on techni- cal substrates and their superconducting properties are presented and the performance gap with respect to films on single crystals is discussed. a)Electronic mail: iida@mp.pse.nagoya-u.ac.jp 2 I. INTRODUCTION Since the first report of superconductivity in LaFeAs(O,F) 10 years ago by Hosono’s group,1 a lot of progress in the growth and synthesis of Fe-based su- perconducting (FBS) single crystals, bulks, wires, and thin films has been made. Now, high-quality thin films [e.g., FeSe, Fe(Se,Te), doped and undoped AEFe2As2, where AE being an alkali earth element, and LnFeAs(O,F), where Ln is a lan- thanoide element, named respectively 11, 122 and 1111] are available on a large variety of single-crystalline and technical substrates. Hence fundamental as well as application-oriented research have been remarkably developed. In this article, we review FBS thin films, especially on technical metallic substrates, and focus on application-related properties like pinning improvement by natural and artificial defects as well as the transparency of grain boundaries (GBs) and GB networks. Many excellent review articles focussing on the growth of FBS thin films on single crystals and their physical properties have already been published2–9 and the in- terested readers may also refer to them. The structure of this article is as follows: Naturally grown pinning centers are summarized, followed by artificial pinning centers. Afterwards, grain boundary experiments on Fe(Se,Te), doped BaFe2As2 (Ba-122) and NdFeAs(O,F) will be reviewed. Finally, FBS thin films on technical substrates will be described and discussed. II. In general, thin films contain a higher density of natural defects than single crystals. In low magnetic fields, Jc for thin films is higher than those for single crystals (fig. 1), whereas in high fields they appear to merge toward similar values. It is also noted that for single crystals Jc has a non-monotonic field dependence due to the fish tail effect, whereas such an effect is absent in thin films. Considering the good performance already obtained in clean thin films, improvements in the in-field behavior are achievable by the introduction of artificial pinning centers with an appropriate control of their dimensionality (1D: columnar defects and dislocations, 2D: grain boundaries and anti-phase boundaries, and 3D: particles27) and their density. In this section, the naturally grown pinning centers and artificial pinning centers in FBS are reviewed, and how these pinning centers influence the in-field Jc is discussed. A. Naturally grown pinning centers 1. Ba-Fe-O nanopillars S. Lee et al. implemented SrTiO3 as buffer layer to grow epitaxial Co-doped LaAlO3 and Si) by pulsed laser deposition (PLD, KrF excimer laser).20 The nom- 5 inal Co doping level of their PLD target was x = 0.08 in Ba(Fe1−xCox)2As2 with a small As excess to compensate the loss of As during the deposition. They con- cluded that the use of a SrTiO3 template widened the range of possible substrates for high-quality Co-doped Ba-122 thin films. They also observed the formation of a high density of Ba-Fe-O nanopillars (∼ 8 vol.%) that grew vertically from SrTiO3 to the film surface (fig. 2). As a result, the critical current density (Jc) for H ∥ c was higher than that for H ∥ ab, which differs from what is expected by the mass anisotropy. The mean distance between nanopillars was 16-17 nm, which corre- sponds to a matching field of 7–8 T.28 Indeed, the pinning force density showed a peak at around 8 T for H ∥ c. Later it was found that the oxygen content of the PLD targets strongly affects the Ba-Fe-O nanopillar density.29 Most importantly, this compound can accept a high density of Ba-Fe-O of up to 20 vol.% without compromising Tc. The detailed microstructural analysis by TEM identified the Ba-Fe-O phase as BaFeO2, which is an isostructure of tetragonal SrFeO2 (a=0.3991 nm and c=0.3474 nm).30 The authors explained that BaFeO2 has a > 0.3991 nm due to a larger ionic radius of Ba compared to Sr and is coherently strained by the Ba-122 matrix. 2. Threading dislocations c-axis-correlated defects were also reported in Fe(Se,Te) thin films fabricated on SrTiO3 (001) substrates by PLD (KrF excimer laser) in Genova. In fig. 3, a high density of such defects with 2 nm diameter, comparable to the coherence length, was observed.31 Bellingeri et al. speculated those defects were threading disloca- tions. Owing to these defects, Jc for H ∥ c was higher than for H ∥ ab at all temperatures, which is similar to the observation reported for nanopillars in Co- doped Ba-122.20 The mean defect separation was about 10 nm, corresponding to a 6 matching field of 20 T. Consequently, Jc for H ∥ c has a weaker field dependence than that for H ∥ ab up to the maximum measured field of 9 T and in the temper- ature range below 0.65Tc. The activation energy U0 for vortex motion was raised by a factor 1.5 in these samples compared to a film on LaAlO3(100) substrate, where interestingly this kind of defects was absent. The introduction of threading dislocations in a controlled manner by tuning the growth conditions was reported for P-doped Ba-122 thin films by Sato et al.32 Shown in fig. 4(a) and (b) are the cross sectional views of two P-doped Ba-122 thin films grown on MgO substrates by PLD (Nd:YAG laser with 2nd harmonic) with different growth rates. Relatively short defects which are almost parallel to the Ba-122 c-axis were observed for a growth rate of 2.2 ̊A/s and identified as threading dislocations (fig. 4(a)). On the other hand, tilted, long defects of relatively low density were seen for the film grown at 3.9 ̊A/s and identified as domain boundaries due to lateral growth (fig. 4(b)). The film grown at 2.2 ̊A/s with threading dislocations showed the highest Jc in the full angular range among the films measured at 12 K and 3 T (fig. 4(c)). 3. Fe precipitates H ̈anisch et al. investigated Co-doped Ba-122 thin films containing 45◦ rotated grains grown by PLD (KrF excimer laser).33 They fabricated Co-doped Ba-122 thin films on LSAT(001) at a relatively low deposition temperature of 675◦C in ultra-high vacuum (UHV) condition. As a result, round and c-axis elongated Fe precipitates were observed near or within the grain boundaries (fig. 5). These Fe particles, together with the threading dislocations in the GBs lead to a c-axis peak in the Jc anisotropy. For P-doped Ba-122 thin films grown by molecular beam epitaxy (MBE), Sak- agami et al. reported a maximum self-field Jc of 10 MA/cm2 (4.2 K) for Fe- 7 rich films (Fe/Ba = 2.4) on MgO(001) substrates measured by a magnetization method.34 Although no microstructural investigations have been reported, the au- thors speculated two plausible reasons: one possibility is that nanoparticles of Fe or Fe-based compounds, which were not detectable by x-ray diffraction (XRD), are dispersed in the matrix of such Fe-rich P-doped Ba-122 films and act as pining centers. The other possibility is that an Fe layer is formed at the interface with the substrate and acts as a buffer layer that improves the crystallinity. B. Artificial pinning centers 1. BaZrO3 nanoparticles in PLD-grown Co- and P-doped Ba-122 Using the same strategy for pinning enhancement of YBa2Cu3O7 films,35 BaZrO3-added BaFe2(As1−xPx)2 36 and Ba(Fe1−xCox)2As2 37 sintered pellets were prepared by two groups for use as PLD targets. The former target was ablated by a 2nd harmonic Nd:YAG laser whereas the latter was ablated by a KrF excimer laser. In P-doped Ba-122 films on MgO(001) substrate, nano-sized BaZrO3 particles with average diameter of 5–10 nm were finely distributed in the Ba-122 matrix.36 As a result, an almost isotropic Jc of 1.5 MA/cm2 at 15 K (0.6Tc) and 1 T was obtained. In Co-doped Ba-122 films on CaF2(001) substrate, short and c-axis- oriented BaZrO3 nanorods with ∼ 4 nm diameter, slightly larger than in YBCO (2∼ 3 nm),41 were formed in the Ba-122 matrix.37 The mean separation of BaZrO3 nanorods was 10–11 nm, corresponding to a matching field of 17–20 T. The opti- mized Co-doped Ba-122 with 2 mol% BaZrO3 showed an in-field Jc of 1.3 MA/cm2 at 13 T and 4.2 K. Up to 3 mol% (2.5 vol.%) of BaZrO3, the initial suppression rate of Tc for both films is around -1 K/mol% (-1.3 K/vol.%), which is high compared to common values for 8 GdBCO 38 and YBCO 39,40. The difference in Tc reduction against volume fraction is not as severe because of the smaller molar volume of the Ba-122 phase. Compared to BaFeO2 nanopillars (see section Ba-Fe-O), the reduction of Tc is significant, which may be ascribed to the different lattice mismatch. 2. An excellent review article on radiation effects in FBS has re- cently been published by Eisterer.57 Here, we will briefly summarize the available data on FBS thin films. 10 The defects created by fast neutron irradiation on Fe(Se,Te) thin films produced only a small impact on the superconducting properties (Tc, upper critical field, Hc2, irreversibility field, Hirr and Jc).47 On the other hand, low-energy proton irradiation created nanoscale compressive strain, resulting in an increase of Tc by 0.5 K.48 Additionally, cascade defects combined with nanoscale strain worked as strong vortex pinning centers, and consequently Jc increased by one order of magnitude at 12 K and by a factor of 2 at 4.2 K at fields around and above 15 T. Ozaki et al. also investigated the effect of Au irradiation on the superconducting properties of Fe(Se,Te) thin films.49 The Au-irradiated film showed a slight decrease in Tc by 0.5 K. However, in-field Jc properties were improved for all field orientations at 4.2 K. Furthermore, the Jc enhancement was nearly 70% compared to the pristine film at 10 K and 9 T. Ahmad et al. reported the effect of proton irradiation effect on the superconducting properties of FeSe1−xTex (x=0.4, 0.55) thin films.50 The reduction of superconducting properties caused by the local heating, which may accelerate the displacement of atoms and create vacancies. Sylva et al. pointed out the importance of the position where the proton is implanted: the improvement of Jc up to 50% at 12 K and 7 T was observed when protons were accumulated far from the film/substrate interface. Simultaneously, Tc and Hc2 were unaltered.51 On the other hand, superconducting properties were degraded when the proton implanted layer was close to the interface. Non-superconducting FeTe thin films were irradiated by oxygen ions.52 At the maximum dose of 1 × 1017 cm−2, the FeTe film was amorphous. The effect of 3 MeV proton irradiation on the superconducting properties of Co- doped Ba-122 was reported by Maiorov et al.53 After the irradiation (total dose of 2 × 1016 cm−2), the Tc decreased by only 1 K. Overall Jc for the irradiated film was decreased in low field. At high field and low temperatures, Jc was increased, particularly for Jc (H ∥ ab) due to a clear anisotropic contribution from the point- like defects. 11 The effect of proton irradiation on Co-doped Ba-122 thin films was also reported by Schilling et al. who investigated the disorder-induced symmetry change from s± to s + + by THz spectroscopy.54 On the assumption that the electron-electron coupling constant is positive, disorder induces a transition between s± to s + + states.58 In this report they observed such a transition: the small gap was closed at a certain amount of disorder and re-opened again by further increase of disorder. These results suggested that the superconducting order parameter in the pristine film has s± symmetry. However, no information on Hc2, Hirr and Jc was reported. Daghero et al. reported on the effect of irradiation with 250-MeV Au ions on the superconducting properties of optimally P-doped Ba-122 thin films fabricated by MBE.55 The residual resistivity at T = 0 K (ρ0) increased with dose level, whilst the decrease in Tc was only 0.8 K for a maximum dose of 7.26 × 1011 cm−2. Such a small reduction in Tc would indicate s± symmetry. The authors also considered the role of the substrate in the radiation effect: Au ion irradiation and heating in the substrate may have caused additional damage. Unlike in NdFeAs(O,F) single crystals,59 α-particle irradiation on NdFeAs(O,F) thin films did not create Kondo-like defects,56 suggesting that the type of defects induced by this type of irradiation is also strongly affected by other factors, the most likely being the initial composition. In the irradiated NdFeAs(O,F) films an increase of both Hc2 and Hirr were observed. However, only for H ∥ c a moderate improvement in Jc was found, whereas for H ∥ ab point-defects compete with intrinsic pinning, reducing Jc. III. GRAIN BOUNDARIES There are two main purposes for experiments on FBS thin films fabricated on bicrystal substrates having a single well-defined grain boundary. One is to real- ize Josephson junctions with two FBS electrodes for fundamental research and 12 assessing potential of electronics application. The other goal is to check the Jc transparency across the grain boundary, which is vital information for wire appli- cations. In the former experiments, thin films are prepared on bicrystal substrates with relatively large misorientation angle θGB to realize an SNS (superconductor– normal–superconductor) or SIS (superconductor–insulator–superconductor) junc- tion. It is worth mentioning that all bicrystal experiments on FBS reported to date have been conducted using symmetric -tilt bicrystal substrates (SrTiO3, MgO and LSAT). Hence grain boundary experiments using different types of bicrystals (i.e., valley- or roof-type -tilt and -twist) are necessary to obtain deeper knowledge of grain boundary characteristics of FBS, since inter-grain Jc (JGB c ) depends in general on the GB type, as shown for YBa2Cu3O7 deposited on various types of bicrystals.60 In general, different results are expected in case of different order parameter symmetry (e.g. s–wave or d–wave) as well as strain sensitivity and charge density. To date, for FBS there have been only a few systematic and extended bicrystal experiments on Fe(Se,Te) and Co-doped Ba-122 films. Only singular experiments on P-doped Ba-122 34 and recently on NdFeAs(O,F) 61 have been reported so far, table III. First bicrystal experiments on Fe(Se,Te) were reported by Sarnelli et al. who fabricated 150 nm thick Fe(Se,Te) films on -tilt SrTiO3 bicrystals with a misorientation angle (θGB) of 45◦ by PLD using a Nd:YAG laser with a wave length of 1024 nm.64 The authors made microbridges (2.6–20 μm wide and 20 μm long) on Fe(Se,Te) bicrystals by optical lithography and ion beam milling. To avoid possible damage during the process, the sample holder was cooled to -40◦C. The I-V characteristics at 4.2 K showed a resistively shunted junction (RSJ) behavior with a low normal- state resistance RN in the range 40 ≤ RN ≤ 270 mΩ, which is typical for SNS junc- tions. Compared to the cuprates, those values are two orders of magnitude lower. As a result, the IcRN product was 18∼33 μV, where Ic is the Josephson critical current. Later, Sarnelli et al. fabricated dc superconducting quantum interference devices (dc-SQUIDs) using 100-nm thick Fe(Se,Te) bicrystal with θGB = 24◦.65,66 The resultant device showed a clear voltage modulation under magnetic field with a maximum amplitude of 3.8 μV at 4.2 K. Si et al. fabricated 100-nm thick Fe(Se,Te) thin films on CeO2-buffered SrTiO3 bicrystal substrates (θGB=4◦, 7◦, 15◦ and 24◦) by PLD.67 Microbridges with 300 μm 14 length and 20–25 μm width were fabricated by laser cutting for transport measure- ments. The ρ-T curve of the bridge with θGB = 24◦ showed a double superconduct- ing transition at 20 K and 17.5 K with ρ=0 at around 12 K. On the other hand, the bridge with θGB = 4◦ had one transition at 20 K similar to the intra-grain bridge. Using the bridge with θGB = 24◦, the authors observed multiple Joseph- son current modulations by magnetic field. The magnitude of the Ic modulation was around 80%. The I-V characteristics at 2 K roughly followed the RSJ model with a normal state resistance of RN=28 mΩ. Such a low value of RN is due to the metallic nature of the normal state of the Fe(Se,Te) bicrystal grain boundary junction. The IcRN product of their junctions was estimated to around 22 μV at 2 K. 2. Co- and P-doped Ba-122 Katase et al. reported on Josephson junctions and dc-SQUIDs in PLD-processed Co-doped Ba-122 (Tc=21.5–22.6 K) using LSAT bicrystal substrates with θGB = 30◦.73,74 For single Josephson junctions, the authors fabricated microbridges (10 μm wide and 300 μm long) by photolithography and Ar ion milling. The I-V char- acteristics of the resultant junction at 4.2 K showed RSJ behavior. The normal state resistance RN of the junction was around 12 mΩ, resulting in a low IcRN product of ∼50 μV. For dc-SQUIDs, bridges with a smaller width of 3 μm (area of SQUID loop was 18×8 μm2) were made to reduce the critical current. The re- sultant device showed a clear voltage modulation with an amplitude of 1.4 μV at 14 K. However, the shape of V -Φ deviated from the ideal sinusoidal function. Ad- ditionally, the level of flux noise at 1 Hz was ∼ 4.3×10−4 Φ0Hz−1/2, which was one order of magnitude higher than for cuprates. From those junction experiments, the barrier layer is of metallic nature in Co-doped Ba-122. Indeed, the inter-grain JGB c showed a quadratic temperature dependence, indicative of SNS rather than 15 SIS junctions.70 Hence, for realizing practical SQUIDs it is necessary to increase the junction resistance RN by an artificial insulating barrier. Schmidt et al. also reported on PLD-grown Co-doped Ba-122 grain bound- ary junctions using SrTiO3 with θGB = 30◦.69 To suppress oxygen diffusion from SrTiO3, a 10 nm thick MgAl2O4 layer was deposited on the SrTiO3 bicrystal. Fe- buffer layer followed by Co-doped Ba-122 were deposited on this template. All layers were epitaxially grown as confirmed by XRD. In their junctions (Tc=24 K), a high excess current (Iex = 645 μA) was observed, and therefore a small IcRN product of 6.5 μV at 4.2 K was obtained. Sakagami et al. prepared P-doped Ba-122 by MBE on -tilt MgO bicrystals with θGB = 24◦.34 The I-V characteristics of their device (Tc=29.5 K, bridge width of 30 μm) at 2 K was described by a flux-flow extension of the RSJ model, which gave a normal state resistance of RN=4.4 mΩ and a Josephson critical current of Ic=8.5 mA. As a consequence, a small IcRN product of 37 μV was obtained. Such a low normal state resistance of the junction is due to the metallic nature of the grain boundary. The same group also fabricated P-doped Ba-122 thin films on LSAT bicrystals with θGB = 45◦ 75. The I-V characteristics of the resultant junction can again be fitted by a RSJ model with flux flow current. Their device had an IcRN product of 45 μV at 4.2 K. However, this value was corrected to 11 μV by taking into account the excess current. 3. Fe(Se,Te) The inter-grain Jc of Fe(Se,Te) across a θGB=45◦ grain boundary without mag- netic field was around ∼ 104 A/cm2 at 4.2 K, one order of magnitude lower than the intra-grain Jc (JGB c ).64 These results suggested that the decay of Jc with GB angle may not be as strong as for the cuprates, where at this angle the suppression is significant (4 orders of magnitude). Later, Sarnelli et al. have reported JGB c /JGrain c as a function of θGB.65 It was found that the θGB dependence of JGB c /JGrain c is al- most similar to the result reported by Si et al.67 Additionally, the level of JGB c was almost constant for θGB = 24◦ and θGB = 45◦, which will be discussed later. Si et al. found that JGB c for θGB = 4◦ and 7◦ maintained ∼ 105 A/cm2 even in the presence of large applied magnetic fields (μ0H = 10 T, H ∥ c) at 4.2 K, indicative of strong links in this θGB range.67 On the other hand, JGB c for θGB > 15◦ was significantly suppressed by a small magnetic field: JGB c for θGB = 24◦, e.g., was reduced from 4 × 104 to 7 × 103 A/cm2 even by a small magnetic field of μ0H = 0.5 mT. Based on the experimental results above, the authors concluded that the critical angle θc was around 9◦. 2. Co- and P-doped Ba-122 Already in the early stage of thin film research of pnictide superconductors, S. Lee et al. reported on the weak-link behavior of tilt GBs in Co-doped 17 Ba-122.68 They fabricated 350-nm thick films (Tc over 20 K) on SrTiO3 bicrystals (θGB = 3◦, 6◦, 9◦ and 24◦) by PLD. Low-temperature laser scanning microscopy (LTLSM) and magneto-optical imaging (MOI) on these Co-doped Ba-122 bicrystal junctions with θGB = 6◦ and 9◦ revealed the weak-link behavior also for low-angle GBs (fig. 8). As can be seen in fig. 8(a), a large dissipation across the 6◦ grain boundary was detected by LTLSM. For the 9◦ grain boundary sample, two clear roof-top shapes separated by the grain boundary are observed by MOI (fig. 8(b)). These results indicated that the critical angle θc may be below 6◦, which differs from results reported two years later by Katase et al.70 We speculate that oxygen impurities may have limited the JGB c for these Co-doped Ba-122 films on SrTiO3. In fact, it is likely that the nanorods (see subsection BaFeO3 nanopillars) tend to segregate along the GB reducing the effective cross-section and consequently Jc. Detailed microstructural investigations are necessary to identify such differences. Later Katase, Hiramatsu et al. reported a θc as large as 9◦ from their detailed bicrystal experiments on Co-doped Ba-122.70,71 Here the respective Tc of Co-doped Ba-122 were 20.7 K on MgO and 21.6 K on LSAT bicrystals. Shown in fig. 9 is the θGB dependence of inter-grain Jc measured at 4 K without magnetic field. Clearly, the inter-grain Jc remained at 1 MA/cm2 up to θGB = 9◦. Beyond this angle, the decay of inter-grain Jc sets in. Another interesting feature is that JGB c was almost constant for θGB = 30◦ and θGB = 45◦. A high inter-grain Jc over 1 MA/cm2 even at θGB = 24◦ and 4 K was reported by Sakagami et al.34 for a P-doped Ba-122 thin film grown by molecular beam epitaxy (MBE) on a MgO bicyrstal substrate. Although the number of data points is not sufficient, P-doped Ba-122 most likely also has a higher critical angle than the cuprates. It was reported that polycrystalline K-doped Ba-122 bulk samples showed a large self-field Jc of ∼ 0.1 MA/cm2.78 Microstructural analysis revealed that the grain boundaries were clean without segregation of impurities. Later, atom probe 18 tomography performed on both K- and Co-doped Ba-122 polycrystals revealed that traces of oxygen impurities were present at the GBs, in particular for the Co-doped sample (fig. 10).77 Such oxidized regions may hide the intrinsic characteristics of the grain boundary. Although the microstructures of Co-doped and K-doped Ba- 122 polycrystalline samples are almost identical, the in-field Jc performance of Co-doped Ba-122 is inferior to that of K-doped Ba-122 at 4.2 K.78 These results indicate that grain boundary properties depend on the kind of doping elements (i.e., K, Co and P) and perhaps AE in AEBa2As2 too. Therefore, grain boundary experiments on K-doped Ba- and Sr-122 are highly desirable and also important for on-going development of powder-in-tube processed wires and bulk magnets.79 3. NdFeAs(O,F) LnFeAs(O,F) (Ln = Sm and Nd) compounds have the highest Tc among the FBS. Hence, a lot of effort was devoted to producing LnFeAs(O,F) wires. However, the self-field Jc of polycrystalline SmFeAs(O,F) was only ∼ 4 × 104 A/cm2 at 4.2 K,80 which is one order of magnitude lower than for K-doped Ba-122, indicative of possible weak-link behavior. Hence bicrystal experiments on LnFeAs(O,F) have been long desired to elucidate this question. Omura et al. have recently reported on the GB angle dependence of transport properties for NdFeAs(O,F) thin films fabricated by MBE.61 For θGB = 6◦ and 12◦, the resistivity in the superconducting state was below the instrumental limi- tation(fig. 11). For θGB ≥ 24◦, in contrast a finite resistivity below Tc was observed and hence the inter-grain Jc was zero. The inter-grain Jc for θGB=6◦ was reduced by nearly 30% compared to the intra-grain Jc. Hence the critical angle for these NdFeAs(O,F) films was less than 6◦. However, the authors stated that the results did not reflect the intrinsic grain boundary properties due to the erosion of the grain boundaries by fluorine diffusion. 19 4. Summary of the misorientation dependence of inter-grain Jc Shown in fig. 12 is a summary of θGB dependence of inter-grain Jc for various FBS bicrystals. Clearly, P-doped Ba-122 showed a high inter-grain Jc of 1 MA/cm2 at θGB = 24◦ (fig. 12(a)), which is highly beneficial for wire applications. It is also noted that JGB c for Ba-122 is higher than those for YBCO beyond θGB = 24◦. Although intra-grain and inter-grain Jc are high for P-doped Ba-122, the θGB dependence of JGB c is similar to that of Co-doped Ba-122 (fig. 12(b)). For all FBS except for NdFeAs(O,F), the critical angle θc is around 9◦, which indicates that in- plane misorientation alignments of FBS grains less than 9◦ usually do not impede the critical current flow. Hence one can expect that grain boundary networks whose in-plane misorientation is less than 9◦ work as flux pinning centers. Indeed, the pinning force density analysis of P-doped Ba-122 on technical substrates revealed that the GB pinning contribution is dominant for H ∥ c,83,84 which will be discussed later. The initial slope for the Jc decrease with angle for FBS is almost similar to that of YBCO, fig. 12(b), which is different from the common belief. A distinct difference between FBS and YBCO is recognized above θGB = 24◦: for YBCO JGB c continuously decreases exponentially with θGB, whilst JGB c remains constant for FBS. This may be due to the different symmetry: d-wave for YBCO and sign reversal s-wave for FBS. Additionally, the grain boundaries for both Ba-122 and Fe(Se,Te) are of metallic nature. Another plausible reason may be 45◦ - tilt GBs in Ba-122 and Fe(Se,Te) compounds being special, low-Σ or facetted GBs with a narrower structurally and electronically disturbed region than for GBs with smaller θGB as discussed for high–Tc cuprates.85 It is reported that inter-grain Jc for YBCO is ameliorated by Ca-doping for which the depletion of the carrier density at GBs is compensated.86 Later, electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) revealed high concentrations of Ca at the tensile regions of the GBs.87 On the assumption that the same mechanism could work for 20 overdoped Ba-122, Durrell et al. fabricated Co-doped Ba-122 bicrystals with Co concentration from 8% (optimal) to 10% (overdoped).63 However, no improvement of inter-grain Jc was observed. More of such experiments should be conducted to tackle the GB problem for FBS. IV. FBS THIN FILMS ON TECHNICAL SUBSTRATES As described in the previous section, grain boundaries are less critical an issue for FBS than for cuprates, and powder-in-tube-processed wires based on 11, 122 and 1111 have been fabricated. Among them, the in-field Jc of 122 wires has al- ready reached the practical level of 105 A/cm2,99 and a 120 m long tape of K-doped Sr-122 has been realized.100 However, biaxially textured films on single-crystalline substrates and also single crystals 21 showed superior Jc-H performance to poly- crystalline samples. To date, seven groups have published results on FBS films on technical substrates as prototype coated conductor samples, as summarized in viewed. Most of these studies were done on IBAD-MgO templates (textured MgO templates are prepared on Hastelloy by ion beam assisted deposition), supplied by iBeam inc., USA and Shanghai Creative Supercond. Technol. Co. Ltd, China. Three studies on FeSe and Fe(Se,Te) used RABiTS (rolling-assisted biaxially tex- tured substrate) template for which two of them were supplied by ENEA (Italian National Agency for New Technologies, Energy and Sustainable Economic Devel- opment) and evico GmbH, Germany. The other has been fabricated by in-house R&D collaboration between Brookhaven National Laboratory and Oak Ridge Na- tional Laboratory. 21 A. FeSe and Fe(Se,Te) Despite the large lattice mismatch between Fe(Se,Te) (aFe(Se,Te) = 3.81 ̊A) and MgO (aMgO = 4.21 ̊A), epitaxial Fe(Se,Te) thin films were successfully fabricated on hastelloy tapes, on which epitaxial MgO templates were deposited by IBAD.88 The respective in-plane and out-of-plane full width at half maximum of Fe(Se,Te) were ∆φ = 4.5◦ and ∆ω = 3.5◦. Clearly, ∆φ = 4.5◦ is less than the critical angle θc for -tilt Fe(Se,Te) grain boundaries. Although a critical angle for - tilt Fe(Se,Te) grain boundary junctions has not been reported, it is expected that -tilt and -tilt GBs in Fe(Se,Te) have a similar critical angle due to the small anisotropy and likely the sign reversal s-wave type symmetry. Therefore, weak-link behavior is absent in these Fe(Se,Te) films on IBAD-MgO. The lower Tc (Tc,R=0 = 11 K) of that film compared to the film on LaAlO3 substrate (∼ 15 K) is mostly ascribed to the large lattice misfit. The Fe(Se,Te) film showed an almost isotropic Jc over 104 A/cm2 at 25 T and 4.2 K. The pinning force analysis revealed that point defect pinning governs the Jc-H properties. To further improve Jc-H properties of Fe(Se,Te) on technical substrate, Si et al. have fabricated Fe(Se,Te) thin films on CeO2-buffered RABiTS.89 The lattice pa- rameter of CeO2 is around 3.82 ̊A(∼ 5.41/ √ 2), which almost perfectly matches the in-plane lattice parameter of Fe(Se,Te). As a result, Fe(Se,Te) on RABiTS showed a high onset Tc of over 20 K with a sharp transition of 1 K width. The structural characterization by X-ray diffraction revealed an in-plane FWHM ∆φFe(Se,Te) = 6◦. Despite the larger in-plane texture spread, the Fe(Se,Te) film was capable of car- rying large current densities (fig. 13). A nearly isotropic Jc ∼ 0.1 MA/cm2 at 4.2 K and 30 T was observed. The pinning mechanism was the same as for the Fe(Se,Te) film on IBAD-MgO. The authors concluded that the use of CeO2 buffer layer is the key for achieving high Tc. Additionally, the substrates containing low angle grain boundaries up to 7◦ do not impede the critical current flow, which is different from REBa2Cu3O7. 22 Quite recently, the group of IEE-CAS reported Fe(Se,Te) thin films on IBAD- MgO with LaMnO3 (LMO) buffer layers.90 The Fe(Se,Te) was grown on LMO with cube-on-cube configuration. The respective out-of-plane and in-plane full width at half maximum values of Fe(Se,Te) are ∆ω = 3.4◦ and ∆φ = 7.8◦. The Tc of the sample is 16.8 K, which is slightly higher than that of bulk due to the compressive strain. Albeit the large ∆φ of 7.8◦, an in-field Jc above 0.35 MA/cm2 up to 9 T was recorded at 4.2 K. Additionally, the anisotropy of Jc was relatively small. The group of CNR has been recently working on an attempt to decrease the number of necessary buffer layers or exchanging the metallic substrate to less expensive materials. Both solutions could reduce the fabrication costs of such Fe(Se,Te) coated conductors.91 The films on Ni5W RABiTS covered with a single CeO2 buffer layer (produced by ENEA) showed a Tc of ∼18 K and isotropic Jc above 2 × 104 A/cm2 at 4.2 K in fields up to 18 T. On biaxially textured invar (nickel-iron alloy with 36 mass% of Ni) tape, epitaxial Fe(Se,Te) layers of the same texture quality as the tape itself (FWHM∼ 9◦) were grown without any buffer layer. The superconducting properties of these samples still have to be investigated. The group at NIMS proposed a unique technique for fabricating FeSe films on RABiTS provided by evico GmbH.92,93 They performed electrochemical deposition using three electrodes, Pt (counter electrode), Ag/AgCl (reference electrode) and RABiTS (working electrode) at 70◦C. The electrolyte was prepared from a solution of FeCl2 · 4H2O, SeO2, and Na2SO4 in distilled water. A pH value of 2.1 for the electrode was maintained during the deposition. X-ray diffraction confirmed FeSe phase formation without heat treatment. Although the resulting film had a low Tc of 2.5 K, this technique is promising for further development and optimization.93 23 B. Co- and P-doped Ba-122 The first report on Co-doped Ba-122 on technical substrates was published by Iida et al.94 They fabricated Co-doped Ba-122 films on Fe-buffered IBAD-MgO templates by PLD. Due to the relatively large in-plane mosaicity of the MgO (∆φMgO ∼ 6◦), the crystalline quality of the intermediate Fe as well as Co-doped Ba-122 layers was poor (i.e., ∆φFe ∼ 5◦ and ∆φBa−122 ∼ 5◦). Although biaxially textured Co-doped Ba-122 showed an onset Tc of 21 K, the in-field Jc at 8 K was almost one order of magnitude lower compared to the film on single crystalline MgO due to the poor crystalline quality. Later, employing a high-quality MgO template (∆φMgO ∼ 2.4◦) improved the in-field Jc of Co-doped Ba-122 to a level almost comparable to films on single crystalline substrates.95 Almost at the same time, Katase et al. also reported on the fabrication of Co- doped Ba-122 directly on IBAD-MgO.96 A sharp texture of the Co-doped Ba-122 with ∆φBa122 ∼ 3.2◦ − 3.5◦ was obtained irrespective of the in-plane mosaicity of MgO templates due to the self-epitaxy effect. The onset Tc of Co-doped Ba-122 on IBAD-MgO was 22 K. In-field Jc reached almost 0.1 MA/cm2 under 9 T at 4 K. Very recently, Co-doped Ba-122 was also fabricated on IBAD-MgO with LMO by Xu et al.97 They used SrTiO3 as buffer layer between LMO and Co-doped Ba- 122. The resultant film showed almost isotropic Jc. The respective Jc for H ∥ c and ∥ ab were 0.86 and 0.96 MA/cm2 at 9 T and 4.2 K. P-doped Ba-122 shows the second highest Tc among the 122 compounds and, therefore, Jc-H properties are usually superior to Co-doped Ba-122. Additionally, growth-related and artificial pinning centers can be introduced into the P-doped Ba-122 matrix as discussed in the previous section. Sato et al. fabricated P- doped Ba-122 thin films with PLD on IBAD-MgO with two different in-plain texture qualities, ∆φMgO = 4◦ and 8◦.83 The structural characterization by X-ray diffraction revealed ∆φBa−122 = 2.7◦ and 8◦ for P-doped Ba-122 deposited on the well and poorly textured MgO template, respectively. The film on poor crystalline 24 MgO template had an onset Tc of 23 K, which is comparable to the Co-doped Ba-122 on IBAD-MgO (fig. 14(a)). On the other hand, the P-doped Ba-122 on the well textured MgO template had a higher Tc of 26 K (fig. 14(a)). The low- Tc sample showed nonetheless better in-field Jc properties: almost isotropic Jc exceeding 0.1 MA/cm2 at 9 T and 4 K (fig. 14(b)). This can be explained by grain boundary pinning. SUMMARY AND DISCUSSION In order to understand the performance potential of FBS, we discussed the main application-related properties like pinning improvement by natural and artificial 27 defects, the transparency of grain boundaries and the properties of GB networks. Naturally grown defects (BaFeO2 in Co-doped Ba-122 and threading dislocations in Fe(Se,Te)) mainly contribute to the c-axis correlated pinning, and point pin- ning introduced by strain is beneficial as well. Artificial pinning centers can be introduced in the superconducting matrix by multilayer deposition, irradiation, and the addition of nanoparticles such as BaZrO3 to the PLD targets. Although all methods described here are effective for improving in-field properties, the Jc level at 4.2 K achieved so far is still only up to ∼6% of the depairing current density in films on single crystals, suggesting room for further improvement of superconducting properties of FBS. Bicrystal experiments on Ba-122 and Fe(Se,Te) revealed the metallic nature of grain boundaries. The critical angle above which inter-grain Jc falls off is around 9◦. However, the initial slope for the logarithmic Jc decrease with GB angle for FBS is similar to that of YBCO. A distinct difference between FBS and YBCO is recognised above θGB = 24◦: for YBCO JGB c continuously decreases with θGB, whilst JGB c remains constant for FBS. Overall, those properties are highly beneficial for making cheap FBS conductors. The three main systems with largest application potential (11, 122 and 1111) have already been realized on technical substrates. Most of the FBS coated conduc- tor prototypes showed a comparable Tc to their counterparts on single-crystalline substrates. Fe(Se,Te) and P-doped Ba-122 reached the practical level (0.1 MA/cm2 at 15 T). It is noted that PIT-processed FBS wires (K-doped Ba-122 and Sr-122) also have reached this level,8 despite the presence of many GBs. Therefore, fur- ther GB experiments on FBS, especially for K-doped 122, are desired. To further improve the in-field Jc properties of FBS on technical substrates, the introduction of artificial pinning centers without compromising Tc is necessary . Even though FBS tapes will never compete with YBCO tapes regarding their superconducting properties, they could compete with YBCO in terms of cost. For 28 YBCO tapes, expensive highly-textured metallic substrates and Ag layers to allow oxygenation are necessary. However, FBS can be realized on poorly textured templates without expensive cover layers. For realizing FBS conductors, there are other properties that need to be evaluated: i) thick FBS thin films (∼1 μm) on technical substrates should be fabricated and characterized for realizing FBS coated conductors. ii) Strain tolerance dependence of Jc should be investigated, which is important for making a magnet.
