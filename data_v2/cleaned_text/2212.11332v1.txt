On a normal metal, magnetic impurities generate the correlation-driven Kondo efect. On a superconductor, bound states emerge inside the superconducting gap called the Yu-Shiba-Rusinov (YSR) states. Teoretically, quantum impurity problems have been successfully tackled by numerical renormalization group (NRG) theory, where the Kondo and YSR physics are shown to be unifed and the normalized YSR energy scales universally with the Kondo temperature divided by the superconducting gap. However, experimentally the Kondo temperature is usually extracted from phenomenological approaches, which gives rise to signifcant uncertainties and cannot account for magnetic felds properly. Using scanning tunneling microscopy at 10 mK, we apply a magnetic feld to several YSR impurities on a vanadium tip to reveal the Kondo efect and employ the microscopic single impurity Anderson model with NRG to ft the Kondo spectra in magnetic felds accurately and extract the corresponding Kondo temperature unambiguously. Some YSR states move across the quantum phase transition (QPT) due to the changes in atomic forces during tip approach, yielding a continuous universal scaling with quantitative precision for quantum spin-1⁄2 impurities. Introduction Qantum impurity problems deal with impurities featur- ing a small number of intrinsic quantum degrees of freedom connected to a continuous bath. Te complexity lies in the quantum nature of such impurities resulting in correlations and many-body efects as well as the concomitant break- down of a perturbative or mean feld treatment . Mag- netic impurities are particularly interesting as it is not a pri- ori clear whether a fully quantum theory is necessary or a semiclassical treatment on the spin degrees of freedom suf- fces to describe the system. In some instances, the spin can be treated classically, as in the case of the in-gap Yu-Shiba- Rusinov (YSR) states generated from magnetic impurities on superconducting surfaces [2–5]. Tere, correlation efects play a subordinate role and, therefore, the YSR states can be treated well within the mean feld approximation [6–15]. However, in other cases the spin needs to be treated fully quantum mechanically and correlation efects are indispens- able to describe the observations, such as the Kondo efect, which emerges when magnetic impurities are placed on nor- mal conducting surfaces [16–21]. Te only diference between YSR and Kondo physics is a superconducting instead of a normal conducting bath. Tus, both phenomena can be theoretically described in a uni- fed framework within the single impurity Anderson model (SIAM). A solution of such a model including full correla- tions can be achieved by means of numerical renormalization group (NRG) theory [22–24]. Te hallmark of this theory is a universal scaling relation between the normalized YSR en- ergy εYSR/Δ and kBTK/Δ, where εYSR is the YSR energy, TK is the Kondo temperature, kB is the Boltzmann constant and Δ is the superconducting order parameter. Te quantum phase transition (QPT) associated with YSR-physics is predicted to occur at kBTK/Δ = 0.24 [1, 25–28]. Despite these convincing theoretical predictions for the universal scaling, there are diferent aspects of uncertainty in existing experimental results, especially in atomic junctions where fting the tunneling spectra is essential for extracting the Kondo temperature [29–33]. One reason is that the com- monly reported Kondo systems are not purely spin-1⁄2, which complicates the analysis. Another reason is that the experi- mental procedures followed for the extraction of the Kondo temperature are usually phenomenological, either done by simply taking the half width half maximum (HWHM) of the Kondo peak or by fting with rather phenomenological func- tions like Fano or Frota functions , while a much beter solution is to employ NRG directly . Furthermore, the fnite experimental temperature results in a thermal broad- ening obscuring the spectral peak. Tese obstacles as well as the existence of various defnitions of the Kondo temperature result in an ambiguity of the obtained Kondo temperature, up to a factor of four . Here, we follow a more rigorous procedure and extract the Kondo temperature unambiguously by employing the full NRG theory on the microscopic SIAM to directly ft the Kondo spectra on spin-1⁄2 YSR impurities on the vanadium tip in magnetic felds at low temperatures (10 mK). We compare the Kondo temperature with the YSR energy at zero feld and demonstrates the universal scaling. Since both phenomena are treated within one microscopic framework, the ambiguity of the Kondo temperature is eliminated. Discussion Tere are multiple indications that the impurity induced YSR states in vanadium are spin-1⁄2 systems ranging from single quasiparticle level tunneling and its spin selection , to the observation of a 0-π-transition . Tis is further corroborated by the observation of only one pair of YSR peaks on both sides of the QPT (see Fig. S4(b), in contrast with high spin systems ) and the remarkable quantitative agreement when fting the Kondo spectra with the spin-1⁄2 SIAM model using NRG theory (see Figs. S1(a) and (b)). Tis makes our system a model platform to study the relation be- tween the Kondo efect and YSR states. Similar scaling has been observed before for diferent kinds of impurities (not necessarily spin-1⁄2) [29–33, 49]. In most cases, the experimentally derived defnition of the Kondo temperature leaves some ambiguity in the comparison with the universal scaling. Using the full microscopic NRG model we not only minimized the ambiguity but also accounted for the efect of the applied magnetic feld in fting the data. In addition, since our experimental temperature of 10 mK is much smaller than the Kondo temperature, the intrin- sic temperature broadening of the Kondo peak is negligible . Furthermore, the Fermi-Dirac broadening of the probing electrode can also be neglected due to our high en- ergy resolution at mK temperatures. Consequently, we do not need any deconvolution to extract the real Kondo signal from the experimentally broadened peaks, further achieving quantitative accuracy. Still, the remaining deviations from the universal scaling as displayed in Fig. S2(b) raise interesting questions as to their origin. One possibility, as proposed in previous stud- ies, is the validity of the universal scaling with respect to the parameter space of the SIAM . For atoms on substrates in our case, both the impurity-substrate coupling Γ and the half band width D are always much lager than Δ, such that the universal scaling is valid and precise independent of U . Terefore, this reasoning can be ruled out as outlined in the Supplementary Information . However, a realistic structured electronic band and non-constant density of states can modify the Kondo physics in a non-trivial way [51–53] and thus go beyond the simple assumption of the universal scaling. It is therefore interesting to investigate the validity or breakdown of the universal scaling in weakly coupled sys- tems like certain molecules and in systems with non-trivial band structure. We further fnd that the ft for diferent magnetic felds dif- fers slightly even for the same impurity. Tis indicates that the magnetic feld may change the force exerted on the im- purity and in turn the impurity-substrate coupling. Also, the quenching of the superconductivity could change the atomic forces modifying the impurity-substrate coupling. Tis is reasonable because the relative deviation in terms of the impurity-substrate coupling is only a few percent (see Fig. S1(d)), while the resulting response in the Kondo tempera- ture is much larger due to the exponential dependence (Eq. (1)). Other mechanisms including residual interactions with nearby spins could also infuence the universal scaling be- havior. Conclusion In summary, we have analyzed the scaling between the YSR energy and the Kondo temperature fts of Kondo spectra using microscopic model on the magnetic impurities at the apex of a vanadium tip. Te impurities show spin-1⁄2 charac- teristics and some of them move across the quantum phase transition during tip approach allowing for a continuous con- trol and investigation of the scaling behavior. Directly using NRG theory in the fting of the Kondo spectra reduces the ambiguity in extracting the Kondo temperature compared to experimentally derived defnitions of the Kondo temperature. Additionally, our experimental temperature in the mK regime minimizes the experimental broadening. With this, our re- sults corroborate the universal scaling of the NRG theory at the atomic scale with quantitative precision. Te observed deviations point to a degree of freedom not captured by the current theory like modifed impurity-substrate coupling in a magnetic feld, which calls for further investigations. A fu- ture direction would be to apply such analysis to other spin-1⁄2 systems as well as higher spin systems or coupled YSR dimers .
