XX, XX 2022 1 Capacitively-coupled resonators for terahertz planar-Goubau-line filters Juan Cabello-Sánchez, Graduate Student Member, IEEE, Vladimir Drakinskiy, Jan Stake, Senior Member, IEEE, Helena Rodilla, Senior Member, IEEE Abstract—Low-loss planar Goubau lines show promising potential for terahertz applications. However, a single-wire waveguide exhibits less design freedom than standard multi-conductor lines, which is a significant constraint for realizing standard components. Existing filters for planar Goubau line lack clear design procedures preventing the synthesis of an arbitrary filter response. In this work, we present a design for a bandpass/bandstop filter for planar Goubau line by periodically loading the line with capacitively coupled 휆∕2 resonators, which can be easily tuned by changing their electrical length. The filter’s working principle is explained by a proposed transmission-line model. We designed and fabricated a passband filter centered at 0.9 THz on a 10-휇m silicon-membrane substrate and compared measurement results between 0.5 THz and 1.1 THz to electromagnetic simulations, showing excellent agreement in both 푆11 and 푆21. The measured passband has an insertion loss of 7 dB and a 3-dB bandwidth of 31%. Overall, the proposed filter design has good performance while having a simple design procedure. INTRODUCTION The development of efficient radio-frequency technology and components at terahertz (THz) frequencies is essential for improving the performance and possibilities of THz applications, such as radio-astronomy , security , medical applications , telecommunications , pharmaceutical quality control , and biomolecular dynamics . When planar technology is required—for its good integration, ease of fabrication, and low cost—it is necessary to minimize the high power losses present at THz frequencies to have good circuit performance. A fundamental way to minimize losses is to use power-efficient transmission lines. Some of the most used metal planar waveguides at THz frequencies include coplanar waveguide , coplanar stripline , microstrip , and planar Goubau line (PGL) . The PGL is the planar version of the Goubau line , whose propagation mode is similar to the Sommerfeld or Zenneck waves with a field that decays exponentially in the transverse direction. Similarly, the PGL can propagate a quasi-transverse-magnetic surface wave, which spreads radially from its single conductor, covering a large cross-section, and at the same time has higher field intensity at the edges of its conducting strip. Its propagation conditions depend on the electrical thickness of its substrate , where electrically-thin substrates minimize losses and dispersion. The dispersion, effective refractive index, field confinement, and ohmic losses can be increased by adding periodic corrugations or grooves , a concept explored by Goubau in 1950 , and sometimes referred to as spoof surface plasmon polariton waveguides . Without corrugations, the PGL has shown to have one of the highest power efficiencies for metal planar waveguides , thus being a good candidate for circuit design at THz frequencies. Designing circuit elements for PGL remains challenging due to its lack of ground plane. METHOD The proposed terahertz filter consists of a PGL periodically loaded with capacitively-coupled 휆∕2 resonators in a balanced configuration, see Fig. 1. The resonators are physically separated from the PGL to not short-circuit its propagating mode but close enough to produce coupling between the PGL and resonators. The input impedance presented by the shunt resonators along the line creates a frequency response with stopbands when the resonators exhibit impedances close to a short-circuit. The periodic structure of 푁 equal resonator pairs in cascade increases the bandwidth of the stopbands and, thereby, produces sharper and narrower passbands. The layout parameters of the filter (Fig. 1b) are the PGL’s width, 푤푝; resonator’s length, 푙푟, gap, 푔푟, and width, 푤푟; and the line-resonator coupling gap, 푔푐. This section describes the transmission-line model, design, fabrication process, and on-wafer measurement setup to demonstrate a bandpass PGL filter centered at 0.9 THz fabricated on a silicon membrane. The filter, consisting of repeating resonators along the PGL, can be modeled as periodic unit cells of length 푙푐 = 푔푟 + 푤푟. Each unit cell represents a section of the PGL central filter strip with a coupled resonator-pair and two half resonator-gaps on both sides, modeled as shown in Fig. 2. The inductances, 퐿푐, represent an electrically-short high-impedance line section with length 푔푟∕2. The unit-cell model does not consider any coupling to neighboring resonators. Two types of planar waveguides are used in the unit-cell model: coplanar waveguides (even mode) for the central line crossing the filter, 훾푐 and 푍푐; and parallel metal strips (even mode) for the waves excited in the coupled resonators, 훾푟, and 푍푟. No coplanar waveguide odd modes will be excited in the central line due to the symmetry of the filter . Since the separation of contiguous resonators is much smaller than the wavelength, the excitation of the odd coplanar-stripline mode can be neglected . Filter design A 0.9-THz passband filter was designed, simulated and fabricated on a 10-휇m thick high-resistivity (휌 > 10 kΩ cm) suspended silicon membrane, with a bulk permittivity of 휖푟 = 11.7 and tan 훿 = 1.7 ⋅ 10−5 , and using gold as conductor (휎 = 4.1 × 107 S∕m). An electrically-thin suspended substrate helps reduce dielectric and radiation losses, crucial at THz frequencies. The radiation losses are minimized by having a higher phase constant, 훽, in the planar waveguides than in the undesired substrate modes –. With the proposed substrate, the critical frequencies for coupling to substrate modes are calculated to be beyond 5 THz, and for second-order substrate modes, the cut-off frequency is around 4.6 THz. The PGL was designed with a relatively large strip width of 푤푝 = 10 휇m, which decreases conductor loss . For the resonator width and gap, we took 푤푟 = 5 휇m and 푔푟 = 5 휇m to guarantee sub-wavelength structure and operate well below the Bragg frequency . In our case Im(훾푐)(푤푟+푔푟) = 휋 around 5 THz, thus we operate well below the Bragg frequency. We chose a coupling gap of 푔푐 = 10 휇m, which coupled the resonators while not short-circuiting the line’s propagating mode. After choosing the substrate and filter layout dimensions, the propagation constants and impedances of the three lines involved in the model were calculated with electromagnetic simulations using absorbing boundary conditions. Port sizes were a vacuum wavelength at the lowest frequency of analysis for PGL and resonators, and 3(푤푝 + 2푔푐) by 2(푤푝 + 2푔푐) for the rectangular port for the coplanar waveguide. Simulation results were calculated between 50 GHz and 1.5 THz in several frequency bands and extrapolated down to dc. RESULTS First, Fig. 8 presents the measured S-parameters of a 1-mm-long PGL with no resonators. The loss goes from 1 dB/mm at 0.5 THz to 3 dB/mm for 1.1 THz, having relatively low losses for a metal planar waveguide at THz frequencies. The magnitude of 푆11 is at the noise floor of the measurement setup. In Fig. 9, the measured S-parameters of the filter from 0.5 THz to 1.1 THz are shown together with 3D simulations and the transmission-line model. The results from electromagnetic simulations show excellent agreement with measurements. The minor difference between simulation and experimental results in 푆21 can be explained by fabrication tolerances, variation in thin-film material properties, and calibration uncertainties. The transmission-line model describes well the passband/stopband regime with a slight frequency shift which becomes more noticeable at higher frequencies. The filter’s 푆21 shows a stopband and a passband centered at 0.6 THz and 0.9 THz, respectively, with 3-dB bandwidth of 27% and 31%, respectively. The bandpass has insertion loss of 7 dB—or about 4 dB more than a PGL of the same length without resonators (Fig. 8)—which are competitive compared to planar filters measured in the sub-millimeter band (Table I). Both 푆11 and 푆21 show an asymmetrical passband response, with steeper band transitions at higher passband frequencies. This effect could be explained by an increase in the effective refractive index at higher passband frequencies caused by the periodic resonators in the filter. The ripples in 푆11 result from the impedance mismatch between the main PGL and the Bloch impedance of the periodic filter. The noise level limits in the measurement setup are around -50 dB for 푆21, and around -22 dB for 푆11. IV. CONCLUSION In summary, we present a planar-Goubau-line terahertz bandpass/bandstop filter which can be easily tuned by changing the electrical length of the periodic capacitively-coupled 휆∕2 resonators. The working principle of the periodic filter was confirmed by a simple transmission-line model, which agrees with simulations and measurements. The proposed filter design was validated by fabricating and measuring a periodic structure with 49 unit cells with a passband centered around 0.9 THz, showing good agreement between simulations and measurements, and a relatively low insertion loss of 7 dB compared to planar filters measured in the sub-millimeter band. Future work could explore the design and tune of a filter response by modulating the length of the different capacitively-coupled resonators , and further elaborate the transmission-line model.
