Introduction Bi2Se3, a well-established topological insulator, becomes superconducting when doped or intercalated with copper or other elements such as strontium . There is much debate on the superconducting state for this material. Initially the favorite was the fully gapped odd-parity state with A1u symmetry respecting all rotational symmetries of the crystal . However, possibilities later considered include also conventional s-wave , and more recently, broken rotational symmetry odd parity states . Moreover, the superconducting properties of this material have some unusual characteristics . The mechanism for superconductivity is also hotly debated . Early on, theoretical works on superconductivity in this material started with effective multi-orbital model motivated by the topological insulator Bi2Se3 itself. Thus the minimum model of the normal state part of the Hamiltonian HN contains four degrees of freedom, resulting from two orbitals of different parity together with two spins degree of freedom. For example, we can write, following HN(⃗k) = mσx + vzkzσy + vσz(kxsy − kysx) (1) where σz = ±1 represents the two (mainly) pz orbitals in the quintuple layer of Bi2Se3, and ⃗s the spin. Here ⃗k, kx, ky, kz represent the wavevector and its components, vz, v are velocities. m is a quantity of dimension energy distinguishing the topologically trivial (commonly taken as m > 0) and the non-trivial cases (m < 0). For simplicity, we shall confine ourselves to the vicinity of ⃗k = 0. (This model is thus valid only for very small doping. The main point we would like to discuss in this paper is the methodology of finding the pseudospin basis. Complications such as higher order terms in ⃗k, as well as the fact that the Fermi surface seemingly changes shape substantially for larger doping , would not be considered here. As noted before (e.g. ), the model (1) thus has symmetry D∞,h which is actually higher than D3d of the crystal. ) An important feature of this model is that spins and orbital degrees of freedom are coupled, as this is necessary to describe the topological insulator. Expressing superconducting pairing also in terms of these orbitals and spins, properties of the superconductor can be directly evaluated (e.g. ). While this approach is perfectly alright by itself, the connection with the previous superconductivity literature , in particular those on heavy fermion superconductivity where spin-orbit coupling also cannot be ignored, is unclear. There the approach was to express electronic states near the Fermi surface in the normal state in terms of pseudospins, (note that these then depend only on the normal state Hamiltonian), and Cooper-pairing between pseudospin states at opposite momenta then considered. One can connect these two pictures by, starting from say the multi-orbital model, explicitly construct this pseudospin basis set for the electrons near the Fermi surface, and then express the pairing in terms of that between the pseudospins at ±⃗k. For the basis set to be “more useful” in the sense that connection with the earlier literature can be made, these Pseudospin bases for Cu:Bi2Se3 3 pseudospin states must satisfy certain symmetry criteria, in particular those imposed by time-reversal, parity and rotational symmetries. At a typical point (labeled by the momentum ⃗k) on the Fermi surface, there are two degenerate states since the crystal obeys time-reversal and parity symmetries. Given the Hamiltonian, it is straight-forward to find two orthonormal states, say |⃗k, α′⟩ and |⃗k, β′⟩, at each point on the Fermi surface. Symmetry operations map states from one point of the Fermi surface to a linear combination of those at another (and for some operations, the same) point. The task at hand is to find a suitable unitary transformation among these two states |⃗k, α′⟩ and |⃗k, β′⟩ to form two new ones |⃗k, α⟩ and |⃗k, β⟩ so that the latter would transform the same way as “up” and “down” spins under the symmetry operations. ‡ One such explicit construction for the model (1) was given by the author himself , and another, slightly later, by Fu and his coworkers . The two pseudospin bases differ slightly, reflecting the non-uniqueness of their choice. With these constructions, one can, for example, now re-expressed the pairings ∆1,2,3,4 introduced in , originally in terms of orbitals and spins of (1), in terms of the basis functions listed in, e.g. . § Many properties of these superconducting states are then directly evident. Thus sx,y,z become 2 × 2 matrices, and are then linear combinations of ρ′ x,y,z, the Pauli matrices in |⃗k, α′⟩ and |⃗k, β′⟩ space. In general ρ′ x,y,z would fail to transform as the x, y, z components of a pseudo-vector. The required proper basis |⃗k, α⟩ and |⃗k, β⟩, can be easily identified, since the Pauli matrices ρx,y,z in this space is related to ρ′ x,y,z by a rotation which is related to the unitary transformation between |⃗k, α⟩, |⃗k, β⟩ and |⃗k, α′⟩, |⃗k, β′⟩. As a byproduct, one also obtains an expression of the magnetic moment of quasiparticles in terms of the pseudospins. Actually, the general form for the magnetic moment corresponding to this two- orbital model is more complicated. As pointed out in , the magnetic moment, in the notations of (1), is a linear combination of ⃗s and σx⃗s (more precise statements below). One is actually free to choose any linear combination between these two quantities to carry out the procedure outlined above, since they are both pseudovectors. A slightly different pseudospin basis would be obtained, and this reflects the non-uniqueness of the choice of the pseudospin basis. On the other hand, Fu and his coworkers introduce what they called “manifestly covariant Bloch basis” (MCBB). They construct this by first identifying a point which they regard as invariant under the relevant symmetries. By demanding that |⃗k, α⟩ and |⃗k, β⟩ at this point be proportional to a real number times a wavefunction consisting of spin up and down respectively, they show that this basis automatically possesses the required transformation, in particular rotational properties. For the example of (1), they regard the system as invariant under the parity operation σx. In this way, a pseudospin basis was constructed . A moment of thought shows that, in the method used by the author , if the operator (1 + σx)⃗s was employed instead of ⃗s in , the basis constructed would then just be MCBB. (Lest this is not obvious, an explicit demonstration would be given in Sec 2.1.). Viewed entirely mathematically, in both and , the procedures used amount to reducing the effective wavefunctions to be only column matrices of two entries. In , it was done by choosing a pseudovector operator and evaluating it in the Hilbert space |⃗k, α′⟩ and |⃗k, β′⟩, and one worked with 2 × 2 matrices rather than wavefunctions. In , it was done by identifying a particular symmetry operation that does so. The choice needed for the procedure in can always be chosen as ⃗s or any suitable modifications. The same choice can be used for more general situations, such as when the model contains more orbitals than two, or when the symmetry operations contain screw axes or glide planes. For , the parity operator may not directly be able to reduce the wavefunctions to a two-component one, though one is likely able to identify a suitable generalization. Conclusion In conclusion, we have presented a slight generalization of the method in Ref for constructing the pseudospin basis of a time-reversal and inversion symmetric system. We have also shown how it can generate the “manifestly covariant Bloch basis” of Ref in the example given by (1). We emphasize once more that the method of is applicable beyond (1), in particular it is not restricted to the number of orbitals involved. 4.
