arXiv:2309.10734v1 [cond-mat.mtrl-sci] 19 Sep 2023 Molecular dynamics study of diffusionless phase transformations in HMX: β-HMX twinning and β-ε phase transition Andrey Pereverzev Department of Chemistry and Materials Science & Engineering Institute, University of Missouri, Columbia, MO 65211, USA (*pereverzeva@missouri.edu.) (Dated: September 20, 2023) We use molecular dynamics to study mechanism of deformation twinning of β-1,3,5,7-tetranitro-1,3,5,7- tetrazocane (β-HMX) in the P21/n space group setting for the twin system specified by K1 = (101), η1 = , K2 = (101), and η2 = at T = 1 K and 300 K. Twinning of a single perfect crystal was induced by imposing increasing stress. The following three forms of stress were considered: uniaxial compression along , shear stress in K1 plane along η1 direction, and shear stress in K2 plane along η2 direction. In all cases the crystal transforms to its twin by the same mechanism: as the stress increases, the a and c lattice parameters become, respectively, longer and shorter; soon after the magnitude of a exceeds that of c the system undergoes a quick phase-transition-like transformation. This transformation can be approximately separated into two stages: glide of the essentially intact {101} crystal planes along ⟨101⟩ crystal directions followed by rotations of all HMX molecules accompanied by N-NO2 and CH2 group rearrangements. The overall process corresponds to a military transformation. If uniaxial compression along is applied to a β-HMX crystal which is already subject to a hydrostatic pressure ≳ 10 GPa, the transformation described above proceeds through the crystal- plane gliding stage but only minor molecular rearrangements occurs. This results in a high-pressure phase of HMX which belongs to the P21/n space group. The coexistence curve for this high-pressure phase and β-HMX is constructed using the harmonic approximation for the crystal Hamiltonians. I. INTRODUCTION The high explosive 1,3,5,7-tetranitro-1,3,5,7-tetrazocane, also known as octahydro-1,3,5,7-tetranitro-1,3,5,7-tetrazocine (HMX), is an important energetic material which is used in a number of high performance military explosive and propel- lant formulations. Several polymorphs of HMX are known at standard ambient conditions,[2–5] among which β-HMX is the one that is thermodynamically stable. Several other poly- morphs were reported, most of them under high pressure. Yoo and Cynn used Raman spectroscopy and x-ray diffraction to study phase transition of β-HMX in the quasi-hydrostatic pressure up to 45 GPa. They found evidence of β-HMX trans- forming to ε-HMX at 12 GPa and ε-HMX transforming to φ- HMX at 27 GPa. Molecular dynamics simulations by Lu et al. showed sudden change in lattice parameters of β-HMX at 27 GPa, which could be related to a phase transition. However, Zhang et al., using first-principles calculations, did not find evidence of a phase transition at 27 GPa. Ko- rsunskii et al. obtained a polymorph belonging to P21/c space group with 10 molecules per unit cell; this polymorph is metastable at standard ambient conditions, it quickly trans- forms to β form when ground in a mortar. Sui et al. and Gao et al. used Raman and infrared spectroscopy and x-ray diffraction under hydrostatic and non-hydrostatic pres- sure. They saw evidence of several phase transitions for pres- sures between 5 and 40 GPa. Recently, Fan et al. per- formed simulations of uniaxial compression of β-HMX using DFT and reported a possible structural transformation at the pressure of 6.82–9.15 GPa for orientation. In addition to exhibiting polymorphic transformations, HMX is known to undergo twinning. In particular, β-HMX can form both growth and deformation twins. Growth twins of β-HMX with the (101) twinning plane were reported.[13– 15] Several groups studied plastic deformation of β-HMX experimenatlly. Palmer and Field investigated compression and indentation of HMX and reported the formation of (101) twins. Cady showed that strained HMX crystals form (101) twins which are reversible upon load removal when cre- ated under small strains and irreversible when produced under large strains and at temperatures above 373 K. Microhardness indentation experiments on β-HMX conducted in Ref. showed evidence of twinning on (101) plane. The twin system for deformation twinning of β-HMX specified by K1 = (101), η1 = , K2 = (101) , and η2 = was identified by Armstrong et al. Pal and Picu used molecular dynam- ics to study dislocation slip in β-HMX for (011) and (101) slip planes and saw evidence of twinning in the case of (101) slip plane. A crystal twinning plasticity model of β-HMX was de- veloped by Zecevic et al. Cawkwell et al. calculated the energy the (101) twin boundary using DFT approach. The detailed mechanism of the deformation twin forma- tion in β-HMX is not well understood. Armstrong et al. proposed a mechanism which involves {101} crystal planes translation in direction followed by molecular rotation by 180°. This mechanism is characterized by high potential energy barrier. In this work we use molecular dynamics to study the twinning mechanism in β-HMX and show that it in- volves much more moderate molecular rotation accompanied by some conformational changes. II. β-HMX DEFORMATION TWINNING A. Simulation force field All molecular dynamics simulations were performed us- ing the LAMMPS package. We employed the nonre- active, fully flexible molecular force field for HMX pro- posed by Smith and Bharadwaj and further developed by 2 Bedrov et al. and others. This force field is well- validated and has been used in numerous previous studies of HMX.[19, 24, 27–34] We used the version of the force field first described in Ref. . Sample LAMMPS input decks in- cluding all force-field parameters and details of how the forces were evaluated can be found in the supplementary material of Ref. . B. Twinning induced by the uniaxial compression along To study the mechanism of deformation twining associated with the twin system specified by K1 = (101), η1 = , K2 = (101), η2 = we initially performed molecular dy- namics simulation of uniaxial compression of β-HMX at 1 K and zero initial pressure. At this temperature the thermal noise is eliminated and the twinning mechanism can be seen clearly. The unit cell of β-HMX in the P21/n space group setting is shown in Fig. 2(a). The uniaxial compression simulations were performed for a three-dimensionally periodic supercell of β-HMX comprising 4 × 4 × 4 unit cells (3584 atoms): we verified that using a larger supercell (16 × 8 × 16 unit cells) had little effect on the basic twinning transformation steps. We compressed β-HMX uniaxially along several directions lying in the (010) plane in the vicinity of the direction; this direction was chosen because uniaxial compression along induces shear along both (η1) and (η2). The pressure along a given direction was increased from 0 to 2 GPa over the time interval of 1 ns using NPT simulations. The pressure values for which twinning occurs, P001,α, are shown in Fig. 1 as functions of α, the angle between the direc- tion and the compression direction in (010) plane. The min- imum of the fitted curve in Fig. 1 lies close to the direction with α = 0. For this reason, we used uniaxial compression along for further studies of uniaxial compression. Note that pressure tensor parameters for NPT simulations in LAMMPS are specified with respect to the Cartesian frame and not the crystal frame. As a result, if the direction of uni- axial compression and a chosen crystal direction are exactly aligned at the start of the simulation they will not, in general, be exactly aligned at a higher pressure because the simula- tion box deforms under stress. To circumvent this problem we note that LAMMPS always aligns one of the three edges of the simulation box with the x Cartesian axis. To ensure that the compression was directed exactly along for any pressure, we aligned direction of the 4 × 4 × 4 supercell with the x axis and applied compression along x. The crystal transforms to its twin as the pressure tensor component along , P001, reaches a critical value. The transformation is military and follows the mechanism whose main features are shown in Figs. 2 and 3. As the mag- nitude of the stress increases, the lattice vectors a and c be- come, respectively, longer and shorter and the system poten- tial energy increases. Fig. 2 (b) shows the compression stage at which the lattice parameters a and c become equal. Soon after the magnitude of a exceeds that of c (Fig. 3 (a)) the sys- tem undergoes a quick, phase-transition-like transition to its twin. The separation into the glide and rotation-rearrangement stages is approximate in that the molecular rotation begins before the glide stage is fully complete. For a given HMX molecule, the molecular rotation axis is formed by intersection of the plane passing through the four carbon atoms and the plane passing through the four nitrogen atoms of the axial N-NO2 groups. The rotation angle when transforming from the structure in Fig. 2(c) to that in Fig. 2(d) is approximately 24°. Overall, the transformation results in the β-HMX crystal in which, in the Cartesian frame, the original lattice vectors a and c are switched and the lattice vector b is flipped. The twinned unit cell in Fig. 2(d) can be viewed as a reflection of the original unit cell in Fig. 2(a) in a plane that passes through b and bi- sects β, the angle formed by a and c. The two unit cells are related through the P21/n space group symmetries and, there- fore, both represent β-HMX unit cells. The unit cell potential energy U (Fig. 3(b)) is an approximately quadratic function of P001 for P001 < 1 GPa; U reaches its maximum value at the end of the {101} planes gliding stage and decreases as the molecules rotate and their functional groups rearrange. The value of P001 = 1.1483±0.0001 GPa which induces the twin- ning was taken to be the value at which the derivatives of lat- tice parameters a and c with respect to P001 have the highest magnitude. A video of the twinning transformation is pro- vided in the supplementary material. Applying uniaxial compression along to the same 4 × 4 × 4 supercell of β-HMX at T= 300 K and zero initial pressure induces deformation twinning that follows the same basic steps as at T=1 K. Apart from the presence of thermal noise, the main difference from the 1 K case is that the value of P001 at which the twinning occurs is lower and given by 0.792 ± 0.004 GPa. C. Twinning induced by shear The twin system studied here is specified by the conjugate twinning planes K1 = (101), K2 = (101) and conjugate twin- ning directions η1 = and η2 = . This implies that the deformation twinning can also be induced by applying shear stress either in K1 plane along η1 direction or in K2 plane along η2 directions. We studied both cases at T=1 K and 300 K. Evaluation of the (101) twin boundary energy is given in the Appendix. As in the case of the uniaxial compression discussed in the previous subsection, we needed to choose the proper orienta- tion of the simulation box to ensure that the shear stress is al- ways aligned with the corresponding twinning plane and twin- ning direction as the box deforms under stress. This could not be done exactly with the supercell that we used for the uniaxial compression. To overcome this, we used a non- conventional 4-molecule unit cell of β-HMX whose lattice 4 by shear, projected onto the (010) plane. The shear stress is applied (η2). Projections of the conventional P21/n unit cell (solid gray) and the non-conventional 4-molecule unit cell (dashes) are also shown. vectors a′, b′, and c′ are expressed through the original a, b, and c as a′ = a−c, b′ = b, and c′ = a+c, thus the faces of this new unit cell are parallel to (101), (010), and (101) planes in the original P21/n space group setting. The supercell comprising 3×4×3 of these unit cells (4032 atoms), which was used to study twinning under shear, is shown in Fig. 4. The twinning was induced by increasing the magnitude of the corresponding shear stress component from 0 to 0.8 GPa over the time interval of 1 ns using NPT simulaions. The twinning mechanism both for shear applied in K1 plane and η1 direction and in K2 plane and η2 direc- tion follows the same basic steps as in the case of the uniaxial compression. The magnitude of the shear stress that induces twinning for the case of K1 and η1 is 0.4511 ± 0.0001 GPa at 1 K and 0.320 ± 0.002 GPa at 300 K. For K2 and η2 the val- ues are 0.4463±0.0001 GPa at 1 K and 0.318±0.002 GPa at 300 K. Note that for a given temperature, the magnitudes of twinning-inducing stress for the two twinning directions are almost the same. This can be expected: as in the case of the uniaxial compression, twinning occurs in the vicinity of the cell geometry for which lattice parameters a and c are equal or, equivalently, when the simulation cell shown in Fig. 4 becomes orthogonal; if an orthogonal cell is obtained by ap- plying stress either in K1 along η1 or in K2 along η2, then the magnitudes of these two stresses have to be identical. III. β-ε PHASE TRANSITION The twinning mechanism discussed in the previous section involves a glide of crystal planes followed by rotation and re- arrangement of HMX molecules. This two stage process hints at the possibility of a transformation in which, under proper conditions, the molecular rotation does not occur. Indeed, if ent orientation and geometry of the HMX molecules. the uniaxial compression along is applied to a β-HMX crystal which is already subject to a hydrostatic pressure ≳ 10 GPa, the transformation described above proceeds through the crystal-plane gliding stage (Fig. 2(c)) but only minor molecu- lar rearrangements occur. This results in a high-pressure phase of HMX belonging to the P21/n space group that remains sta- ble or metastable for hydrostatic pressure ≳ 10 GPa (Fig. 7). For notational convenience we refer to this phase as ε-HMX. Since the transformation from β- to ε-HMX is military, the unit cell of ε-HMX consists of two HMX molecules as shown in Fig. 5. The β and ε polymorphs differ by molecular orien- tation, molecular geometry, and the lattice parameter values. The lattice parameters of ε-HMX at T = 300 K and P = 12 GPa are given by a = 5.9529 Å, b = 9.0800 Å, c = 7.5720 Å, β = 97.32° with the unit cell volume Vuc = 405.947 Å3. The lattice parameters of β-HMX under the same conditions are a = 6.2303 Å, b = 9.3231 Å, c = 7.0413 Å, β = 97.12° with Vuc = 405.849 Å3. Molecules of HMX in the ε polymorph are in the chair conformation but have a somewhat different orien- tation in the unit cell compared to the β form. Also note that the equatorial amino nitrogen atoms exhibit pyramidal inver- sion with respect to the same atoms in the β polymorph. Data files for the unit cells shown in Fig. 5 and a video of the β −ε transformation under uniaxial compression are provided in the supplementary materials. The phase diagram for β- and ε-HMX in P-T space was obtained using the condition ∆G = 0, where ∆G is the differ- ence of the Gibbs free energies for β and ε phases. ∆G was calculated from ∆G = ∆U + P∆V − T∆S, (1) where ∆U, ∆V, and ∆S are, respectively, the differences of internal energies, volumes, and entropies for the two phases. (1) were obtained as functions of pressure and temperature from MD simulations: for a selected fixed temperature NPT simulations were run with increasing hydrostatic pressure for β- and ε- HMX. The resulting phase diagram is shown in Fig. 7. ε-HMX is thermodynamically stable above approximately 45 GPa; the phase boundary shows very weak dependence on temperature over the considered temperature range. ε-HMX remains metastable well below 45 GPa. The dashed curve in phase boundary is shown in solid black. ε-HMX is metastable in the region between the dashed curve and the phase boundary. Fig. 7 corresponds to the pressure and temperature at which ε-HMX transforms to β-HMX. In particular, ε-HMX trans- forms to β polymorph at approximately 6.1 GPa at T = 1 K, 8.7 GPa at T = 300 K, and 10.5 GPa at T = 500 K; the transformation is military. A video of ε-β transformation is provided in the supplementary materials. Note that the phase diagram in Fig. 7 does not include α, δ, or other possible high-pressure polymorphs of HMX. IV. DISCUSSION Armstrong et al. studied deformation twinning of β- HMX for the twin system specified by K1 = (101), η1 = , K2 = (101) , and η2 = . They proposed a twinning mechanism which involves {101} crystal planes translation in direction followed by molecular rotation by 180°. This mechanism is characterized by a high potential energy barrier. Our molecular dynamics results show that β-HMX deforma- tion twinning does involve {101} crystal planes translation and molecular rotation; however, the molecular rotation that leads to a twin structure is much more moderate compared to the one proposed by Armstrong et al. At the same time, the mechanism that we observe in this study involves a change of molecular conformation: each HMX molecule transforms to its mirror image. The twinning mechanism for crystals with a basis which is qualitatively similar to the one observed in this work was suggested by Bilby and Crocker. Twinning of β-HMX studied here may have implications for simulation of shock propagation in β-HMX. Our results suggest that shocking β-HMX along or its vicinity is likely to induce twinning by the same mechanism. A critical aspect of simulating such shocks is to use boundary conditions 6 which would allow the system to expand in the directions nor- mal to the shock direction. Our observation of a high-pressure phase of HMX belong- ing to P21/n space group (ε-HMX) finds some support in the experimental results of Sui et al. and Gao et al. who reported formation of several phases of HMX under high pres- sure. Gao et al. identified two of these phases as belonging to space group P21/c (or, equivalently, P21/n) with two HMX molecules per unit cell. They reported fractional coordinates and lattice parameters for these phases. Unfortunately, we were unable to reproduce meaningful unit cell structures from the fractional coordinates data of Ref. and, therefore, could not directly compare the structure of ε-HMX studied in this work to the two phases discussed by Gao et al. V. CONCLUSIONS We used molecular dynamics simulations to clarify the mechanism of twinning in β-HMX for the twin system spec- ified by K1 = (101), η1 = , K2 = (101), and η2 = . Our results apply to a single perfect crystal in which twinning proceeds militarily. Twinning in crystals with defects is likely to follow more complicated paths which require addi- tional studies. Molecular dynamics studies of other possible twinning planes and directions in β-HMX such as the ones discussed in Ref. can be a subject of future research. It is also of interest to compare the molecular dynamics results for a single perfect crystal reported here to predictions of the twinning plasticity model developed by Zecevic et al. Our simulations show that the formation of the high pres- sure phase of ε-HMX can be viewed as the twinning trans- formation that did not proceed to completion. In this sense, the new polymorph appears to be physically plausible. How- ever, additional studies of ε-HMX such as high-level quantum calculation would be useful to ensure that it does represent a physical high-pressure phase of HMX. VI. Appendix: Energy of the (101) twin boundary It is of interest to estimate the value of the (101) twin boundary energy and compare it to the DFT-calculated results obtained by Cawkwell et al.. The supercell with (101) twin boundary was created using two supercells used in the shear simulations in subsection II C and shown in Fig. (4). One of these supecells was rotated by 180° about the normal to (101) plane and superposed on the non-rotated supercell to form a twin boundary as shown in Fig.8. Note that, at the twin boundary of the composite supercell, the crests and troughs of the zigzag molecular patterns as seen on the right panel of Fig. 8 are matching for the top and bottom constitutive supercells; this is of relevance to the discussion below. The supercell with the twin boundary was equilibrated at T = 1 K and zero pressure using NPT simulations. The av- erage potential energy E of the supercell was calculated from the 500 ps production NPT trajectory. An identical calculation was performed for a perfect crystal of β-HMX constructed from 3 × 4 × 6 non-conventional 4-molecule unit cells dis- cussed in subsecton II C to obtain the average potential en- ergy E0 for a crystal without the twin boundary. Following Ref. the twin boundary energy γ was calculated using γ = E − E0 2A , (A.1) where A is the area of the twin boundary and the factor of two accounts for the presence of two twin boundaries in a periodic cell. The resulting energy of the (101) twin boundary is 28.2 mJ/m2. Note that our results apply to a system at T = 1 K 7 and zero pressure. However, at this temperature, we can ex- pect them to be close to the values obtained by energy mini- mization, the approach used by Cawkwell et al. The value obtained by Cawkwell et al. is 163 mJ/m2, which is approxi- mately six times higher than our result. It seems unlikely that such a big difference in γ values is due to the different com- putational approaches used. It is possible that the (101) twin boundary studied by Cawkwell et al. is microscopically dif- ferent from the one considered here. Cawkwell et al. did not provide detailed description or images of the twin boundary that they studied. However, based on their description of the supercell preparation procedure, it appears that the top half of their supercell is the mirror image of the bottom half. If this is indeed the case then the twin boundary of Cawkwell et al. is different from ours and can be obtained from the one shown in Fig. 8 by displacing the top half of the supercell half a unit cell along . This will result in a supercell in which the crests of the zigzag patterns for the bottom half of the supercell (as seen on the right panel of Fig. 8) are facing the troughs (with the same functional groups) of the top half. Such twin bound- ary has higher energy than the one studied by us and cannot be simulated using the approach used here: the supercell with such twin boundary quickly transforms into the one in Fig. 8 in equilibrium simulations.
