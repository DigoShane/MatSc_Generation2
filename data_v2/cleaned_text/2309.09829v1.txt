Schrieffer-Wolff transformation for non-Hermitian systems: application for PT –symmetric circuit QED Grigory A. Starkov,1, ∗ Mikhail V. Fistul,1 and Ilya M. Eremin1 1Institut fur Theoretische Physik III, Ruhr-Universitat Bochum, 44801 Bochum, Germany (Dated: September 19, 2023) Combining non-hermiticity and interactions yields novel effects in open quantum many-body systems. Here, we develop the generalized Schrieffer-Wolff transformation and derive the effective Hamiltonian suitable for various quasi-degenerate non-Hermitian systems. We apply our results to an exemplary PT –symmetric circuit QED composed of two non-Hermitian qubits embedded in a lossless resonator. We consider a resonant quantum circuit as |ωr − Ω| ≪ ωr, where Ω and ωr are qubits and resonator frequencies, respectively, providing well-defined groups of quasi-degenerate resonant states. For such a system, using direct numerical diagonalization we obtain the dependence of the low-lying eigenspectrum on the interaction strength between a single qubit and the resonator, g, and the gain (loss) parameter γ, and compare that with the eigenvalues obtained analytically using the effective Hamiltonian of resonant states. We identify PT –symmetry broken and unbroken phases, trace the formation of Exceptional Points of the second and the third order, and provide a complete phase diagram g − γ of low-lying resonant states. We relate the formation of Exceptional Points to the additional P-pseudo-Hermitian symmetry of the system and show that non-hermiticity mixes the ”dark” and the ”bright” states, which has a direct experimental consequence. I. INTRODUCTION Seminal Schrieffer-Wolff transformation [1–3](also known as Van Vleck Quasi-Degenerate Perturbation the- ory [4–8] in some other contexts) has been used for many years as an extremely useful tool in a theoretical study of weakly interacting quantum systems. The Schrieffer- Wolff transformation allows one to consistently derive the effective low-energy Hamiltonian composed of the non- interacting part and the interaction terms taken into ac- count as a perturbation. Generally, Schrieffer-Wolf trans- formation was successfully applied to the perturbation analysis of various quantum-mechanical problems arising in electronic or optical systems, such as the Kondo prob- lem in the Anderson impurity model in normal metals or superconductors , multiorbital Hubbard models , Floquet spectrum of periodically driven systems , in- teracting qubits [12–18]. Moreover, the Schrieffer-Wolff transformation was also adapted to intrinsically dissipa- tive quantum systems described by Markovian master equations and, more recently, discussed in the con- text of some non-hermitian systems . A particular example of the qubits-type systems coined as circuit QED, i.e., various arrays of superconducting qubits coupled to a low dissipative resonator, presents a special interest for quantum computing, quantum simula- tions, and precise quantum measurements . Even a weak coupling of qubits to the resonator leads to numerous interesting phenomena, e.g., AC Stark shift of qubits frequencies , or the interaction between well separated qubits mediated by the exchange of virtual photons of the resonator , and the Schrieffer- Wolff transformation are routinely used to analyze these ∗ Grigorii.Starkov@rub.de effects. Recently, this field of study has got a new twist as so-called PT –symmetric non-Hermitian qubits systems have been experimentally realized in various solid state systems such as trapped ions, ultracold and Rydberg atoms [26–28], Bose–Einstein condensate , supercon- ducting [30–32] or nitrogen-vacancies qubits . In a PT –symmetric non-Hermitian qubit, specially chosen quantum states demonstrate a nonequilibrium growth of the population, i.e., the states with a gain. To establish the PT –symmetry the gain has to be completely equal- ized by a loss present in other parts of a system. A general theoretical study of PT -symmetric non- Hermitian Hamiltonians has been started by the works of Bender and coworkers [34–36]. They have shown that depending on the values of physical parameters the PT –symmetric non-Hermitian Hamiltonian exhibits two kinds of energy spectrum: purely real eigenvalues, identi- fying the unbroken (preserved) PT -symmetric quantum phase, or complex conjugate ones, indicating the appear- ance of broken PT –symmetry quantum phase. The Ex- ceptional Points (EPs) separate these phases. Systems of PT -symmetric interacting qubits (two- levels or spin 1/2) have been theoretically studied for two PT – non-Hermitian qubits with an exchange inter- action as well as in the framework of the quantum Ising model with a longitudinal or transverse non- Hermitian fields . At the same time, one should stress that in most previous works a theoretical study of the be- havior of eigenvalues and eigenvectors near an n-th order EP has been carried out using the local effective n × n Hamiltonian. Therefore, a study of the relation between the EPs arising in the presence of interaction between qubits, and the properties of non-interacting qubits sys- tem did not receive a proper attention so far. In this Article, we develop the generalized Schrieffer- Wolff transformation for arbitrary non-Hermitian inter- arXiv:2309.09829v1 [quant-ph] 18 Sep 2023 2 acting systems and derive the effective Hamiltonian in which the interaction is treated as the perturbation up to the second order. Since the Schrieffer-Wolff trans- formation is especially suited for quantum systems with well-defined groups of quasi-degenerate states , we ap- ply our generic analysis to an exemplary PT –symmetric quantum system, i.e., a PT –symmetric circuit QED com- posed of two non-Hermitian qubits weakly coupled to a lossless resonator. The non-Hermiticity is introduced in the circuit QED model in the form of an imaginary lon- gitudinal magnetic field of a strength, γ, or equivalently as a gain (loss) of corresponding energy levels of a sin- gle qubit. The resonant regime will be addressed as the qubit’s frequency Ω is assumed to be close to the fre- quency of a resonator, ωr . Combining the di- rect numerical diagonalization with the analysis based on the derived effective Hamiltonian, we study the low- energy spectrum of PT –symmetric circuit QED in detail. We identify various PT -symmetry preserved and broken quantum phases, and quantitatively analyze the forma- tion of EPs of different orders. The outline of the paper is as follows. In Section II we formulate the general Schrieffer-Wolff transformation suitable for quasi-degenerate non-Hermitian Hamiltonian systems. Using such transformation, we derive the ef- fective Hamiltonian. In Section III we introduce a ba- sic model of PT –symmetric circuit QED, i.e., two non- Hermitian qubits embedded in a lossless resonator, dis- cuss important symmetries of a system, and, using the direct numerical diagonalization, obtain the dependence of the eigenspectrum on the coupling strength, g, and the gain (loss) parameter, γ. In Section IV, we use the Schrieffer-Wolff transformation to derive the effec- tive Hamiltonian matrix of the resonant states and dis- cuss its symmetry properties. After that, in Section V, using the effective Hamiltonian we study the formation of Exceptional Points in detail, and produce a complete phase diagram of the low-lying resonant states. In Sec- tion VI the conclusions will be provided. There are also Appendix A, where we compute the matrix elements of the effective Hamiltonian, and Appendices B, C and D, where the derivation details regarding the phase diagram and EPs are presented. Next, by making use of the direct numerical diagonal- ization we compute the low-lying eigenspectrum of the Hamiltonian (22). Since the formation of EPs in the model is based on the parity-indices of the states due to its P-pseudo-Hermitian symmetry , we assign to each level a topological index equal to the parity of the level at γ = 0 . CONCLUSIONS To conclude, we extend the formalism of the gener- alized Schrieffer-Wolff transformation to arbitrary non- Hermitian interacting quantum systems, and derive the effective Hamiltonian taking into account the interaction terms as the perturbation up to the second order. To demonstrate the effectiveness of the method we apply this generic procedure to a basic PT –symmetric circuit QED composed of two non-Hermitian non-interacting super- conducting qubits weakly coupled to a lossless resonator of the frequency ωr. In this model the non-Hermiticity is introduced as a staggered imaginary longitudinal mag- netic field or gain/loss γ of corresponding qubits states. We consider PT –symmetric circuit QED biased in the resonant regime as the frequency of a single qubit Ω is close to the frequency of the resonator, i.e., Ω ≃ ωr. The resonant regime of a circuit QED is a simplest one providing well-defined groups of quasi-degenerate eigen- states even for moderate values of the coupling strength, g/Ω. Using the explicit expression for the effective Hamil- tonian, we calculate analytically the eigenspectrum of a PT –symmetric circuit QED for different parameters γ and g and identify the PT –symmetry preserved and bro- ken quantum phases, obtain the EPs of different orders. In particular, we obtain the values of parameters γcr and gcr for the third-order EPs. The values of γcr and gcr decrease with the detuning ωr − Ω and the mixing angle θ ≪ 1. We compare our analytical results with the direct nu- merical diagonalization of the Hamiltonian (22) and find a good agreement in the range of parameters g, γ ≪ 1 for both a longitudinal and transverse coupling between qubits and the resonator. Moreover, for PT quantum circuits with a longitudinal coupling we find a good agreement in a whole range of the interaction strength, g/Ω ≤ 0.4, and as a result one can obtain the position of the third-order EP with a great accuracy. The reason for that is the absence of a substantial mixing between eigen- states of different groups in PT –quantum circuit with a longitudinal coupling. The most important physical signature of the non- hermiticity is the mixture of ”bright” and ”dark” states. In particular, in the absence of non-Hermitian terms the energy spectrum of a circuit QED system splits into the subgroups of ”bright” and ”dark” states having different parity indices, and therefore, these subgroups do not mix with each other .
