Acoustophoresis of Legionella species in water and the influence of collective hydrodynamic focusing Alen Pavlic,∗a Marjan Veljkovic,b Lars Fieseler,b and Jürg Duala Legionella are gram-negative, facultative intracellular, and pathogenic bacteria that pose a risk for human health and cause significant energy losses due to extensive preventive heating-up of water installations. We investigate acoustically-driven motion — acoustophoresis — of several Legionella species, Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Acanthamoeba castellanii, a common Le- gionella host in water. All the investigated cells can be acoustically manipulated in an ultrasonic standing wave in water, as they possess a non-zero acoustic contrast that is positive for all of the cells, leading to the focusing into pressure nodes of the standing wave. Multi-body simulations indi- cate that an increase in cell concentration could significantly accelerate the rate of focusing due to hydrodynamic interactions — a phenomenon that we call collective hydrodynamic focusing (CHF). The results form a foundation for acoustic manipulation of bacteria and could pave a path towards acoustically-aided detection of Legionella in water. a Institute for Mechanical Systems, Swiss Federal Institute of Technology Zurich, Zürich, Switzerland. b Institute of Food and Beverage Innovation, Zurich University of Applied Sciences, Wädenswil, Switzerland. ∗ E-mail: apavlic@ethz.ch 1 arXiv:2211.11508v1 [physics.app-ph] 21 Nov 2022 Introduction Legionella is a genus of gram-negative bacteria that are harmful for human health and the causative agent of legionellosis that can appear as legionnaires’ disease, Pontiac fever, or Pittsburgh pneumonia.1 Due to the danger of Legionella, there are regula- tions in place to monitor and minimize their presence in water installations. A common approach to eradicate Legionella is peri- odically heating-up water supplies to above 60°C. However, pe- riodic heating requires continued energy supply.2 To save energy and to minimize the general impact of diseases originating from Legionella bacterial monitoring and analysis could be further im- proved. Currently, the detection of Legionella from water samples relies on manual collection of a rather large sample (1L), fol- lowed by labour-intensive and time-consuming culturing (up to 10 days).3 Low concentration limits stipulated by legislature and the associated low abundance of Legionella further complicate the process. One way to improve the monitoring of Legionella pres- ence could be an up-concentration of Legionella in an at-line fash- ion, prior to the analysis, combined with the use of novel and faster detection systems. Such a combined system could accom- plish a sustainable and reliable real-time detection of Legionella, leading to considerable energy savings and to the reduction of legionellosis risk on a global scale. The up-concentration of Legionella could be accomplished through the means of acoustophoresis,4 which is known to be a non-destructive method for manipulation of microscopic ob- jects at relatively high throughputs (flow rates of > 100μLmin−1 per device5). Acoustophoresis is often used in biomedical appli- cations to manipulate cells without affecting their viability, pro- vided that the operating power remains below thresholds for the onset of cavitation and for reaching the temperatures harmful to cells.6,7 The acoustic field giving rise to acoustophoresis can for example stem from diagnostic or therapeutic medical ultra- sound, advanced flow cytometers that use standing waves for higher throughput and accuracy,8 or lab-on-a-chip devices for characterization of mechanical properties of eukaryotic cells and organisms.9–12 However, acoustic manipulation of bacteria is lim- ited, mainly due to the small size of bacterial cells that leads to a small acoustic radiation force (ARF), which is the main force in acoustophoresis and scales with the volume of a cell. The acous- tic streaming that is generally present in acoustofluidic devices acts on the cells through the Stokes drag, which scales with the radius of a cell. This difference in scaling of the two competing forces can be characterized with a critical radius, above which the cell motion is dominated by the ARF, while the smaller cells move with the streaming flow. For a cell-like material in water, assum- ing a standing ultrasonic wave with the frequency f of 2MHz, the critical radius is typically ≈ 0.7μm and scales with the frequency as � 1/f.13 Despite the scaling of forces hindering the acoustic manipu- lation of bacteria like Legionella, with the equivalent sphere ra- dius (ESR) in the range of ∼ 0.5μm,14 various methods were de- veloped in the recent years. For example, using larger “seed” particles that trap bacteria supposedly with acoustic interaction forces,15,16 using two-dimensional acoustic fields to avoid the dis- turbance from the acoustic streaming,17 or simply scaling down the dimensions of the acoustofluidic device and increasing the fre- quency to boost the acoustic radiation force.18 Acoustophoresis of bacteria was so far demonstrated on, for example, Salmonella Ty- phimurium,19 Escherichia coli,17,20,21 Enterobacter cloacae,18 and even Legionella pneumophila,22 but in a fixed state and with po- tentially dominating influence of agglutination. The fundamental behaviour of biological cells in a standing acoustic wave was so far characterized for various eukaryotic cells and microorganisms in terms of the acoustic contrast factor Φ that determines the magnitude of the ARF (ARF ∝ Φ) and the loca- tions of stable equilibria of cells in the wave at either the pres- sure nodes (Φ > 0) or pressure antinodes (Φ < 0).9–12 To the best of our knowledge, there are no such characterizations reported for bacteria, apart from some studies qualitatively and indirectly implying a positive acoustic contrast factor of several bacteria in water for Salmonella Typhimurium,19 Escherichia coli,17,20,21 and Enterobacter cloacae.18 We study acoustophoresis of several species of Legionella, E. coli, and P. aeruginosa that are relevant for water safety.1,23 We experimentally determine the acoustic contrast factor of 10 Le- gionella species, specifically L. pneumophila, L. fraseri, L. pascullei, L. anisa, L. cherrii, L. micdadei, L. parisiensis, L. israelensis, L. gor- manii, L. longbeachae, and of Escherichia coli. All of the measured species possess a positive acoustic contrast, leading to the focus- ing into a pressure node of a standing wave. A broad variation in the shape and size across the species, as well as within each species, considerably affects the magnitude of the acoustic radi- ation force, resulting in observable differences in the rate of fo- cusing among the species. Since Legionella are known to infect protozoa, such as Acanthamoeba species,1 and grow within, we also measure the acoustic contrast of A. castellanii in cyst and trophozoite states, co-cultured with L. pneumophila. A. castellanii generally also feature a positive acoustic contrast factor and thus focus into a pressure node. Encysted A. castellanii exhibit a sig- nificantly higher acoustic contrast factor than the trophozoites. To complement experimental observations, we introduce a multi-body dynamics algorithm for investigating the role of the experimentally-observed cell size distribution and cell concentra- tion in the collective acoustophoresis of bacteria. The algorithm is based on Stokesian dynamics for modeling the hydrodynamic in- teractions,24 supplemented by the Brownian motion,25 acoustic radiation forces26 and acoustic interaction forces.27 The simula- tions reveal that an increase in the cell concentration accelerates the rate of acoustic focusing due to hydrodynamic interactions. In the environment (e.g. in water installations), Legionella can enter a so-called viable but non-culturable (VBNC) state, in which they are still dangerous for humans, while also very difficult to detect using standard methods.37 In Fig. 2(c) and Fig. 4(d)- (f), we compare the acoustophoresis among the same strain of L. pneumophila, but exposed to various conditions that trigger VBNC state through starvation: cells conditioned in sterilized tap water, compared to cells conditioned in buffered yeast extract (BYE) and transferred into the sterilized tap water shortly before the exper- iment. Acoustophoresis of amoebae Measurements of the effective acoustic contrast factor Φeff for 14 amoebae are presented in Fig. 5. The amoebae were classi- fied into trophozoites, cysts, and undetermined (amoeba in tran- sition from a trophozoite to a cyst), based on a visual obser- vation of the presence of the cellulose wall that is characteris- tic for cysts.38 The results indicate that the cysts have a higher acoustic contrast factor (Φeff = 0.055 ± 0.018) than the tropho- zoites (Φeff = 0.018 ± 0.003), while the undetermined are in- between (Φeff = 0.029 ± 0.010). This is reasonable also from a theoretical perspective, since the wall of a cyst contains cellu- lose which is denser (ρ ≈ 1.6gcm−3)39 and less compressible (κ ≈ 5 × 10−11 Pa−1)40 than a cell-like material (ρ ≈ 1.1gcm−3 and κ ≈ 3.3 × 10−10 Pa−1 for a red blood cell)9, leading to an increase in the effective density and to a decrease in the effective compressibility of a cell, increasing its Φeff. In our experiments, the cysts with ESR = 6.5 ± 1.5μm) ap- pear marginally smaller than the trophozoites with ESR = 7.0 ± 1.0μm. Since the trajectory of an amoeba in the standing acous- tic wave depends on the factor ΦeffESR2, which amounts to 0.86μm2 for trophozoites and 2.34μm2 for cysts, the rate of fo- cusing of cysts is significantly higher than that of trophozoites. Swimmers in acoustophoresis To contain a bacterium with a given maximal swimming speed of vmax bac within the one-dimensional acoustic trap, the pressure amplitude has to exceed a certain threshold (pmin a ). vmax bac defines a swimming thrust through the balance with the Stokes drag on the bacterium, and balancing the swimming thrust with the ARF gives the approximation for pmin a , namely, pmin a = � 3ηρ0c3 0 πfESR2Φeff vmax bac , (11) resulting in pmin a ≈ 667kPa for E. coli (Φeff = 0.11±0.046, ESR = 0.65 ± 0.047μm) with vmax bac = 25μms−1 44 and f = 3.9MHz, as- suming a spherical shape. In the acoustophoresis experiments, we observed swim- ming motion in several of the bacterial species, namely, L. cherrii (vmax bac = 15.4μms−1), L. dumoffii (vmax bac = 6.1μms−1), L. parisiensis (vmax bac = 14.6μms−1), P. aeruginosa (vmax bac = 40.1μms−1), E. coli (vmax bac = 5.4μms−1), and L. pneumophila conditioned in sterilized tap water for 2 months (vmax bac = 13.6μms−1). In the case of elongated rod-shaped swimmers (e.g. L. dumoffii), the acoustic torque45,46 aligned the swimmers par- allel to the pressure nodal line. Discussion Bacteria are known to have a cell volume-dependent volumetric percentage of biomass,47 which has a density larger than water at 1.35gcm−3 for E. coli.48 This implies that the density of bac- terial cells increases with the decrease in the volume of the cells, which is proportional to ESR3. Since the deviation of the cell density from the density of water increases the acoustic contrast factor, this relation between the cell density and ESR could offer an explanation for the observed dependency of Φeff on the ESR In the future, it would be important to analyze Φeff of Legionella that are grown in amoebae, as the growth process in amoebae has been shown to significantly influence the morphology of the bacteria,49 which could influence the acoustic focusability of Le- gionella. The difference in the acoustic contrast factor of the amoebae in different stages of the encystment cycle could be exploited to acoustically sort the different states of amoebae. This could be realized by combining an on-the-fly analysis of the acoustic con- trast factor of an amoeba, similar to the device used by Wang et al. 50, and a subsequent step that would sort the amoeba into a state-specific outlet based on the readout of the acoustic contrast factor. An applicable acoustic sorting device was already devel- oped, for fluorescent activated sorting of particles.51 Literature suggests that the difference in the size of the two states of A. castellanii should be more significant, for example, Bowers and Korn 38 reported mean diameters of 26.5 ± 0.17μm for trophozoites and 16.2 ± 0.13μm for cysts, but could also be affected by the culturing process.52 The relatively small sizes of the amoebae that were present in our experiments could stem from the co-culturing with L. pneumophila, which are known to destabilize and lyse the host amoebae.1 Some of the destabilized amoebae could be lysed during the sample handling steps and during the pumping of the sample into the BAW device; Mof- fat and Tompkins 53 for example drew a sample through a 27- gauge needle with an inner diameter of 210μm three times to lyse the destabilized amoebae; for comparison, the capillaries used to pump-in the sample into our BAW device have an inner diame- ter of 100μm. During such processes, the larger amoebae would experience higher shear stresses in the narrow sections of the tub- ing and BAW device, and therefore exhibit a higher probability of lysis, explaining the small size of amoebae in our analysis. The theoretically demonstrated acceleration of the acoustic fo- cusing and thus higher apparent Φeff explains some previously re- ported experimental findings. For example, the influence of the E. coli concentration on the feasibility of acoustically trapping them has been observed by Hammarström et al. 15. Similar concentra- tion dependencies of bacteria-sized objects were observed already before,28 but no study so far, to the best of our knowledge, pro- vided a generally-accepted explanation of the underlying mech- anism. Our finding agrees also with a theoretical study in mag- netophoresis, where focusing of paramagnetic particles due to a magnetic field was found to accelerate with an increase in particle concentration.29 We call this phenomenon that stems from the many-body hydrodynamic interactions collective hydrodynamic focusing (CHF). CHF could apply to many existing studies on acoustophoresis of bacteria and bacteria-sized particles that ne- glected multi-body effects.17,18,41–43 The phenomenon could be significantly influenced by the presence of walls in the experimen- tal devices, analogous to the paradoxical difference in sediment- ing particle suspensions of low volumetric concentration that sed- iment faster when they do not fill the whole container, which al- lows the fluid flow to go around the cluster of particles.35 A sim- ilar effect could be caused by local variation in cell concentration or spatial variation of the force field. In addition, specifically to acoustophoresis — the presence of the acoustic microstreaming that can affect the ARF34,54 as well as the AIF55 — could con- tribute. These, so-far unexplored influences diminish the CHF in our system for the measurement of Φeff, which we showed exper- imentally. However, in larger channels and with more complex acoustic modes, past experiments15 already demonstrated the 8 CHF and its potential benefits. In the future, studying CHF for differently-sized particles and with an extended computational model that accounts for the walls56 and other influences could be used to quantify the multi-body effects in past and future exper- imental studies. Furthermore, CHF could be exploited to control and improve methods that rely on the presence of larger func- tionalized57 or non-functionalized15 particles to up-concentrate bacteria. To acoustically focus all the Legionella in an at-line system, including the swimmers, the pressure amplitudes of ≳ 0.7MPa would be required, assuming f = 4MHz and an appropriate fo- cusing length within a device for a given flow rate. In the litera- ture, pa of above 1.5MPa have been reported for similar devices and operating conditions,58 making such an application feasible in the near future. Methods and theory Devices and fabrication The lab-on-a-chip devices, an example of which is shown in Fig. 1, were produced through cleanroom processing and feature a three-layered structure made of silicon and glass. A blank silicon wafer (200±10μm thickness) was first anodically bonded to a glass wafer (500μm thickness). The channel designs with 200μm and 700μm width of the main channel were then patterned on the silicon through photolithog- raphy (resist: S1828/S1818, Shipley, 4000rpm; developer: AZ351B, Microchemi- cals) and then etched with an inductively coupled plasma deep reactive ion etching (ICP-DRIE) machine (Estrellas, Oxford instruments) through the entire depth of the silicon ∼ 200μm. Afterwards, a glass wafer (200μm thickness) was anodically bonded onto the etched silicon-glass wafer. The wafer was then diced into individ- ual 11×50mm chips with a wafer saw (DAD3221, Disco corporation). Fused silica capillaries (164±6μm outer diameter, 100±6μm inner diameter, Molex) were in- serted into the inlets and outlets of the chips and fixed with a two-component glue (5 Minute Epoxy, Devcon). A piezoelectric transducer (PZT) (length×width×thickness = 25 × 1.55 × 1mm for 700μm-channel width device and 20 × 4 × 0.5mm for 200μm-channel width device, Pz26, Meggitt Ferroperm) was glued to each device with a conductive epoxy (H20E, EPO-TEK). PZT was attached to the bottom side of the device that was not facing the microscope lens, in order to allow for the use of a high-magnification (100x) solid immersion lens. Copper cables (0.15mm diame- ter) were connected to the PZT using a conductive silver paste and glued to a device with instant glue for mechanical stability. To fix the devices under a microscope, a chip-holder with the outer dimensions corresponding to a standard microscope glass slide (75mm × 25mm) was designed and laser-cut from acrylic glass (PMMA). Experimental procedure For visual observation of phenomena within the channels of the lab-on-a-chip de- vices, we used a phase-contrast microscope (Leica) with a camera (Leica MC 120 HD) recording at 30 frames per second. To visualize and characterize the acous- tic field inside a device, 36 polystyrene (PS) beads (white 4.97 ± 0.06μm and red 2.21 ±0.03μm, microParticles GmbH) were used. A regular syringe was connected to the inlet capillary of a device, for supplying the particles/cells dispersed in wa- ter, as well as for cleaning the chip in-between experiments. A function generator (JDS-2900, JUNTEK) was used to drive the PZT, monitored through an oscilloscope (UTD2025CL, UNI-T). Devices were additionally characterized by measuring the ad- mittance of the PZT with an impedance analyzer (Sine Phase Z-Check 16777k). Cleaning in-between experiments with different species was performed by a flush- ing sequence of ≥ 0.1mL of 10% sodium hypochlorite solution, ≥ 0.05mL of 70% ethanol solution, and ≥ 0.32mL of sterilized tap water from the same batch as the water used for suspending the cells. The trajectories of PS particles that were used for estimation of the acoustic pres- sure amplitude (pa) in the channel were obtained through a particle tracking ve- locimetry plugin TrackMate 60 in Fiji, 61 and post-processed with a custom Matlab 62 code that fits the theoretical trajectory from eq. (10) to an experimental trajectory, using pa and wavenumber k as the fitting parameters. The trajectories of amoe- bae were obtained the same way as those of PS particles, while an additional cell- segmentation step was performed for bacteria, using iLastik. 63 Lastly, the acoustic contrast factor (Φeff) of cells was determined by using a modified version of the Matlab code that fits the theoretical trajectory to an experimental cell trajectory, using a measured mean equivalent sphere radius (ESR) of individual species as an input parameter and Φeff as the fitting parameter. Depending on the rate of focus- ing, the number of frames for the analysis of trajectories of bacteria was reduced by a factor of 5 to 15, to minimize the processing time. To minimize the influence of multi-body interactions, only sufficiently isolated PS particles and amoebae were considered. The size of individual bacteria species was measured from high magnification (1000x) phase-contrast microscopic images, by measuring the maximum and min- imum Feret diameters in a software package Fiji, and using a methodology intro- duced by Saccà 64 to compute the mean cell volume V , which was used to extract the ESR, specifically, ESR = � 3V 4π �1/3 . Recently, it was shown by Pavlic et al. 34 that for a cell-like material at δ/ESR ≈ 0.7, the viscous contributions to the ARF can reach up to ≈ 10% of the inviscid ARF. For amoebae, the viscous contributions are lower due to the larger size. In our approach, this contribution is implicitly included in the measured Φeff. The inviscid and viscous acoustic interaction force between bacteria are expected to be small compared to the ARF, except in the vicinity of a pressure node. 55 The shape of individual cells, under the condition that it does not deviate con- siderably from a sphere (as for example L. dumoffii), has a negligible viscous and inviscid contribution to the ARF, as long as the actual volume of the cell is taken into account. 34 However, the overall dynamics could be influenced by the shape-, orientation-, and configuration-dependent drag. 35,45 Both these effects are inher- ently accounted for in the measured Φeff, as defined in eq. (9). The correction to the drag coefficient Kyy from eq. (7) due to the presence of the walls is for bacteria-sized particles in our channels below 1%, while for amoebae it could go above 10%. 11 In the measurements, the influence is minimized, since the reduced drag due to the presence of the walls is to a similar degree affecting pa that is mea- sured by tracking the 4.97μm polystyrene particles at the same focal plane. The correction to the ARF due to the presence of the walls is, however, negligible. 66 Multi-body computational model The multi-body computational model is based on the force-torque (FT) Stokesian dynamics algorithm proposed by Durlofsky et al. 24, which computes the velocity of an individual particle based on the positions of all the particles (xxx), their sizes, the properties of the fluid, and the external forces FFF ext acting on the particles. The change in the position of the particles ∆xxx can be computed as 67 ∆xxx = R−1 F U ··· FFF ext∆t + kBT ∇ ∇ ∇ ··· R−1 F U∆t + X X X(∆t), (16) with the time step of the numerical integration ∆t, Boltzmann constant kB, temper- ature T , random displacement due to Brownian motion X X X(∆t), and with the resis- tance matrix RF U that describes the many-body hydrodynamic interactions, as well as the near-field lubrication effects. 24,25,67 We neglect the Brownian motion term in eq. (16) involving ∇ ∇ ∇ ··· R−1 F U, as we are working with relatively low volumetric con- centrations of bacteria. The random displacement X X X(∆t) is the only contribution of the Brownian motion to our multi-body system, and it is computed by enforcing a zero mean value and a covariance given as cov(X X X(∆t),X X X(∆t)) = 2kBT R−1 F U∆t. For external forces, we consider the inviscid acoustic radiation force from equation (3), 26 and the inviscid acoustic interaction forces (AIF) from Silva and Bruus 27. Unless stated differently, we compute and update the many-body hydrodynamic in- teractions (M∞ matrix) every 20∆t, in line with Bossis and Brady 67. The computational domain that we consider has a cross-section of 120μm × 120μm and the width of 200μm, corresponding to the channel width in the ex- perimental device. The number of randomly dispersed particles Np in each simula- tion is determined by the initial cell concentration φc, ranging from Np = 288 for φc = 1 · 108cells/mL to Np = 5760 for φc = 2 · 109cells/mL. The temperature used in simulations is 300K, unless stated otherwise.
