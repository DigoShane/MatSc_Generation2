Ogunbunmi,§ Eli Stavitski,∥ Klaus Attenkofer,∥,¶ Svilen Bobev,§ and Cedomir Petrovic †,‡,∗∗ †Condensed Matter Physics and Materials Science Department, Brookhaven National Laboratory, Upton, New York 11973, USA ‡Materials Science and Chemical Engineering Department, Stony Brook University, Stony Brook, New York 11790, USA §Department of Chemistry and Biochemistry, University of Delaware, Newark, Delaware 19716, USA ∥National Synchrotron Light Source II, Brookhaven National Laboratory, Upton, New York 11973, USA E-mail: yuliu@lanl.gov;petrovic@bnl.gov Abstract We report giant anisotropy in thermoelectric power factor (S2σ) of marcasite structure- type PtSb1.4Sn0.6. PtSb1.4Sn0.6, synthesized using ambient pressure flux growth method upon mixing Sb and Sn on the same atomic site, is a new phase different from both PtSb2 and PtSn2 that crystallize in the cubic Pa ̄3 pyrite and Fm ̄3m fluorite unit cell symmetry, respectively. Giant difference in S2σ for heat flow applied along dif- ferent principal directions of the orthorhombic unit cell mostly stems from anisotropic Seebeck coefficients. 1 Introduction Anisotropy in functional characteristics is rather important in many diverse scientific dis- ciplines including thermoelectricity.1–8 Thermoelectric materials are of interest for power generation without greenhouse gas emissions as they convert heat into electric power and vice versa and are characterized by the figure of merit ZT at temperature T.9 For high figure of merit ZT = (S2σ/κ)T, where S is thermopower, σ and κ are electrical and thermal conductivity, respectively: high thermoelectric power factor (S2σ) and low thermal conduc- tivity κ are important. Whereas anisotropy in S2σ and κ has been used in the past to boost thermoelectric performance, the observed anisotropy was modest, up to about an order of magnitude for heat flow along different principal axes of the crystal structure in known materials.10–13 Marcasites often feature high values of thermopower and thermoelectric power factor; in FeSb2 thermoelectric properties can be further defect-tuned and are somewhat anisotropic.14–17 Here we report giant thermoelectric power factor anisotropy in orthorhombic marcasite-type PtSb1.4Sn0.6. PtSb2 and PtSn2 are known to crystallize in a cubic structure of Pa ̄3 pyrite and Fm ̄3m fluorite unit cell symmetry, respectively.18,19 PtSb2 features large thermopower and enhanced power factor due to the flat band in valence band dispersion.20–22 PtSn2, on the other hand, is a metal with good catalytic performance.23,24 Even though crystallo- graphic unit cell of end members are cubic, orthorhombic marcasite-type crystal structure was detected in the Pt-Sb-Sn ternary phase diagram.25 EXPERIMENTAL Synthesis A large PtSb1.4Sn0.6 single crystal with dimensions ∼ 8×5×3 mm3 was grown by the self-flux technique starting from an intimate mixture of pure elements Pt, Sb, Sn with a molar ratio 2 of 0.15 : 0.425 : 0.425. Starting materials were sealed in an evacuated fused silica tube. The tube was heated to 1150 ◦C over 20 h, kept at 1150 ◦C for 3 h, then slowly cooled to 580 ◦C at a rate of 1 ◦C/h. Structure and Composition Analysis Powder x-ray diffraction (XRD) data were taken with Cu Kα (λ = 1.5418 ̊A) radiation of a Rigaku Miniflex powder diffractometer. Element analysis was performed using energy- dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS) in a JEOL LSM-6500 scanning electron microscope. The chemical composition determined by the SEM-EDS gives Pt : Sb : Sn = 32(1) : 46(2) : 22(2). Single crystal XRD data were collected on crystal mounted on a low background plastic holder using Paratone-N oil under the cold stream of nitrogen gas. Data acquisitions took place on a Bruker APEX-II CCD diffractometer using graphite monochromatized Mo Kα radiation (λ = 0.71073 ̊A). The data collection, data reduction and integration, as well as refinement of the unit cell parameters were carried out using the Bruker software. Semi- empirical absorption correction was applied with the aid of the SADABS software package. The structure was subsequently solved by direct methods and refined on F 2 with the aid of the SHELXL package.26 All atoms were refined with anisotropic displacement parame- ters with scattering factors (neutral atoms) and absorption coefficients.27 X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) was measured at 8-ID beamline of the NSLS II at Brookhaven National Laboratory (BNL) in fluorescence mode. X-ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) and extended x-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) spectra were processed using the Athena software package. The EXAFS signal, χ(k), was weighed by k2 to emphasize the high-energy oscillation and then Fourier-transformed in k range from 2 to 10 ̊A−1 to analyze the data in R space. 3 Thermal Transport and Thermodynamic Measurements Transport, thermal and thermodynamic properties were measured in a quantum design PPMS-9 system. The crystal was oriented and cut into rectangular bars along principal crystallographic axes for four-probe resistivity and thermal transport measurements. Silver paint and thin Au wires were used for contacts in electrical transport measurement. The drive current and frequency were 1 mA and 17 Hz. Hall resistivity ρxy was measured with current along the c-axis and magnetic field along the b-axis direction. Thermal transport was measured by using one-heater-two-thermometer setup. Epoxy and Cu leads were used for contacts in thermal transport measurement. Continuous measuring mode was used with maximum heat power and period set at 50 mW and 1430 s along with the maximum tem- perature rise of 3%. The sample dimensions were measured by an optical microscope Nikon SMZ-800 with 10 μm resolution. (c) Temperature dependence of anisotropy in thermoelectric power factor S2/ρ. 8 Specific Heat and Thermopower The specific heat Cp(T) of PtSb1.4Sn0.6 single crystal [Fig. 4(a)] approaches the value of 3NR at room temperature, where N = 3 is the atomic number in the chemical formula and R = 8.314 J mol−1 K−1 is the gas constant, consistent with the Dulong-Petit law. The low derived electronic specific heat coefficient γ ∼ 1.41(9) mJ/mol-K2 and the Debye temperature applied along all three principal axes of the marcasite structure. The sign of S(T) in the whole temperature range is positive, indicating hole-type carriers. This is consistent with the Hall resistivity analysis. The S(T) is almost linear at high temperature and changes its slope below 50 K. At low temperatures, the diffusive Seebeck response of Fermi liquid dominates and is expected to be linear in temperature. In a single-band system, S(T) is given by S T = ±π2 3 k2 B e N(εF) n ,32,33 where kB is the Boltz- mann constant, N(εF) is the density of states at the Fermi energy εF, and n is the carrier concentration. The electronic specific heat can be expressed as γ = π2 3 k2 BN(εF). Therefore S/T = ±γ/ne, where the units are V K−1 for S, J K−2 m−3 for γ, and m−3 for n, respec- tively. In general, we express γ in J mol−1 K−2. Then we can define a dimensionless quantity q = S T NAe γ , where NA is the Avogadro number, gives the number of carriers per formula unit (proportional to 1/n). The derived S/T from 5 to 30 K is ∼ 0.103(1) and 0.128(2) μV K−2 for a- and b-axis, respectively, and 0.007(1) μV K−2 for c-axis; the calculated q is ∼ 7.0(4) and 8.7(4) for a- and b-axis, respectively, and 0.48(4) for c-axis of the orthorhombic marca- site structure for PtSb1.4Sn0.6. Given the volume of unit cell ∼ 0.139 nm3, we calculate the carrier density per volume n ≈ 1.5(1) × 1022 cm−3 for the c-axis, close to the value obtained from the Hall measurement. 9 Thermoelectric Power Factor Thermoelectric power factor S2σ where σ = 1/ρ is plotted in Fig. 4(c). The S2σ maximum below 300 K for the heat flow applied along a- or b-axis is smaller to what was observed in weakly defective FeSb2 crystals without metal-insulator transition.14,17,34 On the other hand, the maximum appears at room temperature with positive slope. This is far above the temperature of colossal S2σ value for FeSb2 which is observed at 20 - 30 K, and suggests possible higher thermoelectric power factor above room temperature. Furthermore, S2σ is strongly anisotropic: heat flow applied along the c axis results in about two orders of magnitude difference when compared to a or b direction [Fig. 4(c)]. The calculated values of ZT at room temperature are ∼ 1.6×10−3 and 2.0×10−3 for a and b axis, respectively, which are also about two orders of magnitude larger than ∼ 2.6×10−5 for the c axis of PtSb1.4Sn0.6. The absolute values are relatively small for thermoelectric application. The ZT of nominal PtSb2 reaches a maximum ∼ 0.009 between 220 to 250 K, which can be enhanced to ∼ 0.059 - 0.073 around 350 K in samples with 2% Te-doping at Sb sites.35 CONCLUSION In summary, PtSb1.4Sn0.6 single crystals crystallize in orthorhombic marcasite structure even though both PtSb2 and PtSn2 are cubic materials at ambient pressure. The alloying in between PtSb2 and PtSn2 creates not only simple random distribution of atoms but also significant change in bond energies and ethalpies of formation so that a new phase is stabilized for PtSb1.4Sn0.6.36 Most importantly, we observe giant anisotropy in S2σ when the heat flow is applied along a- or b-axis of the crystal structure S2σ is much larger when compared to c-axis. 10 ASSOCIATED CONTENT Accession Codes The corresponding crystallographic information file (CIF) has been deposited with the Cam- bridge Crystallographic Database Centre (CCDC) and can be obtained free of charge via http://www.ccdc.cam.ac.uk/conts/retrieving.html (or from the CCDC, 12 Union Road, Cam- bridge CB2 1EZ, UK; Fax: +44-1223-336033; E-mail: deposit@ccdc.cam.ac.uk - depository number 2181562).
