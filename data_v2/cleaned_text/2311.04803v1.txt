Coherent control of magnetization precession by double-pulse activation of effective fields from magnetoacoustics and demagnetization M. Mattern,1 F.-C. Weber,1 D. Engel,2 C. Korff-Schmising,2 and M. Bargheer1, 3, a) 1)Institut f ̈ur Physik & Astronomie, Universit ̈at Potsdam, 14476 Potsdam, Germany 2)Max-Born-Institut f ̈ur Nichtlineare Optik und Kurzzeitspektroskopie, 12489 Berlin, Germany 3)Helmholtz-Zentrum Berlin f ̈ur Materialien und Energie, 12489 Berlin, Germany (Dated: 9 November 2023) We demonstrate the coherent optical control of magnetization precession in a thin Ni film by a second excitation pulse which amplifies or attenuates the precession induced by a first pulse depending on the fluences of the pump-pulses and the pump-pump delay. This control goes beyond the conventional strategy, where the same mechanism drives the precession in or out-of phase. We balance the magneto-acoustic mechanism driven by quasi-static strain and the shape-anisotropy change triggered by laser-induced demagnetization. These mechanisms tilt the transient effective magnetic field in opposite directions in case of negative magneto-elastic coupling (b1 < 0). While the strain response is linear in the fluence, demagnetization is nonlinear near the Curie temperature, enabling fluence-based control scenarios. Since the discovery of ultrafast demagnetization in thin Ni films1, the field of ultrafast magnetization dynam- ics has evolved from observing ultrafast magnetization dynamics2,3 to controlling the magnetization on ultrafast timescales4–8. The fastest ways to coherently change the magne- tization state is the excitation of precessional motion reported for a wide range of ferro- or ferri-magnetic metals9–11, semiconductors12 and insulators13,14. De- pending on the magnetically ordered material, the pre- cession can be driven by temperature-induced changes of the magnetocrystalline anisotropy10,11, ultrafast de- magnetization affecting the shape anisotropy9,11,15,16 or by both local quasi-static expansion15,17 and propagat- ing strain pulses12,16,18–20 via material-specific magneto- elastic coupling mediated by spin-orbit coupling. In classical coherent control experiments19,21–24 the magnetization precession is tuned by the pump-pump delay in a double-pulse excitation scheme, where both excitations induce the same driving mechanism, which depends linearly on the fluence. Very recently, Shin and co-workers15,17 controlled the precession amplitude and phase via the composition of a NixFe100−x film, which tunes the magneto-elastic coupling b1, the Curie- temperature TC and the saturation magnetization Msat. This changes the dominant driving mechanism from quasi-static expansion to laser-induced demagnetization that counteract in materials with negative magneto- elastic coupling (b1 < 0). In addition, they showed this counterbalance to depend on the fluence due to the non- linear increase of the demagnetization near TC that be- comes the dominant driving mechanism and switches the precession phase by π. The counterbalance of laser-induced demagnetiza- tion and the negative magneto-elastic coupling (b1 < 0) in our 20 nm-thin Ni film strongly depends on the laser- induced temperature rise and the initial sample temper- ature since the demagnetization increases non-linearly near TC. We demonstrate a transition of the domi- nant driving mechanism of the second excitation from magneto-elastic coupling to demagnetization in three sce- narios: The temperature before the second pulse is tuned via the fluence of the first excitation or the pump-pump delay or, alternatively, the fluence of the second pulse de- termines the temperature rise. The change of the domi- nant driving mechanism switches the effect of the second pulse from amplifying to attenuating the precession in- duced by the first pulse, which opens rich opportunities to control the precession by double-pulse excitation. In our all-optical transient polar magneto-optical Kerr experiments (trMOKE) sketched in Fig. 1(a), a 20 nm Ni film embedded within a 2 nm Pt capping and 3 nm Pt and Ta buffer layers is excited by two p-polarized 120 fs-long 800 nm pump-pulses from the substrate-side at normal incidence with tunable pump-pump delay ∆t and indi- vidually adjustable fluences F1 and F2. We probe the transient out-of-plane magnetization of the Ni film by de- tecting the polarization rotation of a 400 nm probe pulse that is reflected at near normal incidence. For further increased fluence the pre- cession is diminished again, such that the amplitude for 5.0 and 0.7 mJ cm−2 become comparable. This non-linear fluence dependence originates from the opposite effect that laser-induced expansion and demag- netization have on the orientation of the effective mag- netic field in materials with negative magneto-elastic coupling17. Since the round-trip time of the strain pulse through the metallic heterostructure of ≈ 5 ps is an order of magnitude shorter than the preces- sion period, the contribution of ηsp becomes negligible20 and the laser-induced demagnetization and quasi-static expansion of Ni remain as driving mechanisms. Due to the negligible in-plane saturation field of μ0Hsat = 5 mT representing the magnetic easy axis, we also neglect any contribution from a magneto-crystalline anisotropy15,16. These mechanisms induce a change of the effective field ∆ ⃗Heff that adds to the effective field before excitation ⃗Heff,0, that determines the initial direction of the mag- netization. In summary, we demonstrated that a transition of the dominant driving mechanism of a second excitation can be achieved by the fluences of both pulses and the pump- pump delay in a double-pulse excitation scheme. Our model and experiment confirm that the demagnetization and quasi-static expansion counteract each other as pre- cession drivers if the magneto-elastic coupling is negative (b1 < 0). The change of the dominant driving mechanism 5 from magneto-elastic coupling to demagnetization occurs at a fluence-dependent compensation temperature Tcomp below the Curie temperature TC. At this point the ef- fect of the second pulse on the precession switches from amplification to attenuation. We demonstrate that this mechanism can be exploited in various ways thanks to the non-linear fluence-dependent demagnetization mech- anism, enabling coherent control beyond the standard superposition of waves.
