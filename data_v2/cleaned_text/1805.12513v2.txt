arXiv:1805.12513v2 [cond-mat.mtrl-sci] 7 Jun 2018 Anomalous heat flow in 8-Pmmn borophene with tilted Dirac cones Parijat Sengupta and Junxia Shi Dept. of Electrical Engineering, University of Illinois, Chicago, IL 60607 USA Yaohua Tan Dept. of Electrical Engineering, University of Virginia, Charlottesville, VA 22904 USA Enrico Bellotti Dept. of Electrical Engineering, Boston University, Boston, MA 02215 USA We analytically establish an anomalous transverse flow of heat in 8-Pmmn borophene, one of the several two-dimensional (2D) allotropes of Boron (B). The dispersion of this allotrope contains a pair of anisotropic and tilted Dirac cones which are gapped by placing the 2D B sheet under an intense circularly-polarized illumination. A gap in the Dirac dispersion leads to a finite Berry curvature and connected anomalous Hall effects. In the case of thermoelectrics, this manifests as a heat current perpendicular to the temperature gradient - the thermal Hall effect. A quantitative calculation of the attendant thermal Hall conductivity reveals dependence on the intrinsic anisotropy and tilt of the Dirac cone. Further, by estimating the longitudinal thermal conductivity using the Weidemann-Franz law, we also outline steps to compute the thermal Hall angle that gauges the generation efficiency of such transverse heat processes. Finally, we touch upon the idea of thermal rectification wherein the direction of flow of the anomalous heat reverses through a simple switch of the polarization of incident light and is of interest in thermal logic circuits. I. Introduction The two-dimensional (2D) boron monolayers known as borophenes1–3 and have been proposed and synthesized in a variety of allotropes. Prominent examples of such 2D boron sheets predicted through first-principles calcu- lation and eventually grown on silver (Ag) substrates are the striped, β12, and χ3 allotropes. Boron, in general, ex- hibits multiple phases leading to the formation of struc- tures such as quasi-planar clusters, cage-like fullerenes, and nanotubes. The existence of multiple allotropes is ex- plained by taking recourse to the electron-deficient char- acter of the atom - it possesses three valence electrons and four orbitals - from which emerges several complex bond- ing patterns to fulfill requirements of crystal stability. In parallel with development of laboratory techniques4 to realize stable-variants of borophenes, a large body of first-principles study5 employing structural search al- gorithms predicted the possibility of highly stable 2D boron sheets of finite thickness. In particular, a free- standing arrangement of two-dimensional boron atoms with a buckled structure (Fig. 1) and an orthorhombic 8-Pmmn symmetry (Pmmn represents the space group 59; 8 denotes the number of atoms in the unit cell) was shown to carry anisotropic and tilted Dirac cones (DCs); it has since been theoretically expanded.6 In this letter, we examine anomalous thermal trans- port when the 8-Pmmn 2D boron allotrope (henceforth, referred to as β-borophene) with DCs is irradiated by a strong optical beam. Before elaborating on the ‘anoma- lous’ part, we set the context by noting that β-borophene with tilted DCs, a new Dirac material in the family of 2D layered structures, holds promise as a thermoelectric ma- terial7 with a thermal conductivity matching monolayer MoS2 and carrying an innate anisotropy, the very fea- ture that endows 2D layered black phosphorus to possess a very high thermoelectric figure-of-merit. In addition to ‘normal’ heat flow, an additional contribution may arise from the non-trivial band topology of β-borophene; the DCs when gapped carry a finite Berry curvature (Ω (k)) helping establish a class of phenomena collectively rec- ognized as anomalous Hall effect (AHE) observable in charge, spin, and heat currents.8 The β-borophene is in- trinsically gapless and thus precludes the occurrence of Ω (k) and the aforementioned set of Hall effects. It is however now well-understood and verified9 that in pris- tine Dirac materials (zero gap) strain-engineering or a suitable choice of substrates can lead to a gapped phase; additionally, photo-irradiation is also known to rearrange electronic states and in a special case known as the off - resonant approximation, a band-gap (∆) opening can be achieved. Here, under the off -resonant condition, we es- timate a photo-induced ∆ in β-borophene and compute the resulting non-zero Ω (k); in conjunction with a tem- perature gradient (∇T ), the Ω (k) sets up a heat current transverse to vector of ∇T . A similar heat flow is ob- served10 when a real-space magnetic field is applied in- stead of Ω (k) - the well-known Rigi-Leduc effect (RLE). As a brief summary of the results presented herein, we note that the thermal Hall conductance rises for a lower photo-induced ∆ and is further enhanced for a Fermi level located in the band gap. In addition, the conduc- tance also shows a monotonic rise (fall) as the anisotropy of β-borophene is augmented (truncated) via a strain-like perturbation. In fact, we outline how both these observa- tions are encapsulated in a general expression of the ther- mal Hall angle that potentially estimates the efficiency of the Ω (k)-aided anomalous heat flow. We imme- diately recognize two key aspects of the plot: 1) The coefficient Lxy 22 is higher for a reduced value of Λ and 2) drops when μ is deep into the valence or conduction band (μ < −∆; μ > ∆); a rise, however, is observed for the condition −∆ < μ < ∆. To understand this behaviour, simply observe that Lxy 22 is computed by estimating the 4 integral in Eq. 12 for the set of occupied conduction and valence bands that are distinguished, inter alia, by op- posite values of Ω (k), the driver of anomalous heat flow. For μ located in the band gap region, the contribution of the valence states to the overall integral is significantly higher vis-‘a-vis its conduction counterparts reflected in a large value of Lxy 22; contrarily for μ positioned either in the conduction or valence region, the contributions are similarly matched but with a difference in sign leading to a cancellation and the observed low values. As rep- resentative numbers that account for the aforementioned cases, we set μ to {0, 0.5} eV and selecting Λ = 1.0 eV , Lxy 22 is approximately {120, 1.2} × k2 B/h, roughly two- orders of difference between them. A lowering of this reduction, however, is possible by tuning the anisotropy parameter, ν = vx/vy; indeed, for μ = 0.3 (0.4) eV , Lxy 22 marked by a solid (broken) curve in the inset in Fig. 3 rises monotonically for a greater degree of anisotropy. A straightforward explanation can be offered by noting the corresponding change in Ω (k) with anisotropy (also see, caption, Fig. 2). The inset also shows a pair of down- ward sloping curves below the origin and symmetric with those above them; these are simply obtained by flipping the polarization of the incident illumination from right- to left-circular, the consequence of which physically man- ifests as a reversal in the direction of the heat current. It is pertinent to remark here that such complete rever- sals of heat flow leads to conceivable applications that exploit the phenomenon of thermal rectification, a prefer- ential direction of heat transport19, particularly desirable in thermal transistors and logic circuits. IV. Final Remarks In closing, we set up an analytic model for the anisotropy-dependent anomalous thermal Hall conduc- tance of a β-borophene sheet placed under a circularly- polarized illuminating beam. The utility of such calcula- tions lie in suitably harnessing the topology of the Fermi surface as a means to control thermoelectric behaviour. In case of β-borophene, the promise of such control ac- quires significance in light of its underlying anisotropic thermal transport. On this note, surface hydrogenation of 2-Pmmn borophene (an allotrope sharing the same space group of 59 with 8-Pmmn borophene but with two atoms per unit cell) showed remarkable changes22 to its 5 structure; a first-principles calculation shows an elonga- tion of the ‘a’ lattice constant (along the x-axis) by ap- proximately 18% over its pristine counterpart, thereby stretching the B-B bond and re-calibrating the elastic force constants. The distortion to bond length and a newer set of phonon curves therefore further accentuates the anisotropy reflected in tangible reworking of the elec- tric and thermal properties. Such atom-wise explorations that let us tap into the virtues of anisotropy remain to be seen for the 8-atom case discussed here. Besides, a more comprehensive description of the interplay between elec- tron and drag-like phonon effects that may stand altered under structural microscopic adjustments may bring to light greater intricacies than understood heretofore. 1 A.
