Enhanced Superconducting Diode Effect due to coexisting Phases Sayan Banerjee1 and Mathias S. Scheurer1 1Institute for Theoretical Physics, University of Innsbruck, Innsbruck A-6020, Austria The superconducting diode effect refers to an asymmetry in the critical supercurrent Jc(ˆn) along opposite directions, Jc(ˆn) ̸= Jc(−ˆn). While the basic symmetry requirements for this effect are known, it is, for junction-free systems, difficult to capture within current theoretical models the large current asymmetries Jc(ˆn)/Jc(−ˆn) recently observed in experiment. We here propose and develop a theory for an enhancement mechanism of the diode effect arising from spontaneous symmetry breaking. We show—both within a phenomenological and a microscopic theory—that there is a coupling of the supercurrent and the underlying symmetry-breaking order parameter. This coupling can enhance the current asymmetry significantly. Our work might not only provide a possible explanation for recent experiments on trilayer graphene but also pave the way for future realizations of the superconducting diode effect with large current asymmetries. Diodes, which are characterized by a highly asym- metric relation between resistance R and current J, R(J) ̸= R(−J), are an integral part of modern-day elec- tronics. A superconductor where the critical current Jc is different along opposite directions, Jc(ˆn) > Jc(−ˆn), can realize a superconducting analogue of a diode in the While asymmetries in superconducting current-voltage relations in low-symmetry superconductors had been ob- served before, see, e.g., [1–3], the potential technological applications of and fundamental scientific questions as- sociated with this superconducting diode effect (SDE) have attracted significant experimental [4–28] and the- oretical [29–58] attention in recent years; this led to a variety of different realizations both in tunnel-junction setups [10–24] and in single junction-free superconduct- ing phases [4–9, 25–28]. Importantly, critical current asymmetries in superconductors require broken time- reversal symmetry (TRS), which can not only be achieved by applying a magnetic field [4–17], a current , or magnetic proximity , but also result from spontaneous TRS-breaking in a junction [18–20] or homogeneous superconductor ; aside from the fundamental interest in the competition of magnetism and superconductivity in a single electron liquid, this might also be useful for integrated designs where exter- nal fields are not practical. Another crucial aspect for applications is the degree of current asymmetry, conve- niently measured by the dimensionless diode efficiency ciency, as close to 1 as possible, is desirable for the system to operate as a superconducting diode without having to fine-tune the current magnitude. In this work, we propose and demonstrate theoretically an enhancement mechanism for the SDE efficiency η, specifically for systems with spontaneously broken TRS. While it is clear by symmetry that the condensation of a time-reversal-odd order parameter can affect the criti- cal supercurrent and induce a SDE, we here demonstrate that the supercurrent also couples back to the underly- ing symmetry-breaking order parameter. If superconduc- tivity and the TRS-breaking order have similar energy scales, this “back action” can be quite large and, as we demonstrate, can enhance η significantly. Although this mechanism is more generally valid and should apply to different models and forms of the time-reversal-odd order parameter, we use a model inspired by graphene moiré systems; this is motivated by where a junction- free sample of twisted trilayer graphene was studied and a zero-field SDE with a particularly large η was observed. Our work, thus, both provides a possible route to explain- ing the large η of and paves the way for the design of zero-field superconducting diodes with large current asymmetries. Phenomenological coupling.—The main result of this work is that the diode-effect efficiency η can be signifi- cantly enhanced by taking into account that the strength of the valley polarization is affected by the supercurrent or, put differently, ΦV entering Ek,q,η in Eq. (2) also de- pends on the center of mass momentum q of the Cooper pairs, ΦV → ΦV (q). Postponing a systematic micro- scopic computation that treats ∆q and ΦV (q) on equal footing, we start with a simpler phenomenological anal- ysis. In this simplified description, we determine ΦV (q) by minimizing the free energy FV ∼ aΦΦ2 V + bΦΦ4 V + ΦV � q with respect to ΦV , where terms involving fifth and higher powers of the order parameters ΦV , ∆q are ne- glected. The first two terms in FV determine the value of ΦV in the absence of superconductivity. Meanwhile, the remaining terms in Eq. (5) capture the key coupling between the momentum of the Cooper pairs and valley polarization. The last term, ∝ α′′, simply describes the fact that increasing (decreasing) the superconducting or- der parameter magnitude suppresses (enhances) valley polarization. The other term with F(q) = −F(−q) de- scribes the fact that superconductivity at finite q breaks TRS and C2z and can, hence, couple to the first (and in general an odd) power of ΦV . Motivated by twisted graphene systems, which exhibit C3z rotational symme- try, we write F(q) = α ˆeε · X(q) + α′ χ(q), (6) where X(q) and χ(q) are real-valued, periodic on the Brillouin zone, odd in q, and transform as a vector and scalar under C3z, respectively; in the limit of small q, it holds X ∼ (qx, qy)T and χ(q) ∼ qx(q2 x−3q2 y), using a con- vention where one of the primitive translation vectors, aj, of the underlying triangular Bravais lattice points along the x direction. For the following calculations, we extend these functions to the Brillouin zone using the leading lat- tice harmonics (see Appendix A). Importantly, the first term in Eq. (6), requires finite strain or nematic order which break C3z symmetry explicitly. These phenomena, which seem to be ubiquitous in graphene moiré systems [61–67], define a preferred in-plane direction, captured by the unit vector ˆeε. For instance, in the case of strain, ˆeε ∝ (εxx − εyy, εxy + εyz)T , where εij are the strain- tensor elements. We note that the coupling would also be present when ΦV is replaced by another order param- eter that itself already breaks both TRS and C3z simul- taneously . The second term in Eq. (6), albeit subleading for small q, is finite regardless of whether C3z is broken. Back action and SDE.—We start with the first term in Eq. (6), i.e., set α′ = α′′ = 0 in FV . As can be seen in Fig. 1(a), finite α now induces a q dependence in ΦV , which breaks C3z symmetry as a result of the preferred direction associated with ˆeε. As we set β = 0 for now, Γq looks virtually C3z symmetric, while larger α will lead to a noticeable asymmetry in Γq as well, see Fig. 1(b). This results from a “back action mechanism”, where a finite supercurrent, which is associated with detuning the center of mass momentum q0 → q + δq0 of the Cooper pairs away from their equilibrium value q0, changes the strength of ΦV ; as increasing (decreasing) ΦV will weaken (strengthen) superconductivity, this, in turn, influences Γq. This can be thought of as the supercurrent analogue of the coupling between the dissipative current and valley polarization observed in graphene moiré systems [69–71]. We note in passing that a sufficiently strong supercurrent can also flip the sign ΦV in our theory. This back action also affects the q dependence of the supercurrent, which allows to enhance the SDE as can be seen in Fig. 1(c). Exactly as in the phenonemological theory, we find that broken C3z symmetry can enhance the SDE and that the current asymmetry first increases with small ΦV (as significant TRS breaking is needed to generate a large current asymmetry) but then eventually decreases with large ΦV (as it destabilizes superconductivity and also weakens the back action). This is also visible in Fig. 4(d), where we plot the SDE efficiency η of the full self-consistent theory (black line) as a function of valley polarization at q = 0 and compare it with the situation without back action (red dashed line); for the latter, we just fix ΦV at the indicated value ΦV (q = 0), indepen- dent of q and solve for superconductivity via Eq. (2). We observe that the back action not only enhances, as be- fore, the maximum value of η that can be achieved, but also increases the efficiency significantly at smaller values of valley polarization. The reason for this amplification is the aforementioned dominance of the α′′-like couplings in the self-consistent theory, which enhance ΦV (q) when suppressing ∆q with finite q, see also Fig. 3(a). So even if we have very small ΦV (q = 0), valley polarization can reach sizable values at non-zero q due to the coupling 5 to the supercurrent, which in turn enhances the diode effect. This is why η reaches finite values in the limit ΦV (q = 0) → 0+ with back action, while it has to vanish linearly with ΦV (q = 0) without it in Fig. 4(d). It shows that the proposed back action mechanism also enhances the typical η which can reach large values without the need of fine-tuning ΦV (q = 0) to its optimal strength. Conclusion.—We have shown, both using a simple free- energy expansion and a self-consistent theory, that the supercurrent can couple to and, hence, affect the valley polarization, ΦV , which in turn can enhance the SDE efficiency significantly, see Fig. 1(d) and Fig. 4(d). Mo- tivated by , this was formulated for a theory ap- plicable to graphene-based systems; however, we expect that the basic mechanism based on coupling of the su- percurrent to a TRS-breaking order parameter is more generally valid for zero-field superconducting diodes.
