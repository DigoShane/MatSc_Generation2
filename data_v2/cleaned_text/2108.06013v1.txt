ABSTRACT: High performance metasurfaces for thermal radiative cooling applications can be identified using computational optimization methods. This work has identified an easy-to-fabricate temperature phase transition VO2 nanowire array laid atop dielectric BaF2 Fabry-Perot cavity-on- metal with total coating thickness of 2 μm. This optimized structure has ability to self-adaptively switch between high reflectance at low temperature to high emissivity at high temperature in the broad thermal infrared spectrum. This design demonstrates exceptional turn-down figure-of-merit compared to previously realized configurations utilizing VO2 metasurfaces and multilayers. The mechanism is achieved with a sub-wavelength nanowire array effective medium that switches between anti-reflecting gradient coating and Fabry-Perot interference. This thin metasurface coating could impact self-cooling of the solar cells, batteries, and electrical devices where risk presents at high temperatures. * Corresponding author: zihao.zhang@unt.edu (R. INTRODUCTION 1 Optical properties of thin films can be manipulated by introducing multilayered nano-patterns 2 or periodic nanoelements that interface with electromagnetic waves from the visible to infrared. 3 Various flavors of engineered nanoscale coatings include sub-diffraction plasmonic gratings , ultra-high reflection high contrast gratings , traditional diffraction gratings , and simple 5 multilayers such as Fabry-Perot quarter-wave layers . Among the considered, these 6 nanoscale metasurfaces are potentially useful in photovoltaic device improvement and thermal 7 emission regulation . Yet the key attribute to be sought after is thermochromic tuning – the 8 ability to change optical properties with engineered surface temperature – which may result in self- 9 cooling amid temperature or environmental changes. Temperature phase change materials, such 10 as vanadium dioxide (VO2) , SmNiO3 , Ti3O5 , and many others can tailor 11 the optical properties when things get heated. 12 VO2 integrated in photonic multilayers and gratings have demonstrated thermal radiative 13 switching A common coating design utilizes the concept of Fabry-Perot cavity 14 interference to obtain low emittance when cool and high emittance at hot . More complex 15 multilayers can be added, for the purpose of daytime radiative cooling, as absorption to solar 16 radiation is minimized compared to the emission in the infrared . Other designs have patterned 17 at-wavelength size scale cylindrical gratings . So far, the turn-down thermal performance 18 of such coatings has been less than anticipated. The optimal thermochromic metastructure needs 19 to be designed by weighting the Planck blackbody distribution at the corresponding wavelengths 20 and temperatures close to the phase transition, as determined by Wien’s displacement law. This 21 work also considers the optimized structure that is easily feasible for nano/micro-fabrication, for 22 a future in large scale-produced flexible radiative cooling blankets. 23 This work proposes a high turn-down grating by computational optimization of VO2 nanowires 24 in a thin film. VO2 has a phase transition temperature at 340 K , where the phonon- 25 electron structure transforms from insulating to metallic at temperatures above this transition 26 temperature . By randomized trials of at-and sub-wavelength deposited VO2 nano/micro- 27 features, a periodic VO2 nanowire array and a solid VO2 monolayer that sandwiches a quarter- 28 wave thick lossless dielectric. Fabricated VO2 nanowires typically of 50-150 nm in diameter were 29 recently used demonstrating the breakdown on the Franz-Wiedemann law in thermoelectricity 30 . These VO2 nanowires were fabricated through vapor transport method, a bottom-up method 31 , and these VO2 nanobeams can be laid and aligned in-plane, compared to the top-down 32 photolithography and etching processes for traditional at-wavelength gratings . This bottom- 33 up method can greatly reduce complexity in fabrication, where minimal coating thickness is 34 needed. 35 We utilize a Monte-Carlo optimization method to obtain the best nanowire array period and 36 width of square cross-section VO2 nanowires . This method is applied to obtain the VO2 37 monolayer as well, which serves as a gradual index anti-reflection layer above the fully-reflecting 38 silver substrate. The broadband reflectance or broadband emissivity turn-down performance is 39 quantified with a turn-down performance figure-of-merit (FOM), and compared with other VO2 40 phase transition passive thermal radiation self-cooling coatings. The proposed structure has a total 41 thickness of about 2.5 μm, including the 500 nm silver substrate. In this design, the coating on a 42 flexible polymer backing sheet can be applied to alleviate the loss in efficiency for solar cells, 43 whose temperatures can be as high as 80°C in summers under highly irradiative climates, such as 44 Texas. The flexible coating can be applied to one or both sides of a vacuum gap between a solar 45 cell housing and the roof. Similarly, it can be utilized for cooling compact processor components 46 that gets as warm as 80 to 100°C. Other usages include thermal management for lithium-ion cells 47 casings, passive-switched spacecraft insulation blankets, and other common Earth-temperature 48 thermal regulation applications. 49 2. COMPUTATIONAL METHODS 50 2.1. Theory of Optical Coatings 51 In this paper, Rigorous Coupled-Wave Analysis (RCWA), a well-established multilayered 52 diffraction method to solve Maxwell equations , is utilized to calculate the optical radiative 53 properties of the optimized periodic coatings. The materials and geometrical parameters of the 54 proposed coating is shown in Figure 1: The grating has a period of Λ, nanowire width of w, 55 nanowire height of h, distance between nanowires b, thickness of the lossless dielectric is dD, and 56 thickness of the VO2 sub-monolayer is dVO2. Initially, the nanowire aspect ratio can be non-square 57 to demonstrate possible effects. 58 In designing toward desirable high reflection or high emission properties, selection of optical 59 parameters is important in tailoring its optical parameters. The contrast of the dielectric layers 60 helps construct an anti-reflecting coating, where each transparent layer satisfies the geometric 61 mean of the surround layers, such that 1 0 2 n n n  . The underlying totally reflecting metal 62 substrate can vary among aluminum, silver, gold, and others that are opaque and reasonably 63 produced at sub-micron thickness. The anti-reflecting layer thicknesses must also satisfy quarter- 64 wave law, such that h = λ/4n. This quarter-wave method is widely used to determine broadband 65 reflectance at certain wavelength range known as Bragg reflector, where high- and low- refractive 66 index materials are stacked into multilayers with quarter-wave thickness . On the other hand, 67 Fabry-Perot resonance are formed by constructive interference of electromagnetic waves within 68 the dielectric spacer sandwiched by metallic thin films. Ideally, these metallic boundaries are 69 infinitesimally-thin and lossless. This produces perfect absorptivity or emissivity, and perfect 70 transmissivity at moded frequencies . The switching between one to the other mechanism is 71 how our turn-down capability is designed. In addition, for periodic features at the sub-wavelength, 72 such that Λ << λ, effective medium theory (EMT) can be applied to approximate the optical 73 properties of gratings as expressed by a single layer composed of a filling ratio of dielectric 74 functions . 75 2.2. Our Monte-Carlo optimization 366 method was utilized to obtain the optimal structure parameters for VO2 components. It is compared 367 with Fabry-Perot multilayers using VO2, and a higher figure of merit (FOM) of 1.47 is obtained 368 compared to other structures (FOM < 1). Ideally, the optimal refractive index of the dielectric 369 spacer is n = 2.2, which is slightly higher than BaF2 (n = 1.55). The electric field is reflected at the 370 interface between VO2 bottom layer and silver for lower temperature for high reflectance, but a 371 high value of power dissipation density (PDD) is observed within the VO2 in higher temperature, 372 leading to high emissivity in infrared region.
