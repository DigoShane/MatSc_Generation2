However, many emission lines such as Ar-I, Ne-I, and Ne-II have closely spaced doublet emission lines, which result in superimposed replica on the measured ARPES spectra. Here, we present a simple method for sub- tracting the contribution of these doublet emission lines from photoemission spectra. Benchmarking against ARPES spectra of well-characterized 2D materials, we demonstrate that this algorithm man- ages to subtract the doublet signal and reproduce the key features of the monochromated He-Iα spectra in a physically sound manner that reliably reproduces quantifiable dispersion relations and quasiparticle lifetimes. INTRODUCTION Angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES) is a powerful tool in modern experimental condensed matter physics for characterizing the electronic structure of quantum materials [1–3]. A key component of any ARPES system is the light source, the characteristics of which determine much of the system’s experimental ca- pabilities. The beam profile, photon polarization, and in- cident flux of the photon source determines the effective spatial resolution, orbital sensitivity, and photoemission signal intensity, respectively. Meanwhile, the photon en- ergy hν serves as a critical parameter in determining the probed out-of-plane momentum k⊥. It is for this reason that high-intensity, continuously tunable synchrotron ra- diation sources are strongly preferred for band mapping of 3D materials. In contrast, lab-based ARPES systems frequently rely on noble gas discharge lamps which pro- duce a series of intense narrow emission lines at discrete photon energies depending on the gas plasma used. The most common emission lines are He-Iα at 21.2 eV and He-IIα at 40.8 eV. Other gases can also be used to generate useful dis- charge lines at 8.4 eV (Xe I) , 10 eV (Kr-I) , 11.8 fortunately, many of these discharge spectra include sec- ondary “doublet” lines of comparable intensity and with narrow energy spacings less than the 1.5 eV that a typical lab-based vacuum ultraviolet (VUV) monochro- mator is able to effectively filter, resulting in a shifted, overlapping signal in the measured photoemission spec- tra. ∗ yt493@cornell.edu † bdf53@cornell.edu The challenge of using such doublet emission lines for ARPES experiments is illustrated in Fig. 1. Our ex- perimental system consists of a high resolution hemi- spherical electron analyzer (Scienta Omicron DA30-L), a toroidal monochromator (Scienta Omicron VUV 5040), and a multi-gas RF discharge lamp (Fermion Instruments BL1200s). In Fig. 1(a), we show VUV discharge spectra measured with this system from a polycrystalline gold reference sample using Kr, Ar, He, and Ne plasma. The peak intensity profiles shown are generated by taking the derivative of an angle-integrated Fermi edge collected at 6 K. The presence of this overlapping doublet signal is highly problematic, particularly when attempting to extract quantitative pa- rameters such as dispersion relations and quasiparticle lifetimes from ARPES spectra. Consequently, there is a need for a method to eliminate the β line contribution from ARPES spectra collected using doublet emission sources. II. SUBTRACTING THE DOUBLET PHOTOEMISSION SIGNAL In a conventional photoemission experiment using a monochromatic photon source with energy hν, the rela- tionship between the measured kinetic energy ω of the outgoing electrons and the original binding energy EB is ω = hν − φA − EB, (1) where φA is the known work function of the electron analyzer. When a doublet photon source is used, the measured photoemission signal Itot becomes a superpo- sition of the emission from both lines, such that Itot(k, ω) = α(k, ω) + β(k, ω), (2) where α(k, ω) and β(k, ω) are the α and β signal’s contribution to the total intensity at the measured kinetic energy ω and momentum k. We now consider a simplified scenario, in which the measured spectral intensity scales directly with the pho- ton flux for a given discharge line, but that the binding- energy dependent lineshapes are identical. In this situa- tion, the overall signal Itot(ω) at a fixed k can be written as (3) where R is simply the fractional intensity ratio of the β photon flux to that of the more intense α line, and ∆E = hνα - hνβ is the doublet energy separation. A solution for the isolated α(ω) signal can be deter- mined algorithmically from raw ARPES spectra using the form of Eq. 3 so long as ∆E and R are known accurately. We show this process diagrammatically in Fig. 2. We start by accounting for background above the Fermi level as well as detector non-linearity in the ARPES spectra and extract their energy distribution curves (EDCs). ∆E is determined using a Fermi edge fit [Fig. 2(a)]. ∆E is a property of the chosen noble gas discharge line and does not change between experiments. In the case of Fig. 2, Ne-I has a ∆E of 176 meV. Next, we define a sub-range of the original EDC in a small window [EF − ∆E 2 , EF + ∆E 2 ] straddling the Fermi level of the α signal [Fig. 2(b)]. Within this energy range the contribution of the β signal is negligible. BENCHMARKING PERFORMANCE In order to characterize the performance of our al- gorithm, we performed ARPES measurements on the misfit layered compound (LaSe)1.14(NbSe2)2, which has strong two-dimensional character and is therefore ideal for comparing measured spectra at different photon en- ergies . Single crystal samples were cleaved under good UHV conditions (< 6 x 10-11 Torr) and measured at a temperature of 6 K with a nominal instrumental energy resolution of 6 meV. In Fig. 3, we compare ARPES measurements of (LaSe)1.14(NbSe2)2 using both monochromated and dou- blet emission sources to benchmark the efficacy of our subtraction algorithm. Fig. 3(a) shows the Fermi surface as measured with He-I, which consists of hole-like pockets at K and at Γ consistent with expectation for a heavily electron-doped NbSe2 surface layer as observed in pre- vious studies . Fig. 3(b) shows a high-statistics energy-momentum image along the Γ-M high symmetry direction of the NbSe2 surface taken also with He-Iα ra- diation, showing a pair of well-isolated dispersive bands crossing the Fermi level, arising from Nb 4d states of the hole-like pocket centered on Γ. The performance of our algorithm can be bench- marked directly by comparing processed ARPES images taken using doublet emission lines against this baseline monochromated He-I spectra. In Figures 3(c,e) we show raw ARPES spectra along the same momentum cut and identical measurement geometry as the He-I spectra of Fig. 3(b), but taken with Ne-I and Ne-II emission, re- spectively. DISCUSSION It is worth noting that the simplified description of the doublet photoemission signal presented in Section II assumes that the binding-energy dependent EDC line- shapes of both the α and β signals are identical. In prin- ciple, however, the k position being sampled along an EDC on an ARPES image collected with a hemispheri- cal analyzer varies with the kinetic energy in a fashion dependent on the exact measurement geometry . A substantial variation in measured k between doublet line signals can in turn lead to differences in the intrin- sic EDC lineshapes for the α and β signals in the case of highly dispersive band features. To evaluate the im- portance of this effect, we consider first the measured out-of-plane photoelectron momentum, given by k⊥ = � 2me/ħ2(V0 + Ek cos2(θ))1/2, (7) where θ is the emission angle relative to normal and V0 is the inner potential (typically ∼10 eV). If we consider doublet lines of energy separation ∆E = 0.2 eV at normal emission, the absolute difference in k⊥ is ∼ 0.01 �A −1, which for a typical perovskite lattice constant of a = 4�A is only ∼ 0.5% of the Brillouin zone. The in-plane momentum is k∥ = 1 ħ � 2meEk sin(θ). (8) In the case of k∥, assuming a typical emission angle of θ = 25◦, the absolute difference in k∥ is 0.005 �A −1, less than 0.3% of a typical perovskite Brillouin zone. The difference in sampled k for a typical doublet line is therefore negligible relative to the Brillouin zone, justify- ing our earlier treatment. Similarly, we would anticipate that differences due to photoemission matrix element ef- fects would also be minimal for the small photon energy differences observed here. V. CONCLUSION We have created a simple method for effectively sub- tracting the undesired doublet signal’s contribution to ARPES spectra in a way that is mathematically unique and insensitive to noise. This method is advantageous in that the process is more or less universal for any in- ert gas with higher energy doublet lines as it bypasses 6 the need for specific VUV filters. Our quantitative anal- ysis of this method’s performance on the non-dispersive misfit compound (LaSe)1.14(NbSe2)2 demonstrates its vi- ability with regards to the determination of dispersion relations and quasiparticle lifetimes, particularly in 2D and weakly-dispersive non-2D systems. As having access to more photon energies is useful in general, labs with ready access to such multi-gas discharge lamps may ben- efit from this method at no additional expense. Our ap- proach therefore unlocks numerous doublet emission lines for lab-based ARPES studies on quantum materials. Appendix A: Measurement of Source Emission Lines The plasma discharge spectra shown in Fig. 1 were gen- erated via angle-integrated photoemission measurements on a reference polycrystalline gold film, using the iden- tical experimental setup and measurement conditions as for the ARPES data presented throughout the main text. The gold reference sample was generated by e-beam de- position and cleaned via vaccum annealing before being transferred to the ARPES cryostat. For photoemission measurements, the cleaned Au film was held at a temper- ature of 6K under good UHV conditions (< 7.5 x 10-11 Torr), with the electron analyzer set to a nominal energy resolution of 10 meV using He, Kr, Ar, and Ne gases to generate discharge emission. In each case, raw integrated EDCs for each noble gas line were obtained, correspond- ing to the red curves on Fig. A.1. Ar-I, Ne-I, and Ne-II are colored in purple, green, and blue respectively. These curves were then offset by the work function of our an- alyzer, 4.32 eV, to give photon energies hν = Ek + Φ. Taking the negative of the derivative for the integrated EDCs then produces the relative intensity and shape of the various strong emission lines after convolution with the detector energy resolution, as presented in Fig. 1. We have chosen a cubic polynomial fit to avoid compounding noise when taking derivatives. Appendix C: Sensitivity to Noise Here we show that our algorithm performs well even on low ARPES statistics. Using the same data from the (LaSe)1.14(NbSe2)2, but using 10% and 100% ARPES statistics, allows us to probe our algorithm’s sensitivity to noise. In Fig. C.1(c) and (d), we show that the out- put spectra for 10% and 100% statistics are qualitatively identical. Further examination of the integrated EDCs [Fig. C.1(e)] also show the same quasiparticle peak and line shape. There is no kink in either the low or high statistics EDC at ∆E = 90 meV below the Fermi level corresponding to the doublet energy separation of Ne-II. Additionally, the two optimal R values determined using the method in Appendix B exhibit a fractional difference of under 3%, which attests to the algorithm’s consistency even in the presence of worse signal-to-noise ratios.
