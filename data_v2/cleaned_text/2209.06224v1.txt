This raises the question for the relevance of mesoscale patterns near zero temperature phase transitions, also known as quantum phase transitions (QPTs). Here we report the magnetic susceptibility of LiHoF4 – a dipolar Ising ferromagnet – near a well-understood transverse-field quantum critical point (TF-QCP)3,4. When tilting the magnetic field away from the hard axis such that the Ising symmetry is always broken, a line of well-defined phase transitions emerges from the TF-QCP characteristic of an additional symmetry breaking, in stark contrast to a crossover expected microscopically. We show that a continuous suppression of ferromagnetic domains, representing a breaking of translation symmetry on mesoscopic scales in an envi- ronment of broken magnetic Ising symmetry on microscopic scales, is in excellent qualitative and quantitative agreement with the field- and temperature dependence of the susceptibility and the magnetic phase diagram of LiHoF4 under tilted field. This identifies a new type of phase transition that may be referred to as mesocale quantum criticality, which emanates from the text-book example of a microscopic ferromagnetic TF-QCP. Our results establish the surroundings of QPTs as a regime of mesoscale pattern formation, where non-analytical quantum dynamics and materials properties without classical analogue may be expected. 2 In recent decades a plethora of new phenomena reflecting strong quantum correlations have been discovered5–9. Remarkably, many of them appear to involve mesoscale patterns, with key exam- ples including the interplay of ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic domains10, the condensation of topological defects in valence bond solids6, or the formation of spin-density wave order at ferromagnetic quantum criticality11,12. Yet, starting purely from microscopic considerations, an account of mesoscale textures represents a major challenge. Moreover, such textures are typi- cally described at a classical level. To address the emergence of textures in the presence of well- understood quantum fluctuations we studied the arguably simplest example of a quantum phase transition9, namely the response of an insulating easy-axis ferromagnet to a magnetic field, B, ap- plied transverse to the preferred magnetization axis. This transverse-field quantum magnetism is of broad interest, as it allows to benchmark the entanglement of complex spin systems, the tunnelling of single moments and domain walls, quantum annealing, and Rabi oscillations4. Moreover, it is well known that the dipolar stray fields drive the formation of magnetic domains.13–16 In order to identify tractable evidence of magnetic domains in the quantum regime near a TF-QCP we used a symmetry-breaking field to disentangle spontaneous symmetry breaking and domain formation. I. MAGNETIC PHASE DIAGRAM OF LIHOF4 The most extensively studied material with a ferromagnetic TF-QCP is LiHoF4. Measurements of the ac susceptibility, the magnetization, and inelastic neutron-scattering established dipolar Ising ferromagnetism with a TF-QCP at Bc = 5.1 T.3,17,18 In its ordered state, LiHoF4 forms simple do- main patterns13–16,19,20, with branching confined to the surface of bulk samples14,15,21 and domain walls without roughening22. Illustrated in Figs. 1 a through 1 d is the well-established microscopic scenario of the TF-QCP in LiHoF4, which, however, ignores magnetic domains. Easy-axis ferro- magnetic order is suppressed for perfectly transverse field. Under a tilted field (φ � 0) a crossover emanates from the TF-QCP at B∗, because the longitudinal field-component breaks the Ising sym- metry between up and down states, hence preempting spontaneous symmetry breaking. Shown in Figs. 1 e through 1 h are the main results of our study including magnetic domains. For φ = 0 the microscopic TF-QCP coincides with the transition from the multi-domain to the single-domain state. However, under tilted fields (φ � 0) the multi-domain state survives under increasing field up to a sharp second-order phase transition at which the minority domains vanish continuously, even though the underlying properties on atomic scales undergo a crossover only. 3 Hence, a line of quantum critical transitions (QCTs) that are purely due to magnetic domains (cf mesoscale QCTs) emanates from a microscopic TF-QCP. Under increasing |φ|, the transition field, Bc, decreases rapidly, because the field component along the easy axis favors a uniform magne- tization that destabilizes the multi-domain configuration. The qualitative difference between the purely microscopic scenario and that accounting additionally for magnetic domains, as illustrated in Figs. 1 d and 1 h, respectively, is corroborated by the quantitative agreement between experi- ment and theory. II. MICROSCOPIC SETTING Summarized in Fig. 2 are key characteristics of the dipolar Ising ferromagnetism in LiHoF4. In the tetragonal crystal structure [Fig. 2 a, lattice constants a = b = 5.175 Å, c = 10.75 Å, space group C6 4h (I41/a)], the crystal electric field (CEF) at the location of the Ho3+ ions exhibits an S 4 symmetry that originates in the surrounding F− ions. The CEF lifts the 17-fold degeneracy of the 5I8 configuration, such that the ground state is a non-Kramers doublet. This doublet displays a magnetic moment along the crystallographic c axis only, representing the easy magnetic axis, without appreciable magnetic anisotropy in the ab plane. In our study the magnetic field was ap- plied in the ac-plane as denoted by the angle φ, Fig. 2 b. The magnetic coupling between the Ho3+ ions consists of long-range dipole-dipole interactions and weak exchange contributions, resulting in ferromagnetism below TC = 1.53 K. As a function of transverse field (φ = 0), the lowest CEF doublet splits with a quadratic as op- posed to linear field dependence due to its non-Kramers nature, Fig. 2 c and 2 d. For φ = 0 this non-linear splitting competes with the dipolar interactions, suppressing ferromagnetism at Bc. In addition, LiHoF4 exhibits also a sizeable hyperfine coupling to the nuclear spins of the Ho ions that enhances the magnetic order at low temperatures and causes a point of inflection in the field– temperature phase boundary3. In turn, the coupled electronic and nuclear modes soften at the TF-QCP3,17,18,23,24. For the interplay of the microscopic TF-QCP with magnetic domains, the non- Kramers nature of the Ho3+ ions in combination with the hyperfine coupling cause a strong angle dependence providing a very sensitive test for theory, as we argue below. 4 III. EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS We inferred the phase boundaries from the real and imaginary parts of the ac susceptibility, χ′ and χ′′, as measured along the easy axis (Methods and Extended Data Fig. 1). The susceptibility for various angles φ of the static field B relative to the hard axis are shown in Figs. 3 a,b and 3 c,d for T = 1.2 K and 62 mK, respectively. Further data are presented in Extended Data Fig. 2 and Extended Data Table 1. The definition of Bc is shown in Extended Data Fig. 3. For what follows, we note that χ in the ordered state is dominated by the presence of ferromagnetic domains25–27, while it is dominated by the microscopic response in the disordered regime. Starting at 1.2 K and φ = 0, i.e., perfectly transverse field, χ′ is essentially constant up to Bc(T = 1.2 K) = 3.2 T and decreases in the paramagnetic state, Fig. 3 a. Analyzing the field dependence of χ′ for B > Bc according to literature we find excellent agreement with the mean-field critical exponent γ = 1.02 ± 0.051 reported before3 (Extended Data Fig. 4). Under tilted fields, φ � 0, we observe a well-developed phase transition, which contrasts the crossover predicted. Namely, χ′ remains constant up to a sharp drop at a transition field Bc. In addition χ′ no longer follows a power-law dependence above Bc (not shown) and develops a broad maximum for large φ signalling a crossover in the disordered state. With increasing φ the transition field Bc(φ) decreases rapidly. The emergence of a sharp drop of χ′ across Bc for φ � 0 strongly suggests that the nature of the transition changes immediately upon tilting the field, providing evidence of the different dominant contributions to χ′ in the ordered and disordered states. Further, for fields up to Bc and small φ, the imaginary part of the susceptibility, χ′′, is finite, with a high value at low fields and a faint maximum at Bc. The dissipative processes underlying χ′′ are attributed to the motion of domain walls in the ab-plane in response to the small ac field along the easy axis3,25. Finally, as the field direction approaches the easy axis for φ → 90◦ (Fig. 3 a), χ′ is characteristic of a ferromagnet probed along its easy axis. In this limit the abrupt drop of χ′ corresponds to the coercive field. Consistently, χ′′ at small fields increases with increasing φ and is very large up to the coercive field for φ → 90◦. For φ = 0 the transition field increases for decreasing temperature and limits to Bc = 5.1 T in excellent agreement with the literature3 (Fig. 3 c for 62 mK, Fig. 3 e to 3 i, Fig. 4 e, Extended Data Fig. 5 a). While the field dependence across Bc at 62 mK and φ = 0 is qualitatively unchanged and characteristic of a continuous mean-field transition, χ′ becomes vanishingly small below Bfr ∼ 2 T. Associated with the drop in χ′ is a pronounced maximum in χ”, characteristic of an increase of 5 dissipation. The behaviour of χ′ and χ” around Bfr can be attributed to a freezing of the domain- wall motion at low temperatures and low fields (Extended Data Fig. 4). As shown in Fig. 3 d both χ′ and χ′′ at 62 mK display the same qualitative evolution across the transition at Bc as observed at higher temperatures under angles φ up to ±15◦. Since Bc is smaller than Bfr for φ ≳ 15◦, the susceptibility essentially vanishes suggesting that the domain walls no longer move. In turn, it is not possible to track the transition for φ exceeding ±15◦. The magnetic phase diagrams up to φ = 15◦ are shown in Figs. 3 e to 3 i (Extended Data Fig. 5 a). The color shading depicting χ′ underscores the location of Bc and the regime of the frozen response at low fields. With increasing φ the enhancement of Bc below ∼ 0.8 K attributed to the hyperfine coupling for φ = 03 disappears, such that the phase boundary at φ = 10◦ is flat as T → 0. In addition, an expanded view of Bc at small fields, shown in Extended Data Fig. 5 b, displays a small regime suggestive of a reentrant magnetic field dependence in the vicinity of Tc(B = 0). This might be consistent with fluctuations near Tc beyond mean-field behaviour that are quenched under an applied magnetic field. In the low-temperature limit, Bc decreases rapidly as a function of φ, Fig. 3 j. To access the maximum of Bc requires an alignment better ±0.1◦. This sensitivity, which highlights the need to study spherical samples to ensure homogeneous demagnetizing fields, is corroborated by the jump of the susceptibility, ∆χ, at Bc , Fig. 3 k. IV. THEORETICAL MODELLING Our main findings under tilted fields to be explained theoretically are: First, the onset of magnetic order displays the characteristics of a well-defined phase transition in χ′ and χ′′, in stark contrast to the crossover expected in a purely microscopic scenario. Second, the transition immediately changes its character for φ � 0 and exhibits a jump in χ′ with a broad maximum above Bc and dissipation below Bc seen in χ′′. Third, Bc decreases rapidly with increasing φ. Fourth, the tem- perature dependence of Bc changes qualitatively when φ exceeds a few degrees, where the point of inflection and increase of Bc for T → 0 disappears. The sharp phase transition we observe for φ � 0 suggests spontaneous symmetry breaking. Be- cause the spin symmetry of the microscopic Hamiltonian for finite field is already fully broken18 (Extended Data Fig. 6), an obvious candidate is translation symmetry breaking due to domain formation13–16,19,20. This hypothesis is strongly supported by the smooth angle dependence and the qualitative similarity of χ up to φ = 90◦ (Fig. 3 a). Going beyond previous modelling, we combine 6 a microscopic description in tilted fields with a domain treatment, explicitly keeping domain vol- umes as variational parameters (Supplementary Notes S1 and S2). The non-Kramers ground state implies that the complete CEF scheme must be retained for the correct quantitative account3,17, because both the magnetization and the applied field for φ � 0 feature components along the easy and the hard axes. A description of the Ho3+ moment in terms of an effective spin 1/218,28,29 proves to be insufficient. For our analysis, we developed a combined and consistent account of the microscopic interactions, the mesoscopic domain energy terms, and the backaction of the stray-field on the microscopic expectation values. We start from a microscopic model of Ho3+ ions taking into account all CEF levels, the hyperfine coupling to the Ho nuclear spin, and an inter-site interaction (Methods and Supplementary Information Note S3). Due to its long-range character, the dipolar coupling may be approximated by a Heisenberg interaction and decoupled in a mean-field fashion, while all local terms are treated exactly (Supplementary Note S4). A large body of experimental studies in bulk samples of LiHoF4 and related 3D Ising ferromagnets showed that the domains are well-described by an alternating up- down-pattern without roughen- ing in the ordered state19,30 or at the critical point31,32. To take into account the domain structure, we assumed an alternating pattern of up and down domains, with their volume ratio, v, as a varia- tional parameter (Supplementary Notes S5 and S6). Assuming a homogeneous magnetization density in each domain, we combined the microscopic interactions and the stray fields into a consistent mean-field description of the multi-domain ferro- magnet (Supplementary Note S7). To account for the back-action by the domains, we expressed the sum of the stray-field and the domain-wall energies as a bilinear product of expectation values of magnetic moments, ̄J1,2, in the domain types 1, 2, Edom = M � α � cα 1 ̄Jα 1 ̄Jα 1 + cα 2 ̄Jα 2 ̄Jα 2 + cα 12 ̄Jα 1 ̄Jα 2 � , with α = x, y, z and M the total number of moments. The last term, with cα 12 > 0, represents an ef- fective antiferromagnetic interaction between domains which drives the multi-domain state in the vicinity of a TF-QCP. This may be viewed as emergent mesoscale antiferromagnetism, although the experimental domain pattern is not strictly periodic. In a first assessment we confirmed that the agreement between theory and experiment is not sensitive to the precise choice of the domain structure in the model (Extended Data Fig. 7, Supplementary Note S8). We finally solved the model numerically (Supplementary Note S9). 7 V. MESOSCALE QUANTUM CRITICALITY For B < Bc and φ = 0 the up and down domains occupy equal volumes, v∗ = 1/2, and the transition from a spontaneous multi-domain to a single-domain state coincides with the microscopic TF- QCP, Fig. 4 a. This microscopic TF-QCP drives the disappearance of domains above Bc(φ = 0). For φ � 0, however, v∗ decreases and vanishes continuously at Bc(φ � 0) characteristic of a second-order phase transition, Fig. 4 b (Extended Data Fig. 8, Supplementary Note S9.1). In the magnetization parallel to the field, JB, the suppression of minority domains results in a kink at Bc, which gets more pronounced with increasing φ, Fig. 4 c. Interestingly, χzz drops discontinuously at Bc for φ � 0 despite of the second-order nature of the transition, because it is dominated by magnetic domains below Bc and the microscopic response above Bc, as emphasized above Fig. 4 d. The calculated field dependence of χzz, up to a single scaling parameter, is in excellent quantitative agreement with experiment (Fig. 4 d) including ∆χ′ (Fig. 3 k) and the broad maximum above Bc for large φ, where the latter represents a crossover similar to that at B∗ in the purely microscopic model. Summarized in Fig. 4 e is the calculated phase diagram for various φ. The strong angle-dependence of Bc (Figs. 1 h and 3 k) originates in the combination of the non-Kramers ground state and the hyperfine coupling (Extended Data Fig. 9, Supplementary Note S9.2). For φ = 0 the magnitude of the moment, |J|, is large and parallel to the z axis for B = 0, but displays a sharp cusp-like minimum at Bc because the field-induced x component is small. Under tilted fields the minimum of |J| becomes less pronounced as it retains a finite z component above Bc. The energy gain due to the hyperfine coupling, which scales with |J|, consequently favors the ferromagnetic phase and thus enhances Bc strongly, however, for φ ≲ 5◦ only. The calculated phase diagram for a conventional moment with J = 8 without CEF term, underscores this observation. While the nature of the transition for φ � 0 remains unchanged, changes of Bc as a function of φ are significantly weaker and the hyperfine coupling influences Bc only marginally, because |J| is now independent of the field, and the hyperfine coupling no longer favors a particular moment direction. Taken together, we identify two immanently different though closely connected forms of field- driven quantum criticality. For φ = 0, dipolar ferromagnetism terminates in a well-understood microscopic Ising TF-QCP. Due to the long-ranged nature of the dipolar interactions this transition is mean-field with critical exponents α = 0, β = 1/2, γ = 1, δ = 3. However, the divergence of the correlation length on the ordered side of the TF-QCP will be ultimately limited by the domain 8 size. In contrast, for φ � 0 the transition involves magnetic domains only, where the continuous suppression of the domain ratio v∗ (Fig. 4 b) drives a mesoscale QCP. This transition is highly unusual as it is dominated by the long-ranged character of the stray fields. Formally, one may assign critical exponents α = 0, β = 1, γ = 0, δ = 1, where β = 1 reflects the linear behavior of v∗ near Bc and γ = 0 is consistent with the jump in χzz. VI. CONCLUDING REMARKS Our study benefits from the simplicity of 3D Ising ferromagnetic order with long-range dipolar interactions, for which mean-field behaviour is expected and observed (Extended Data Fig. 4 and ) with additional logarithmic corrections in the magnetization and susceptibility at the upper critical dimension.26,33 Likewise, we find the phase diagram of LiHoF4 to be quantitatively consis- tent with mean–field theory. Yet, there are several regimes where fluctuations beyond mean-field must be anticipated, for instance, when the size of the minority domains approaches microscopic distances near Bc, or near the reentrant phase boundary at small φ (cf. Extended Data Fig. 5 b). This motivates the development of new methods to study mesoscale texture in near quantum criti- cality and quantum critical mode softening of carefully tailored mesoscale textures in the future. Without defect-related pinning the character of the mesoscale quantum phase transitions we ob- serve under tilted fields remains qualitatively unchanged up to φ = ±90◦. They connect, hence, continuously with the coercive field along the easy axis, suggesting that pure anisotropic ferro- magnets display mesoscale QCPs generically. In turn, the interplay of pinning with mesoscale QCPs offers a new perspective of quantum annealing and generic random field physics. Deep connections exist also with ferroelectrics and multiferroics2,34 which we expect to display related mesoscale quantum phase transitions. However, in ferroelectrics and multiferroics the search for microscopic quantum transitions is still at an early stage.35,36 The observation of mesocale QCPs in LiHoF4 finally motivates also to revisit the large body of unresolved inconsistencies, including instances of frozen spin order, observed at quantum phase transitions of metallic magnets.37–40 The identification of emergent mesoscale quantum criticality in LiHoF4 thus promises the discovery of a rich variety of materials properties in the future – some of which even without classical analogue. 9
