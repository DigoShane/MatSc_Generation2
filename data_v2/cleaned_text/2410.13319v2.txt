Evolution of pairing symmetry in FeSe1−xSx as probed by uniaxial-strain tuning of Tc Ruixian Liu§,1 Qi Tang§,1 Chang Liu,1 Chunyi Li,1 Kaijuan Zhou,1 Qiaoyu Wang,1 and Xingye Lu1, ∗ 1Center for Advanced Quantum Studies, School of Physics and Astronomy, Beijing Normal University, Beijing, 100875, China (Dated: October 21, 2024) In iron-based superconductors (FeSCs), the interplay between electronic nematicity and superconductivity is essential for understanding the exotic superconducting ground state. In the nematic regime, uniaxial-strain (ε) tuning of the superconducting transition temperature Tc [∆Tc(ε) = αε + βε2] offers a unique approach to investigating the evolution of pairing symmetry if both s and d wave pairing instabilities are relevant. Here, we employ uniaxial strain to tune the Tc of FeSe1−xSx, in which both nematicity and superconductivity un- dergo significant changes with doping. While Tc is usually suppressed quadratically with ε in optimally doped BaFe2As2, ∆Tc(ε) in FeSe1−xSx dominated by ∆Tc(ε) = βε2 changes its sign from β < 0 in FeSe to β > 0 in FeSe1−xSx (x ≳ 0.10), indicating an evolution of the pairing symmetry from an s± state towards an s + d wave state. These findings highlight the ∆Tc(ε) as a powerful probe for elucidating the superconducting pairing symmetry in the nematic regime of FeSCs and provide new insights into the evolution of pairing symmetry in FeSCs. The electronic nematic state is one of the most important emergent orders in iron-based superconductors (FeSCs) [1– 3]. While the origin of this state remains debated, nematic fluctuations are considered a potential pairing mechanism for superconductivity, and electronic nematicity is thought to sig- nificantly influence the superconducting properties. There- fore, understanding the interplay between electronic nematic- ity and superconductivity is crucial for unraveling the mystery of high-temperature (Tc) superconductivity in these materials . FeSe1−xSx is a unique system where magnetic order is ab- sent, allowing nematic order and fluctuations to interact di- rectly with superconductivity [3–6]. Figure 1(a) illustrates the phase diagram of FeSe1−xSx. The parent compound, FeSe, undergoes a tetragonal-to-orthorhombic structural transition at Ts ≈ 90 K, below which a C2-symmetric electronic ne- matic phase emerges, with the nematic transition temperature Tnem = Ts. In this nematic phase, FeSe enters a supercon- ducting ground state at Tc ≈ 8 K . Upon sulfur dop- ing, the orthorhombicity and nematic order are gradually sup- pressed and vanish around x ≈ 0.17. Beyond this regime, ne- matic fluctuations persist over a broad range of temperatures and doping levels , becoming especially pronounced near x ≈ 0.17, a concentration identified as the nematic quan- tum critical point (NQCP) . The Fermi surface of FeSe1−xSx consists of two nested hole pockets at the Γ point, along with electron pockets at the X and Y points . Fermi surface nesting between elec- tron pockets and central hole pockets (Γ-X and Γ-Y) gener- ates stripe-type spin fluctuations at (π, 0)/(0, π) , which potentially lead to an s±-wave pairing symmetry. Addition- ally, the Néel-type spin fluctuations at (π, π), likely aris- ing from particle-hole scattering between the electron pock- ets located at the X and Y points (X-Y) [13–16], suggest the possibility of d-wave pairing symmetry . Scanning tun- neling microscopy (STM) and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES) studies on FeSe have revealed highly anisotropic superconducting gaps [18–20], which can be de- scribed by an extended s + d wave gap function . In FeSe1−xSx, both thermal conductivity and specific heat measurements have revealed a pronounced change in the su- perconducting gap structure near the NQCP . STM inves- tigations have further identified two distinct superconducting pairing states on either side of the NQCP, indicating a signifi- cant evolution of the pairing symmetry or gap structure across this critical point [21–23]. However, despite these findings, the detailed nature of this evolution remains insufficiently ex- plored. While directly obtaining evidence for the doping evolu- tion of gap symmetry in FeSe1−xSx remains highly challeng- ing, the interplay between electronic nematicity and supercon- ductivity offers an alternative approach to address this issue. Theoretically, Fernandes et al. proposed that electronic ne- maticity could be used to probe superconducting pairing in FeSCs, as a d-wave instability may compete with the domi- nant s-wave instability, given that their free energies could be comparable . By incorporating a trilinear coupling term, FSC−nem ∝ φ∆s∆d cos θ, between nematicity (φ) and the s- and d-wave order parameters (∆s and ∆d) in spin fluc- tuation Eliashberg calculations, Ref. examined the ef- fects of tunable nematicity on s- and d-wave superconduc- tivity. They predicted that nematicity-induced changes in Tc [∆Tc(φ)] could serve as a probe to distinguish between s- and d-wave pairing regimes. Multicomponent superconductivity, involving a mixture of s- and d-wave pairing symmetries, has recently been observed in Ba(Fe1−xCox)2As2 through elastocaloric measurements under uniaxial strain, confirming the competition between s- and d-wave pairing in iron-pnictide superconductors . A subsequent theoretical study by Fernandes et al. suggested that similar competition is expected to occur in FeSe, leading to an evolution of pairing symmetry driven by specific tuning parameters. This is consistent with the anisotropic supercon- ducting gap and the doping-dependent evolution of the gap structure in FeSe1−xSx . The functional form of ∆Tc(φ) [equivalent to ∆Tc(ε)], dictated by the coupling FSC−nem ∝ φ∆s∆d cos θ, allows us to unveil the evolution of underlying pairing sym- metry in the nematic regime of FeSe1−xSx. In undoped and lightly doped FeSe1−xSx, ε-induced change in Tc is dom- inated by ∆Tc(ε) = βε2 with β < 0, consistent with the predominant s± wave pairing. At higher doping, β starts to evolve to β > 0 and ∆Tc(ε) is well characterized by ∆Tc(ε) = −|β|ε2 across the NQCP, indicating a transition from s± pairing towards a mixed state of s±- and d-wave pairing instabilities. The FeSe1−xSx single crystals used in this study were grown using the chemical vapor transport method, as de- scribed in detail elsewhere . The doping levels of the samples were determined by Energy-Dispersive X-ray Spec- troscopy measurements. To cover the nematic regime, we se- lected four doping levels (x = 0, 0.10, 0.16, and 0.21) across the NQCP for this study [Fig. 1(b)]. The corresponding phase transition temperatures are indicated by solid circles in the phase diagram of FeSe1−xSx [Fig. 1(a)]. Uniaxial strain was applied using bowtie-shaped titanium platforms (0.15 mm in thickness) as substrates, following the procedure outlined in Ref. . Specifically, thin, flat FeSe1−xSx single crys- tals (approximately 10 μm in thickness) were affixed to the neck of the titanium platform using EP29LPSP epoxy, as il- lustrated in Figs. 1(b) and 1(c) . The titanium platform was subsequently mounted in a sandwich configuration onto a commercial FC100 strain cell, allowing for the application of either tensile or compressive strain . Previous studies have demonstrated that strain applied to the titanium platform can be effectively transmitted to the thin crystal affixed to it . Before investigating the interplay between nematicity and superconductivity, we first validate the doping evolution of the nematic susceptibility within the nematic fluctuating regime in FeSe1−xSx. The persistence of χB2g nem provides a strong foundation for investi- gating the interplay between nematicity and superconductiv- ity. After illustrating the χB2g nem in FeSe1−xSx, we proceed to investigate the uniaxial strain dependence of Tc. Fig- ures 3(a)-3(d) present the resistivity curves for doping levels x = 0, 0.10, 0.16 and 0.21, measured under varying uniaxial strain ε. The superconducting transition temperatures, Tc, marked by black solid dots in each figure, exhibit systematic shifts in response to ε. The strain-induced change of Tc — ∆Tc(ε), shows linear strain dependence in x = 0 while at higher doping levels, it takes on a quadratic-like shape. These behaviors become clearer in the first-order temperature deriva- tive of the resistivity (dR/dT) as shown in Figs. 4(e)-4(h). Uniaxial strain tuning of phase transition temperatures (such as Ts and Tc) in the study of FeSCs is a well-established experimental method [26, 32–34]. To obtain a quantitative un- derstanding, it is necessary to decompose the strain-tuning ef- fects into all the irreducible (A1g, B1g and B2g) strains under the D4h point group that describes the structure of the unit cell of FeSe1−xSx. Due to the Poisson’s effect, the longitudinal strain εxx = ε generates εyy = −νε (ν ≈ 0.32 for the titanium substrate), resulting in an in-plane symmetry pre- serving strain εA1g = 1 2(εxx + εyy) = 1−ν 2 εxx = 0.34 ε and anti-symmetric strain εB2g = 1 2(εxx − εyy) = 1−ν 2 εxx = 0.66 ε. In the small range of ε, it has been demon- strated that the relative change of Tc can be described by a quadratic function ∆Tc(ε) = aεA1g + bε2 B2g, where the sym- metric εA1g contributes only to the linear term, while the an- tisymmetric εB2g dominates the biquadratic term . In terms of ε = ε, the formula becomes ∆Tc(ε) = αε+βε2, in which α = 0.34a and β = 0.44b. The dR/dT color map Figs. 4(e)-4(h) reveal a pure linear tributed to εA1g-induced ∆Tc during the detwinning process in a prior study of uniaxial-strained FeSe . The strain range corresponding to the linear ∆Tc(ε) defines the twin boundary in the phase diagram of ∆Tc(ε) . Indeed, such a linear region (associated with twinning) gradually shrinks with increasing (or decreasing) of the doping (Ts) and disap- pears across the NQCP [Fig. 4(h)]. To effectively explore the interplay between nematicity and superconductivity, it is cru- cial to bypass the linear region and measure the effects of εB2g on Tc. from Fig. 4 [black dots in Figs. 4(a)-4(d)]. The data be- yond the twin boundary in these figures (blue dots) can be fitted using ∆Tc = αε + βε2, where ε = ε. In Fig 5(c), data points in large strain regions deviated from the quadratic function are excluded. This is due to the requirement of an in- finitesimal strain approximation when decomposing Tc tuning results into quadratic functions, following the D4h irreducible representations framework. The normalized quadratic coefficient β derived from our fitting represents the εB2g sensitivity of Tc. In FeSe, the combined fitting of our data and that from prior study in Ref. generates a negative β (β < 0). Note this value might contain large uncertainty due to the large detwinning boundary in FeSe. Negative quadratic coefficient in ∆Tc = αε + βε2 have been reported in Ba(Fe1−xCox)2As2 and 5 BaFe2(As1−xPx)2 , that is, Tc is suppressed by ε. To facilitate a direct comparison, we measured the ε tuning of Tc using the same strain cell and obtained β ≈ −1.28×104 following the same analyzing method. Surprisingly, as the in- crease of the doping in FeSe1−xSx, the quadratic coefficient β evolves to positive values at x ≳ 0.1, indicating that Tc is enhanced by ε [Fig. 4(e)]. At higher doping, β in- creases with doping, evolves monotonically across the NQCP, and reachs β ≈ 8 × 104. It is worth noting that the lin- ear coefficient α in the optimally doped iron pnictides and in FeSe1−xSx is much smaller, suggesting marginal effects of the symmetric strain εA1g in tuning Tc in these systems . The ∆Tc(ε) in FeSe1−xSx and optimally doped Ba(Fe1−xCox)2As2 and BaFe2(As1−xPx)2 can be uni- fied within the aforementioned theoretical framework incorporating in the free energy the interplay between electronic nematicity, and s and d wave pairing instabilities — φ∆s∆d cos θ. In the presence of nematic order and fluctuations, it is found that the electronic nematicity leads to deviations of Tc [solid lines in Fig. 5(g)] from its original values [dashed lines in Fig. 5(g)]. The sign and magnitude of the deviation is essentially determined by the competition between s and d wave instability driven by controlling parameter such as doping levels, or the competition between different types of spin fluctuations favoring distinct pairing symmetries. As shown in Fig 5(h), in optimally doped iron pnictides and at a lower concentration of FeSe1−xSx [marked by blue arrow in Fig. 5(g)], β < 0 [blue curve in Fig. 5(h)] indicates their superconducting electron pairing is dominated by s± wave instability. In contrast, at a higher doping of FeSe1−xSx [marked by green arrow in Fig. 5(g)], an enhancement of Tc [green curve in Fig. 5(h)] can be attributed to the emergence of competing d wave instability. The coefficient β increase monotonically across the NQCP, indicating the system is evolving towards an s + d wave state. At the degeneracy point between s and d wave pairing, it is predicted that ∆Tc is a nonanalytic linear function, expressed as ∆Tc(ε) = |α|ε, which could occur at even higher doping x > 0.21 and requires validation in future studies. Nonetheless, the current results for ∆Tc(ε) provide compelling evidence concerning the significance of both s-and d-wave pairing instability in FeSe1−xSx and suggest that the system evolves from a predominant s± wave towards an s + d wave. Our study confirms the effectiveness of nematicity as a probe of pairing symmetry in FeSCs . In summary, we have demonstrated that uniaxial strain serves as an effective probe to elucidate the evolution of pair- ing symmetry in FeSe1−xSx across its nematic regime. Our measurements of strain-induced changes in Tc reveal a transi- tion from an s±-wave pairing state in undoped FeSe towards a mixed s+d-wave pairing state at higher sulfur doping. This evolution underscores the intricate interplay between nematic- ity and superconductivity. These findings not only establish uniaxial strain as a valuable tool for exploring superconduct- ing pairing mechanisms but also suggest that competing elec- tronic instabilities significantly influence the superconducting ground state in this class of materials. Further spectroscopic investigations under strain are essential to fully uncover the nature of the pairing symmetry near the nematic quantum crit- ical point and beyond.
