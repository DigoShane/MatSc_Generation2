arXiv:2307.12206v1 [cond-mat.supr-con] 23 Jul 2023 Novel π-type vortex in a nanoscale extreme type-II superconductor: Induced by quantum-size effect Haiyan Huang, Qing Liu, Wenhui Zhang, Yajiang Chen∗ College of Engineering and Design, Lishui University, Zhejiang 323000, China Abstract By numerically solving the Bogoliubov-de Gennes equations, we report a novel π-type vortex state whose order parameter near the core undergoes an extraor- dinary π-phase change for a quantum-confined extreme type-II s-wave super- conductor. Its supercurrent behaves as the cube of the radial coordinate near the core, and its local density of states spectrum exhibits a significant negative- shifted zero-bias peak. Such π-type vortex state is induced by quantum-size effect, and can survive thermal smearing at temperatures up to a critical value Tτ. The Anderson’s approximation indicates that the π-type vortex may re- main stable under sufficiently week magnetic field in the case less deep in the type-II limit. Moreover, we find that its appearance is governed by the sample size and kF ξ0 with kF the Fermi wave number and ξ0 the zero-temperature coherence length. Similar effects may be expected in quantum-confined ultra- cold superfluid Fermi gasses, or even high-Tc superconductors with proper kF ξ0 value. Keywords: π-type Vortex, Quantum-size effect, Bogoliubov-de Gennes equations, Extreme type-II superconductors, s-wave superconductor, In-gap fermion 1. Introduction Recently, π-type Meissner state, in which the spatial order parameter changes sign, has been found in many circumstances, e.g. at a superconductor-ferromagnet interface , in d-wave superconductors , and near an impurity site in s- wave superconductors . From these cases, it seams that the existence of π-type state in superconductors requires two necessary factors: inhomogeneous super- conductivity and interactions suppressing superconductivity. Superconducting vortex satisfies these conditions: a normal-superconducting transition domain is created by magnetic flux around the core and the system shows inhomogeneous superconductivity, π-phase shift in vortex states has not been discovered yet. ∗Email address: Yajiang.Chen@lsu.edu.cn Preprint submitted to Physica B: Condensed Matter July 25, 2023 Caroli, de Gennes and Matricon first discovered the microstructure of a superconducting vortex strongly depends on in-gap fermions, and the corre- sponding pronounced symmetric zero bias peak (ZBP) of the local density of states (LDOS) at the core was confirmed in experiments . Recently, it was found that the quantum interference of Andreev quasiparticles can induce Josephson vortex. For a macroscopic single-quantum vortex state, the energies and angular momenta of these in-gap fermions have the same sign, such chirality leads to a collapse of the vortex core in a clean s-wave superconductor near zero temperature (T ). It is called the Kramer-Pesch (KP) effect . Similar behav- ior was also found in bulk superfluid fermionic condensates , e.g. ultracold Fermi gases and superfluid neutron star matter . Scenario may change dramatically in the regime of nanoscale superconductiv- ity, where quantum confinement breaks the translational symmetry, and strongly modulates the behavior of quasiparticles. It has been reported that the quantum confinement leads to inhomogeneous superconductivity even for the Meissner state . The existence of single- and multi-quanta vortex states has already been verified in high-quality superconducting Pb nanofilms by the scanning tunnelling spectroscopy . The calculations based on the Bogoliubov-de Gennes theory also show that the quantum confinement may cause several unconventional vortex states, such as asymmetric ones and giant- multivortex combinations. For an isolated vortex in quantum-confined systems, we previously found that quantum-size effect may break down the chi- rality of in-gap fermions with small angular momenta, and cause a significant extension of the vortex core at finite temperatures, i.e. the superconductivity near the core is surprisedly depressed. Therefore, a natural question arises: whether such depression can produce an extraordinary vortex state with a π- phase shift in the order parameter near to the core. In this paper we numerically solve the Bogoliubov-de Gennes (BdG) equa- tions for a single-quantum vortex in a nanoscale cylindrical extreme type-II superconductor, and find that a π-phase shift may occur in the radial order pa- rameter near the core at T = 0 K when the chirality of the in-gap fermion with the lowest azimuthal quantum number m breaks down due to quantum con- finement. Such π-type vortex state shows ρ3-dependence in supercurrent j(ρ). Moreover, we find that kF ξ0 and radius R of the sample control the chirality breakdown of the in-gap fermion with the lowest m, i.e. the appearance of the π-type vortex state. Similar behavior may also be expected in other nanoscale extreme type-II superconductors, e.g. metallic nanoislands on Si , and ultracold superfluid Fermi gases in cigar- and pancake-shaped atomic traps . The paper is organized as follows. In Sec. 2, we outline the theoretical model based on the BdG equations for a single-quantum vortex in an extreme type-II s-wave superconducting nanocylinder. In Sec. 3, the behavior of the radial order parameter and supercurrent of a typical π-type vortex state is investigated at T = 0 K. We also study the critical temperature up to which a π-type vortex state can survive, as well as its distinct feature in the LDOS. The Anderson’s approximation is applied to analyze the stability of the π-type vortex under finite magnetic field in the case less deep in the type-II limit. Moreover, the 2 kF ξ0 versus R phase diagram of the vortex type at T = 0 K is investigated. Sec. 4 gives our main conclusions. 2. Results and discussions 3.1. The π-type vortex at T = 0 K Fig. 1 (a) shows the radial order parameter ∆(ρ) for a typical π-type vortex state of kF ξ0 = 11 and R = 1.6 ξ0 at T = 0 K. We see that ∆(ρ) becomes negative in the region from the center to the point Γ at ρ ≈ 0.066R, while in the rest region it stays positive, i.e., ∆(ρ) clearly undergoes a π-phase shift around the core. The π-phase-shift area has a radius about half the Fermi wavelength λF 2 ∼ 1 kF ≈ 0.057R. This behavior is related to the breakdown of the chirality of the in-gap fermion state of azimuthal quantum number m = − 1 2, which is labeled by the red star in the quasiparticle energy spectrum Fig. 1(b). The azimuthal quantum number (m = − 1 2) and its quasiparticle energy (E− 1 2 = 0.11∆0) have opposite signs. In fact, the state m = − 1 2 shows up in Fig. 1(b), while the conjugated bound state m = 1 2 disappears, i.e. its energy becomes negative. The π-type vortex at finite temperatures When switching on thermal smearing, to what extent the π-type vortex state can survive is of great interest and importance. As increasing T , the first term in Eq. (8) for the chirality-breakdown bound state m = − 1 2 turns to be nonzero. Thus, the linear dependence of j(ρ) gradually shows up around the core, and finally takes the major trend at a certain temperature Tτ, i.e. the characteristic of the supercurrent of π-type vortex state gets smeared out. The value of Tτ can be set by the temperature at which the state m = − 1 2 give half contribution of the electron-like part, i.e. f(E− 1 2 , Tτ) = 1 2. Such criteria leads to Tτ = E− 1 2 /kB. For instance, for the π-type vortex state of kF ξ0 = 11 and R = 1.6 ξ0, Tτ = 0.19 Tc. This criterion is quantitatively supported by numerical results. The temperature dependence of Γ value is shown in Fig. 1(a), which goes to zero when the π-type vortex state is terminated. Fig. 4(a) shows the zoom of ∆(ρ) in the core region at T/Tc = 0, 0.1 and 0.2 for kF ξ0 = 11 and R = 1.6 ξ0. We see that the π-phase-shift region of the radial order parameter decreases as T increases, and it finally disappears at a critical temperature. More detail about the temperature dependence of the π-phase-shift region, i.e. Γ(T ), is shown as the squared line in Fig. 4(b). The LDOS of the π-type vortex To find the evidence of the chirality-breakdown bound state or even π-type vortex state in experiments, Fig. 5 (a) and (b) plot the LDOS spectra of the whole π-type vortex state and the single chirality-breakdown bound state m = − 1 2 for R = 1.6 ξ0 and kF ξ0 = 11 at T = 0.05Tc, respectively. The LDOS for the whole sample in Fig. 5(a) shows similar particle-hole asymmetry and isolated peaks as in quantum limit in the absence of quantum confinement , but there is a significant difference. Here, the major peak at the vortex center ρ = 0 locates at E ≃ −E− 1 2 = −0.11∆0, i.e. on the positive energy side, while in Fig. 5 of Ref. the largest peak associated with the bound state m = 1 2 is on the negative energy side. Moreover, as seen from Fig. 5(b), the major peak at ρ = 0 and E = −0.11∆0 is mainly contributed by the chirality-breakdown bound state m = − 1 2. This negative-shifted zero-bias peak feature qualitatively differs from the previous studies, and can be checked by STM experiments. 3.4. Now, we study the question: when will a single-quantum π-type vortex state appear in a nanoscale extreme type-II s-wave superconductor? The phase diagram of the vortex type as R versus kF ξ0 at T = 0 K is shown in Fig. 5. We find that numerous π-type vortex state (red/dark) area discretely distributes mainly in the region of R < 2 ξ0 and kF ξ0 up to 50. Such discrete structure is caused by the quantization of the single-electron energy spectrum. As decreasing R for a fixed kF ξ0, the area of the π-type vortex state increases because of the 8 enhancement energy gap between quantized single-electron states (see Eq. (9)). When R increases and reaches a certain value Rmax, the π-type vortex state vanishes. Rmax varies with kF ξ0 as a result of competition between quantum confinement and ̄∆ν in Eq. (9). For kF ξ0 ∼ 50, the single-electron energy spectrum is almost continuous, so, it is difficult to find the chirality-breakdown in-gap state m = − 1 2 according to Eq. (9), i.e. the π-type vortex state. But, one may steadily observe the π-type vortex state at a proper range of R for a clean s-wave extreme type-II superconductor with relatively low kF ξ0, e.g. a typical A-15 compound V3Si with kF ξ0 ≃ 12, or even YBCO with kF ξ0 ∼ 4. The π-type vortex state also can be found in the high kF ξ0 region such as for Pb nanoislands , however, it requires well-controlled boundary roughness according to the narrow area in Fig. 6. 3.5. Conclusions To conclude, by numerically solving the Bogoliubov-de Gennes equations, we demonstrated that a single-quantum vortex state in a nanoscale cylindrical ex- treme type-II s-wave superconductor may undergo a π-phase shift in the radial order parameter near the core. We refer to it as π-type vortex state induced by quantum-size effect. The chirality-breakdown bound state of azimuthal quan- tum number m = − 1 2 plays the key role on such behavior. Such state shows two distinctive features: first, the supercurrent has a ρ3 behavior near the core, rather than the conventional linear dependence; second, the LDOS exhibits a significant negative-shifted zero-bias peak. The π-type vortex state may sur- vive thermal smearing when T/Tc < E− 1 2 /∆0 with E− 1 2 quasiparticle energy of bound state m = − 1 2, and it may remain stable under sufficiently week mag- netic field in the case less deep in the type-II limit. Based on the numerically calculated phase diagram of vortex type as kF ξ0 versus R, we may expect to find π-type vortex state in extreme type-II superconductors and superfluids with low kF ξ0 (∼ 10) and R < 2 ξ0, e.g. Pb nanoislands , ultracold super- fluid Fermi gases in cigar-shaped and pancake-shaped atomic traps , or even high-Tc superconductors, e.g. YBCO .
