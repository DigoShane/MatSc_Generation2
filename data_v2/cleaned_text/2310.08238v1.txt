I. INTRODUCTION While initially ab initio simulations were primarily used to provide physical insights on properties that are difficult to be observed experimentally , or to verify and understand experimental data, nowadays, they are increasingly being used to find new stable crystal structures . This research aims to predict, before experiments, novel materials that have targeted physical properties. The importance of ab initio sim- ulations is therefore increasing in materials science. But the demand to predict a new stable material is also increasing the accuracy required in the results; which in turns demands im- proving the underlying theory. The diffusion Monte Carlo (DMC) method is emerging as one the most practical many-body ab initio methods, because of its moderate cost scaling per step (в€ј N3) and excellent parallelizability . In addition, DMC has comparable accuracy and reliability to the coupled cluster single-double and perturbative triple [CCSD(T)] method [6вЂ“8]. DMC is of- ten implemented within the fixed-node (or fixed-phase) ap- proximation (FNDMC) to avoid the fermion sign problem . The fixed-node (phase) approximation introduced a positive bias in the total energy known as вЂњnodal errorsвЂќ. Nodal errors can be systematically reduced using multideterminant expansions, orbital optimization methods, or back-flow approxi- mations [14вЂ“16]. Accurate effective core potentials (ECPs) are also needed for practical problems. ECPs are constructed so as they accu- rately reproduce multiple electronic scattering properties of all-electron (AE) calculations, often obtained at the frozen core approximation level. In DMC the core electrons and ionic cores are replaced by an ECP to mitigate the compu- tational cost. Without ECPs, the calculation cost of FNDMC scales as Z5.5в€’6.5, where Z is the nuclear charge . With ECPs, the cost scaling gets reduced to be Z3.4 ECP, where ZECP is the effective core charge . The ECP approximation, however, brings a bias depending on the quality of ECPs (ECP errors) for any ab initio method . The typical semilocal form of ECP shown in Eq. (4) makes another type of errors (locality errors) when evaluated in FNDMC. Locality errors stem from the projection oper- ators to the angular momentum channels: пїЅl m=в€’l |lmвџ© вџЁlm|. Here, |lmвџ© is the eigenstate for angular momentum (l, m). Some approximation is needed to estimate the projections, пїЅl m=в€’l ОЁT |lmвџ© вџЁlm| ОЁ0, avoiding an additional sign problem . The conventional locality approximation and the alternative T-moves approximation can introduce er- rors in the range of 0.2вЂ“0.3 eV per atom for the 3d transi- tion metal elements for cohesive energies and relevant proper- ties [24вЂ“26]. The size of this error is roughly 10 times larger than the remaining ECPвЂ™s errors. The state-of-the-art ECPs for DMC reach 0.02вЂ“0.03 eV errors for the binding curves of 3d transition metal and oxygen dimers in CCSD(T) calculations . Therefore, the locality errors currently constitute the main contribution of ECP errors in DMC applications to 3d transition metal composites. arXiv:2310.08238v1 [cond-mat.mtrl-sci] 12 Oct 2023 2 Moreover, these locality errors are sometimes larger for the 4d transition metal elements . The magnitude of locality errors is dependent on the quality of the trial wavefunction ОЁT, including the Jastrow factor . Therefore, any compar- ison of results of different FNDMC calculations (required to predict magnetic properties, defect formation energies, band gaps etc.) becomes more dependent on the trial wave func- tions. Often trial wave functions have different locality errors since they are optimized independently. The uncertainty of the cancellation of locality errors increases with the size of the electronic system considered. Accordingly, similar to the case of nodal errors, avoiding locality errors is a priority to improve the overall accuracy of the FNDMC approach. Another alternative, the auxiliary field quantum Monte Carlo (AFQMC) approach, [33вЂ“36] can avoid using pseu- dopotentials altogether with a frozen core approximation. Moreover, pseudopotentials can be incorporated in AFQMC without incurring in locality errors. AFQMC calculations are often one or two orders of magnitude more expensive than FNDMC. Therefore, alternative ways to accurately represent atomic cores, that do not include locality errors or increase significantly the computational cost, must be developed in FNDMC to compete in accuracy with AFQMC. A possible approach to avoid the locality errors in FNDMC is the pseudo-Hamiltonian (PH) framework . Although the PH framework was originally proposed in 1989 , this framework has been investigated only in a few cases [38вЂ“ 40]. This is due to the numerical difficulty to satisfy the con- straint that the eigenenergies of nodeless orbitals increasing monotonically with the angular momentum l . It is even harder to correctly describe the scattering properties of multi- ple angular-momentum channels together along with the con- straint . This constraint makes it difficult to construct an accurate PH, especially for some first row elements and Ar- core 3d TMs. However, the PH framework should be now reconsidered because recent progresses such as the multideterminant ex- pansion and Pfaffian pairing wave function [42вЂ“44], and increased computational resources have made possible to reduce nodal and statistical errors in FNDMC so much that locality errors may become the leading source of error in deli- cate energy sensitive comparisons involving transition metals. Therefore, some of us have developed a theoretical framework and procedures to construct PHs and demonstrated their effectiveness for the Co element . In this paper, we report newly constructed PHвЂ™s for the 3d transition metal elements from Cr to Zn with a slight modifica- tion of a previous procedure . We also compare the trans- ferability of our PHs with highly accurate semilocal pseu- dopotentials used with FNDMC [27, 28, 46вЂ“49]. The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In section II, we explain the theory and the procedures used to generate multiple PHs using a combination of Hartree-Fock (HF) and CCSD(T) methods for each transition metal (TM) element. In Section III, we explain the details of calculations conducted for the optimization and verification of the PHs. In Subsection IV A, we explain how we selected the optimal PHs (named OPH23) based on the atomic properties and TM-O binding curves from the multiple candidate PHs we constructed for each TM element. In Subsection IV B, we confirmed the reproducibility of AE binding curves of TMвЂ“O and TMвЂ“F molecules by the OPH23 set, which is compared with highly accurate semilocal pseudopotentials used with FNDMC, BFD , SBKJC , and ccECP . CALCULATION DETAILS We used libraries in the Nexus workflow management software to optimize the PHs and convert ECP file for- mat. We employed the NelderвЂ“Mead algorithm imple- mented in SciPy for parameter fitting of PHs. During the optimization process, we used a numerical atomic HartreeвЂ“ Fock code to evaluate the eigenengies, norms, and ex- citation energies of atoms. We used the Molpro code to evaluate the в€†ECC j term of Eq. (22) and the dimer bind- ing curves and atomic excitation energies with CCSD(T). We evaluated the dimer binding curves with our PHs and semilocal pseudopotentials used with FNDMC: BFD, SBKJC, and ccECP, for comparison, and by AE calculations as the reference. Here, we truncated the l > 7 terms of PHs in Eq. (3) because Molpro cannot deal with Л†L2 directly. We have confirmed that the binding curve suf- ficiently converges with this truncation in the case of CoвЂ“ O. We used the 10th order DouglasвЂ“KrollвЂ“Hess relativistic Hamiltonian for the AE calculations. We used the non-relativistic Hamiltonian for calculations with ECPs. We evaluated the ionization energies shown in Figures 2 and 3 with the complete basis set (CBS) limit calculated with the fully uncontracted aug-cc-pwCVnZ-DK basis sets (n=T, Q, and 5) . The CBS limit was determined according to the methodology outlined in the ccECP paper for 3d TM el- ements . V. CONCLUSION We have developed a set of PHs (OPH23) for the CrвЂ“Zn transition metal elements. We have used a revised procedure from our previous work. This procedure can be used for other elements in the periodic table. The set OPH23s are trained to reproduce all the valence orbital norms and eigenen- ergies with the HF method as well as the neutral excitation and ionization energy spectrum in 3d and 4s levels with the HF or CCSD(T) method using accurate semilocal pseudopo- tentials, and the AE TMвЂ“O binding curve with the CCSD(T) method. The set OPH23s reproduced the AE binding curves of TMвЂ“F within or quite near chemical accuracy. The largest deviation from the AE binding curve at the equilibrium dis- tance was 0.067 eV of CrвЂ“F. The accuracy of the PHs is com- parable to the best FNDMC pseudopotentials in the literature . The pseudo-Hamiltionian approach removes the need of locality approximations in FNDMC, thus remov- ing locality errors. Therefore, even when in some cases the deviations exceed chemical accuracy, they are significantly smaller than the typical magnitude of locality errors for tran- sition metal elements, 0.2вЂ“0.3 eV . Our aim is, in the future, to employ the use of OPH23s to delve into an in-depth examination of strongly correlated tran- sition metal complexes, focusing on their magnetic structures and other intrinsic properties. While the PHs were designed to reproduce the AE ionization energies and AE TM-O bind- ing curves, they are found to reproduce the AE TM-F binding curves. Further test in solids are required to assess their trans- ferability. This will be and addressed in future works.
