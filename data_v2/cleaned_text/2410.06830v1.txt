Received YYY; in original form ZZZ ABSTRACT The inner and outer cores of neutron stars are believed to contain type-I and -II proton superconductors, respectively. The type-I superconductor exists in an intermediate state, comprising macroscopic flux-free and flux-containing regions, while the type-II superconductor is flux-free, except for microscopic, quantized flux tubes. Here, we show that the inner and outer cores are coupled magnetically, when the macroscopic flux tubes subdivide dendritically into quantized flux tubes, a phenomenon called flux branching. An important implication is that up to ∼ 1012(r1/106 cm) erg of energy are required to separate a quantized flux tube from its progenitor macroscopic flux tube, where r1 is the length of the macroscopic flux tube. Approximating the normal-superconducting boundary as sharp, we calculate the magnetic coupling energy between a quantized and macroscopic flux tube due to flux branching as a function of, f1, the radius of the type-I inner core divided by the radius of the type-II outer core. Strong coupling delays magnetic field decay in the type-II superconductor. For an idealised inner core containing only a type-I proton superconductor and poloidal flux, and in the absence of ambipolar diffusion and diamagnetic screening, the low magnetic moments (≲ 1027 G cm3) of recycled pulsars imply f1 ≲ 10−1.5. Key words: dense matter – stars: interiors – stars: magnetic fields – stars: neutron – pulsars: general – stars: evolution 1 INTRODUCTION Neutron stars are believed to contain a type-II proton superconductor, located mainly in the outer core, and a type-I proton superconductor, located mainly in the inner core (Sedrakian et al. 1997; Jones 2006; Glampedakis et al. 2011; Haskell et al. 2013; Haber & Schmitt 2017). The geometry of the interface between the two types of superconduc- tivity is complicated in general (Lander 2013). Typical neutron stars have magnetic field strength B ∼ 1012 G ≪ Hc ∼ Hc1 ∼ 1015 G, where Hc is the critical magnetic field of the type-I superconductor and Hc1 is the first critical magnetic field of the type-II supercon- ductor. Consequently, the equilibria of both the type-I and -II su- perconductors are Meissner states, where magnetic flux is expelled ultimately from the bulk. However, due to the high electrical con- ductivity of the relativistic electrons in the core, the flux expulsion time-scale is greater than the nucleation time-scale and the age of the star (Baym et al. 1969a), so the proton superconductor stays in a metastable state threaded by normal regions containing magnetic flux. The topologies of the normal regions depend on the history of the electrical currents in the star and the surface energy, γ, of the normal-superconducting boundary. Type-II superconductors have γ < 0, favoring microscopic normal regions (quantized flux tubes) with magnetic flux quantum Φ0 = πħ/2e = 2.07 × 10−7 G cm2. In contrast, type-I superconductors have γ > 0, favoring macroscopic normal regions with magnetic flux nΦ0 and n ≫ 1 (Tinkham 2004). ★ E-mail: kokhongt@student.unimelb.edu.au † E-mail: amelatos@unimelb.edu.au Magnetic field lines cannot terminate at the type-I-II interface be- cause of Gauss’ law for magnetism. Hence it is natural to ask how the poloidal fluxes in the type-I and -II superconductors are connected. Recent studies have shown that type-1.5 proton superconductivity is expected at the type-I-II interface due to the interpenetrating and strongly interacting neutron superfluid; the neutrons establish a sec- ond attractive length-scale for the quantized flux tubes (Haber & Schmitt 2017; Wood & Graber 2022). But the puzzle remains: how do the field lines from different phases connect? For example, near the type-I-II interface, do discrete, quantized flux tubes from the outer core coalesce into smoothly continuous flux in the inner core? If the quantized flux tubes coalesce into macroscopic flux tubes near the type-I-II interface but remain separate in the outer core, what en- ergy would be required for a quantized flux tube to wholly separate from the macroscopic flux tube to which it is connected in the inner core and move freely? In this paper, we explore the possibilities and consequences of flux-tube coalescence via flux branching, where the macroscopic normal regions from the inner core branch out to form quantized flux tubes in the outer core. Flux branching has been observed terrestrially near the surface of type-I superconductors with γ > 0 in a uniform, applied magnetic field (Huebener 2001; Han et al. 2002; Tinkham 2004). Branching in type-I superconductors costs free energy, but the reduced magnetic energy outside the superconductor compensates the cost. Bunched-up field lines in the macroscopic normal regions become more uniform spatially near the surface, so that magnetic field lines within the nor- mal regions join more smoothly with those outside (Landau 1938, 1943). We employ the flux branching formalism devel- oped by Landau (1943) to consider the possibility of branching near the type-I-II interface in neutron stars. A striking similarity exists between the boundary of a type-I su- perconductor in a uniform, applied magnetic field and the type-I-II interface. Bunched-up field lines are preferred inside the type-I super- conductor, while more uniform field lines are preferred outside the type-I superconductor and inside the type-II superconductor. Draw- ing intuition from flux branching in type-I superconductors immersed in a uniform, applied field, one expects flux branching to occur also at the interface of a type-I and -II superconductor. Conversely, if flux branching does not occur, are the macroscopic normal regions and quantized flux tubes separately closed, so that the type-I and -II super- conductors are magnetically disconnected (Kozlov & Samokhvalov 1993; Lander 2013; Anzuini & Melatos 2020; Cadorim et al. 2023)? Or is the whole core filled with mesoscopic normal regions? Each possibility mentioned above has different implications for observable phenomena in a neutron star, e.g., secular spin down (Beskin et al. 1993; Gurevich & Istomin 2007; Barsukov et al. 2014), rotational glitches (Link 2003; Haskell et al. 2013; Drummond & Melatos 2018; Sourie & Chamel 2020; Thong et al. 2023), magnetic evolu- tion, e.g., the time-scale of magnetic decay (Srinivasan et al. 1990) and polar magnetic drift (Macy 1974; Shaham 1977; Alpar & Oegel- man 1987; Melatos 2000), and gravitational wave emission (Melatos et al. 2015). The paper is structured as follows. In Section 2, we review the canonical flux branching formalism introduced by Landau (1943) and apply energetic arguments to flux branching in a neutron star. We find that flux branching is the preferred mechanism to magnetically couple the type-I and -II superconductors under a wide range of plausible parameters. In Section 3, we calculate the coupling energy between a macroscopic and quantized flux tube of the same flux tree. We then discuss an important astrophysical implication of flux branching in Section 4, namely how magnetic coupling retards magnetic decay, lengthening the time-scale over which flux remains “frozen” in the inner and outer cores. The observed magnetic moments of recycled pulsars are used to set an upper bound on the volume of the type-I superconductor based on the above phenomenon. 2 FREE ENERGY OF FLUX BRANCHING In this section, we apply the sharp-interface model introduced by Landau (1943) and later developed by others (Andrew 1948; Choksi et al. 2004, 2008; Conti et al. 2015; Cinti & Otto 2016; Conti et al. 2018) to study the behaviour of flux branching in a neutron star. The sharp-interface model is outlined in Section 2.1. We calculate the free energy of a single branching cell in Section 2.2 and concatenated branching cells or a flux tree in Section 2.3. The expected size of the macroscopic normal regions is briefly discussed in Section 2.4. Applying the results to neutron stars, we show in Section 2.5 that the free energy of flux branching is negative under a range of plausible conditions, implying that flux branching occurs. 2.1 Sharp-interface model The sharp-interface model can be derived from the Ginzburg-Landau model in the limit that the coherence length, ξ, and the London penetration depth, λ, are much less than the size of the normal and superconducting regions (Chapman 1995; Choksi et al. 2004). In the sharp-interface model, normal regions have a uniform magnetic field a macroscopic normal region (bottom cylinder) branches into four smaller normal regions (top cylinders). B = Hc, while superconducting regions have B = 0. The surplus free energy, F, when including a normal region in an otherwise superconducting material, is given by F = H2cV 4π + H2c 8π ∫ dS δ (1) in cgs units, where V is the volume of the normal region and S and δ are the surface area and the scaled surface energy of the normal- superconducting boundary, with δ = 8πHc−2γ ≈ ξ − λ (Tinkham 2004). Type-I superconductors have δ > 0, while type-II supercon- ductors have δ < 0. For a given magnetic flux, Φ, type-I supercon- ductors energetically favor Φ to be contained within a macroscopic normal region. In contrast, type-II superconductors energetically fa- vor Φ to be spread across many microscopic normal regions, often called quantized flux tubes (see Appendix A). As a note of cau- tion, quantized flux tubes have magnetized cores of size ∼ λ and normal regions of size ∼ ξ, so the sharp-interface approximation applies poorly to quantized flux tubes. Indeed, normal regions are modelled as non-interacting in the sharp-interface approximation, whereas quantized flux tubes are mutually and magnetically repul- sive. Such coupling affects the magnetic evolution of the star. Flux branch- ing couples quantized flux tubes (in the type-II superconductor) with a macroscopic flux tube (in the type-I superconductor) of the same flux tree. For a quantized flux tube to move freely in the type-II su- perconductor, the flux tube has to either (i) separate from the flux tree, or (ii) drag the entire flux tree with 4N −1 − 1 other flux tubes along with it. Option (i) increases S in the type-I superconductor and costs energy, because type-I superconductors have δ > 0. We define the coupling energy, Ec, as the energy required to separate a quantized flux tube from the flux tree, as in option (i). Option (ii), on the other hand, is less probable than option (i). A quantized flux tube is unlikely to drag 4N −1 − 1 ≫ 1 other flux tubes along with it, as other flux tubes may be pinned to inhomogeneities such as neutron superfluid vortices (Muslimov & Tsygan 1985; Sauls 1989; Srinivasan et al. 1990). Let us calculate the energy, Ec, required to separate a quantized flux tube from the flux tree in the type-I superconductor, as in option (i). Specifically, let us calculate Ec as a function of the flux tree’s location and the type-I superconductor’s size. This analysis allows one to infer an upper limit for the size of the type-I superconducting region from a measurement of a neutron star’s magnetic dipole moment. 4.1 Neutron-vortex-proton-flux-tube pinning In the outer core of a neutron star, the pinning of quantized proton flux tubes to the neutron superfluid vortices (and vice-versa) has been studied extensively in the literature (Muslimov & Tsygan 1985; Sauls 1989; Srinivasan et al. 1990; Bhattacharya & Srinivasan 1991; Rud- erman et al. 1998; Link 2003; Glampedakis et al. 2011; Glampedakis & Andersson 2011; Gügercinoğlu & Alpar 2014; Alpar 2017; Drum- mond & Melatos 2017, 2018; Thong et al. 2023). Pinning hinders flux tubes from rising buoyantly into the resistive crust, lengthening the Ohmic decay time-scale (Baym et al. 1969b; Srinivasan et al. 1990) of μ(t) (Srinivasan et al. 1990; Ruderman et al. 1998; Fer- rario et al. 2015). Pinning is also cited as a mechanism for vortex avalanches (rotational glitches) and for linking rotational and mag- netic evolution, e.g., μ(t) ∝ Ω(t), where Ω(t) is the magnitude of the angular velocity of the outer core (Srinivasan et al. 1990; Bhat- tacharya & Srinivasan 1991; Ruderman et al. 1998; Alpar 2017). As electromagnetic and gravitational-wave torques decelerate the crust, vortices and flux tubes pinned to vortices are expelled from the outer core into the crust, thereby reducing μ(t) and Ω(t). Srinivasan et al. (1990) invoked this mechanism to explain the contrast between the magnetic moments of isolated pulsars (μ ∼ 1030 G cm3) and recy- cled pulsars with an accretion history (μ ≲ 1027 G cm3). How does Ec in Section 3 compare to the vortex-flux-tube pin- ning strength, Epin? The neutron superfluid and proton supercon- ductor interact strongly via density and gradient (entrainment) cou- pling (Alpar 1977; Sauls 1989; Srinivasan et al. 1990; Ruderman et al. 1998). The dominant interaction between the topological de- fects of the two fluids is magnetic and arises from entrainment; neutron vortices entrain protons, dressing them with a magnetic flux ∼ 10−1Φ0, which pins them to the proton flux tubes with 1.6 × 10−6 ≲ |Epin,max|/(1 erg) ≲ 8.0 × 10−5 per vortex-flux-tube intersection, where |Epin,max| is the magnitude of the maximum pin- ning energy, noting that the magnitude and sign of Epin depend on the relative orientation of the vortex and quantized flux tube (Srini- vasan et al. 1990; Bhattacharya & Srinivasan 1991; Ruderman et al. 1998; Alford & Good 2008; Alpar 2017). We find Epin ≪ Ec for most f1 and fρ, except for f1 ≪ 1 as in equation (29), or fρ ≈ 1 as in equation (30). When a vortex pinned to a quantized flux tube moves outwards, it uncouples the flux tube from its flux tree for Epin > Ec by dragging it, or unpins from and cuts through the flux tube for Epin < Ec. Thus, vortices cut through quantized flux tubes in most situations, except when the type-I superconductor is small (r1 ≪ r2, such that the macroscopic flux tube is shorter than the quantized flux tubes) or if the quantized flux tubes are coupled to a flux tree near the type-I-II interface (ρt ≈ r1). Otherwise, vortices do not drag flux tubes away from their flux trees and into the crust. 4.2 Frozen magnetic field and the maximum size of the type-I region Quantized flux tubes in the outer core of a neutron star fall into three categories: (i) isolated, i.e. they thread the type-II superconductor only and are not part of a flux tree; (ii) weakly coupled to a flux tree, i.e. Ec < Epin; and (iii) strongly coupled to a flux tree, i.e. Ec > Epin. Only categories (i) and (ii) can be pushed around by moving vortices; category (iii) is unaffected. Without a mechanism for moving an entire flux tree to the crust, where Ohmic decay occurs faster, the flux remains frozen within the inner core (Baym et al. 1969b; Srinivasan et al. 1990). The frozen magnetic field in the core may be the source of magnetic moments measured in recycled pulsars with a history of accretion from a binary companion. Suppose first that all quantized flux tubes with Ec( f1, fρ) < Epin are dragged out by vortices during the spin- down phase before accretion begins. The remaining flux trees make up the residual magnetic field. In this section, we discuss some of the idealisations. Chief among them are the assumptions made about the macroscopic evolution and structure of the magnetic field. We ne- glect expulsion of the magnetic field from the proton superconductor in the inner core by ambipolar diffusion (Passamonti et al. 2017; Kantor & Gusakov 2018). We also neglect the potentially significant toloidal component of the magnetic field (Braithwaite 2009; Lander & Jones 2009; Lander 2013). Toroidal flux branches in the same overall manner as poloidal flux if it threads the type-I and -II regions. However, the branching physics is more complicated. The current- current vortex-flux-tube interaction (Ruderman et al. 1998) depends on the relative orientation of an interacting vortex and flux tube, whereas the density interaction does not (Srinivasan et al. 1990). Additionally, the tangled poloidal and toroidal flux tubes may al- low for easier expulsion of vortices through the multi-site unpinning mechanism termed “slithering” (Thong et al. 2023); that is, more flux tubes may be “left behind” in the core, than if the magnetic field is purely poloidal. A vortex can also pin to larger flux tubes near the type-I-II interface or in the type-I region. The study introduces a spatially varying normal-superconducting surface energy, γ, to model the type-I-II superconducting interface, extending previous studies where γ is constant for a type-I superconductor in an ex- ternally applied and uniform magnetic field (Landau 1938, 1943). Assuming that γ varies linearly with radius, we compare the free energy of superconductors with and without flux branching. With- out flux branching, m flux tubes with radii a1/√m thread both su- perconductors rectilinearly. We predict that flux branching is the preferred topology for a magnetic field threading both superconduc- tors, for a wide range of plausible parameters. Specifically, for all m and (L1 + L2)/2 ∼ 106 cm, we find that flux branching min- imizes the free energy for 8.6 × 10−5 ≲ L2/L1 ≲ 1.4 × 104, 2.7 × 10−6 ≲ L2/L1 ≲ 1.4 × 102, 8.4 × 10−8 ≲ L2/L1 ≲ 0.87 for N = 28, 38, 48, respectively. With flux branching, macroscopic flux tubes in the type-I superconductor divide dendritically into micro- scopic quantized flux tubes in the type-II superconductor. Near the type-I-II interface, flux tubes with varying numbers of integer Φ0 are found, similar to the type-II(n) flux tube structure obtained by Alford & Good (2008) due to superfluid-superconductor coupling. In Section 3, we calculate Ec, the energy required to separate a quantized flux tube from its flux tree. The result is 0 ≲ Ec/(1 erg) ≲ 1013. As a result, the magnetic coupling between most quantized and macroscopic flux tubes of the same flux tree greatly exceeds the strength of vortex-flux-tube pinning (i.e. Ec ≫ Epin), impeding magnetic decay and freezing flux trees in the core. Strongly coupled (Ec ≫ Epin) quantized flux tubes are unaffected by vortices during spin down, resulting in μ(t) ̸∝ Ω(t). That is, a neutron star can spin down without significant magnetic decay; only isolated and weakly coupled (Ec ≪ Epin) quantized flux tubes can be dragged by vortices to the crust for Ohmic decay. In reality, of course, outwardly moving vortices may not remove all isolated and weakly coupled quantized flux tubes from the outer core during spin down. If some isolated quantized flux tubes fail to be removed by vortices during spin down, the region 0 ≤ r ≤ rf defined by (33) contains isolated quantized flux tubes, and r1 from (36) represents an upper limit. There is a complex interplay between vortex and flux tube dy- namics, influenced by factors such as interaction strength and tangled geometries (Drummond & Melatos 2017, 2018; Thong et al. 2023). Additionally, a neutron star may not contain a type-I superconductor at all, in which case the inference leading to (36) is void. The inner core can contain other phases of dense matter, such as hyperonic matter (Vidaña et al. 2000; Nishizaki et al. 2002; Schaffner-Bielich et al. 2002) and a quark color superconductor (Alford et al. 2000; Alford 2001; Lattimer & Prakash 2004; Alford & Sedrakian 2010; Baym et al. 2018). It is a question for future work, unanswered at the time of writing, whether flux branching occurs between an adjacent type-I (or -II) proton superconductor and a color superconductor. We emphasize that the treatment in this paper is highly ide- alized and relies on the following assumptions: (i) the normal- superconducting boundary is sharp, and flux-containing normal re- gions (flux tubes) do not interact (Chapman 1995); (ii) γ varies linearly with r, whereas in reality, the functional form of γ depends on the density profile of the protons (Tinkham 2004); (iii) the dy- namical formation of flux trees is possible, which would require a time-dependent calculation; (iv) general relativity may be relevant to flux trees spanning the entire core; and (v) the observed magnetic mo- ment does not trace the internal magnetic field exactly in the presence of toroidal components (Lander 2013; Lasky & Melatos 2013; Mas- trano et al. 2015) and diamagnetic screening from accreted matter (Romani 1990; Cumming et al. 2001; Rai Choudhuri & Konar 2002; Payne & Melatos 2004). Additionally, magnetic field lines with com- plicated geometries, like flux rings (Kozlov & Samokhvalov 1993; Cadorim et al. 2023) or tangled quantized flux tubes (Thong et al. 2023), are neglected in this paper.
