INTRODUCTION The Majorana fermion is a particle that acts as its own antiparticle, satisfying ќ≥пњљ пњљ ќ≥, which was introduced by Ettore Majorana as a solution to the relativistic Dirac equation . Although it is uncertain whether Majorana fermions exist in nature, it is predicted that quasiparticles in Majorana fermionic states can be found in condensed matter systems. The Majorana fermionic states in condensed matter systems, the so-called Majorana zero-energy modes (MZMs), have attracted much attention due to its non-Abelian statistics, in which the exchange operation is non-commutative . Spatially separated MZMs are protected topologically, and thus provide a platform for fault-tolerant topological quantum computation based on non-Abelian braiding operations . Owing to the particleвАУhole symmetry in superconductors, ќ≥пњљEпњљ пњљ ќ≥пњљпњљпњљEпњљ , zero-energy Bogoliubov quasiparticles can be regarded as the Majorana fermionic excitation in topological superconductors. Initially, it was proposed that the Majorana fermionic states appear as low- energy excitations in fractional quantum Hall phases or 3He superfluid . There have been numerous attempts to detect Majorana fermions in topological superconductors, such as one- dimensional spinless p -wave superconductors and two-dimensional chiral pпњљ пњљ ipпњљ superconductors . However, the existence of p-wave superconductors in nature has yet to be confirmed . Instead, topological superconductors have been artificially engineered to realize topological superconductivity and the MZMs via the superconducting proximity effect . Topological superconductivity can be induced when a conventional s-wave superconductor contacts a material with strong spin-orbit coupling. Recently, diverse systems have been hypothesized and investigated experimentally to realize MZMs (see also detailed reviews in Refs. ). There are two major experimental schemes to detect the MZMs in a topological superconductor. The first involves observing quantized zero-bias conductance due to the MZMs, G пњљ 2eпњљ/h, by tunneling spectroscopy. The second requires resolving the fractional Josephson effect in a Josephson weak-link configuration. Experimental studies on quantized zero-bias conductance have claimed the existence of MZMs; however, other effects, such as disorder or the Kondo effect , cannot be ruled out. The fractional Josephson effect emerges only in topological Josephson junctions (JJs), unless a non-adiabatic transition exists between the gapped Andreev bound states (ABSs) of a conventional junction (the so-called LandauвИТZener transition), as shown by the magenta arrows in Fig. 1(a). Because an extremely high transparency is required for such a transition , this has been reported only once in a JJ configuration to date . The conventional ABSs of a short, ballistic JJ are 2ѕА-periodic in phase difference, ѕЖ, with anti-crossing at ѕЖ пњљ пњљ2n пњљ 1пњљѕА [Fig. 1(a)]. In contrast, the ABSs of a topological JJ are 4ѕА- periodic, with an energy level crossing at ѕЖ пњљ пњљ2n пњљ 1пњљѕА [Fig. 1(b)]. Here, n is an integer number. As the energy of the ABSs changes sign at ѕЖ пњљ пњљ2n пњљ 1пњљѕА at a topological junction, the parity of the ground state of the system also changes between even and odd at ѕЖ пњљ пњљ2n пњљ 1пњљѕА. In equilibrium, inelastic processes [represented by the green arrows in Fig. 1(b)] that violate fermion parity conservation relax the system to the ground state. This leads to 2ѕА- periodic dynamics in the currentвАУphase relation (CPR) of a JJ, as in a conventional junction. This also explains why Fraunhofer pattern measurements, in which the dynamical time scale of the junction is much longer than the parity relaxation time of an order of milliseconds , is not applicable for detecting the 4ѕА periodicity of a JJ. However, fast measurements such as Shapiro steps and Josephson emission spectra, in which the characteristic sub- nanosecond time scale is shorter than the relaxation time, can be implemented for observing the fractional Josephson effect. In this paper, we report the detailed features of Shapiro steps in the presence of 4ѕА-periodic CPR over a broad range of experimental parameters. When a JJ is irradiated by microwaves, the currentвАУvoltage curve of the junction exhibits step-like behavior, the so-called Shapiro steps, as shown in Figs. 1(c) and (d). Plateaus emerge at constant voltage steps, although the length of the plateaus (step size) can be modulated by the alternating current (AC) applied to the junction by microwave irradiation (Iпњљпњљ). The step height (voltage interval between these steps) is Vпњљ пњљ ƒІпњљпњљпњљ пњљпњљ in conventional 2ѕА-periodic junctions, whereas the step height is expected to be doubled in entirely 4ѕА-periodic junctions, where ƒІ is PlanckвАЩs constant divided by 2ѕА, ѕЙпњљпњљ is the angular frequency of the irradiated microwave, and e is the charge of an electron. Thus, вАЬperiod-doubled Shapiro stepsвАЭ provide strong evidence for the presence of the 4ѕА-periodic CPR. In practice, however, the 4ѕА -periodic contribution coexists with, and is usually dominated by, the 2ѕА-periodic one, even in the presence of the MZMs, such that the step height follows ƒІпњљпњљпњљ пњљпњљ of the 2ѕА-periodic case. In this situation, it is expected that the steps at even multiples of ƒІпњљпњљпњљ пњљпњљ (even steps) become more pronounced compared to those at odd multiples of ƒІпњљпњљпњљ пњљпњљ (odd steps), due to the 4ѕА-periodic CPR. Therefore, to obtain information about the 4ѕА-periodic nature, the length of the plateau (step size) must be analyzed in depth. Previous reports claiming the detection of a 4ѕА-periodic Josephson current show the distinct features of Shapiro steps. When the microwave frequency irradiating a junction is sufficiently low, the odd steps are suppressed at the low AC limit. As the AC is increased, however, odd steps emerge and their step sizes become comparable to those of even steps. In addition, the size of the odd steps for low AC is restored as the AC frequency continues to increase . Although these features were considered to arise from the presence of a 4ѕА-periodic Josephson current, an in-depth analysis is required. For instance, the abovementioned features have not been reproduced by resistively shunted Josephson junction (RSJ) model analyses, which overlook the hysteretic behavior of a junction. To our knowledge, only two reports have considered the capacitance of a junction when describing the Shapiro steps with 4ѕА-periodic Josephson coupling ; however, no clear explanations were provided regarding the characteristic features of Shapiro steps observed in previous measurements. Here, we report on the step size of Shapiro steps in the presence of 4ѕА-periodic Josephson coupling. We examine the overall features of step size with respect to the broad range of representative junction parameters. The step size was obtained by numerical simulations using the resistively and capacitively shunted junction (RCSJ) model, which includes a capacitance term. The characteristic features of the Shapiro steps observed in previous experiments were reproduced only in the hysteretic junctions with finite capacitance. We also estimated the portion of 4ѕА-periodic current that exhibited good agreement with the experimental conditions. Moreover, we suggest a strategy for distinguishing a small portion of the 4ѕА -periodic Josephson current from the dominating conventional 2ѕА -periodic one, assuming realistic experimental conditions. Conclusion We introduced a strategy to design a junction confirming the presence of a 4ѕА -periodic Josephson component by analyzing Shapiro step measurements. First, it is important to increase the proportion of the 4ѕА-periodic Josephson current with respect to the 2ѕА-periodic one, which arises inevitably from the topologically trivial bulk residual modes of a junction. An increase in Iпњљпњљ/Iпњљпњљ of only a few percent produces a large change in the Shapiro-step behavior, as shown in Fig. 7. For instance, a reduction in the channel width (area) helps to suppress the bulk residual mode for a two-dimensional (or three-dimensional) mesa structure. Lastly, ќ± is so small in usual experimental situations that a low ќ© is required to observe the period-doubled Shapiro steps. However, a low ќ© reduces the step size, as well as the step height, which hinders accurate estimation of the former.
