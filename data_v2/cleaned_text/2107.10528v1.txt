Meissner State in Classical Ideal Charged Fluid Vladimir Toussaint1∗ and George H. Goedecke2† 1School of Mathematical Sciences, University of Nottingham Ningbo China, Ningbo 315100, China 2Physics Department, New Mexico State University, Las Cruces, NM, USA July 23, 2021 Abstract It is shown that a model of a superconductor as a classical ideal charged fluid at constant uniform temperature in a uniform compensating positively charged background exhibits the Meissner state with the correct value of the penetration depth. The differences between this model and the textbook classical model that fails to predict the Meissner state are discussed, as are the similarities of this model to the coupled Schr ̈odinger-Maxwell model, which does predict flux exclusion. In particular, this model does not neglect the pressure term and terms that are bilinear in the velocity in the fluid equations; these terms are essential to the results. ∗vladimir.toussaint@nottingham.edu.cn †ggoedeck@nmsu.edu 1 arXiv:2107.10528v1 [cond-mat.supr-con] 22 Jul 2021 1 Introduction It is a widely held belief that the Meissner state, the exclusion of magnetic flux by a (type I) superconductor, cannot be derived from a classical treatment of collissionless charge carriers, but instead requires a separate ansatz, such as that made by F. and H. The crucial element of the proof lies in showing that the canonical momentum field PPP defined in (2.4) is a gradient, even in a classical treatment. If a conventional collision term (−mνvvv) with collision frequency ν > 0 is included on the right-hand side of Eq. (2.1a), then PPP cannot be a gradient or be identically zero (see Appendix A), and then vvv = 0 rather than PPP = 0 is the classical static limit result in simply connected regions. Note that our approximate expression (2.9) and the exact epression (3.4a) imply that, for frequencies greater than the plasma frequency ωp ≡ λp/c, electromagnetic waves can propagate in the fluid, just as in collisionless plasma. However; our model has several limitations. For example, it does not account for the full Meissner effect: the exclusion of the magnetic flux during the superconducting phase transition. During such a dynamical process the system is not at a uniform constant temperature, whereby (2.6) would not be applicable. In this case, the canonical momentum field PPP could not be chosen as a gradient field. Nonetheless, this classical model suitably describes the Meissner state wherein a superconductor has no internal magnetic field. Finally, we note that neither this nor any other classical treatment can offer an explanation of the absence of electron-lattice interaction in the superconducting state; for that, one needs the insight and power of quantum mechanics and the BCS theory concept of Cooper pairs or some other mechanism that provides an energy gap between the electronic ground state and excited states.
